Picking the right tool for the job.

"Which AI should I use?" has three possible answers: this one is fine, this needs a different tier, or this task does not belong in AI at all. A four-question flow tells you which.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Run any task through a four-question decision flow before opening an AI tool
  • Know the questions to ask before your business adopts a new AI vendor
  • Recognise the "shadow AI" pattern and what to do when you see it

The decision flow

Before pasting anything, run the task through these four questions, in order.

Question 1 — Is the data on the never-paste list?

(Module 3.) If yes, you're already in the right place: either reformulate the task to use the "shape" of the data rather than the data itself, or escalate to the enterprise tool. Don't continue down the flow with sensitive data in hand.

Question 2 — Does my business have a procured AI tool for this?

Most businesses, even small ones, now have at least one. Microsoft 365 Copilot if you're on Microsoft 365. ChatGPT Team or Enterprise. Claude for Work. Notion AI Business. Google Workspace's Gemini. If your business has paid for one, use it. The contract, the DPA, the retention controls, the training opt-out — they're all already in place for you.

If you don't know whether your business has one, ask. The answer is a two-line email to your manager or IT lead.

Question 3 — Is the data internal but low-sensitivity?

Examples: marketing copy you're drafting, a presentation outline, a generic question about how to use a piece of software, brainstorming for a blog post.

For these, even a consumer-tier AI tool is usually fine — provided training is opted off (Module 2), and you're not pasting anything that falls into the never-paste list. The risk surface is small.

Question 4 — Is there a regulated decision involved?

"Regulated decision" sounds heavy, but the categories are concrete: anything affecting someone's hiring, firing, pay, promotion, benefits, credit, insurance, education, immigration, health, criminal justice, or access to essential services.

If the AI is helping shape a decision in any of these categories, you're in EU AI Act territory (Module 7 and Module 12). The right answer is rarely "I'll just use ChatGPT". It's either an enterprise tool with logging, or a human-only process.

"Shadow AI" — when teams adopt tools outside IT's view

The biggest AI risk in most small businesses is not malicious use. It is shadow adoption: someone discovers a useful tool, tells two colleagues, and within three months half the team is pasting client work into it. No contract, no DPA, no audit trail, no one in IT or security aware.

Shadow adoption starts on the friendly side. A free tier doing something useful. The use grows faster than the controls around it. By the time someone in IT notices, a year of company data sits in a third party's logs and the team cannot reconstruct what was sent.

✓
The clean-introduction rule

If you find a free AI tool that would help your team, the right move isn't "start using it quietly". It's a five-minute conversation with your manager or IT lead. Three possible outcomes:

  1. "Yes, we already have it under licence" — you save time and use the right tier.
  2. "Yes, we'll get it under licence — give us two weeks" — you become the person who quietly improved the team's toolkit.
  3. "No, here's why" — you avoid a problem you wouldn't have seen coming.

None of these outcomes makes you look foolish. The outcome that makes you look foolish is the breach notification three months later.

Vendor questions worth asking

Whether you're the one choosing the tool or you're being asked to evaluate one your manager has suggested, the same questions apply. Most can be answered from the vendor's trust or security page in ten minutes.

Data and training

Does the contract guarantee our prompts and outputs won't be used to train any model? Is there a configurable retention period? Where (which country) is the data stored?

Security posture

SOC 2 Type II? ISO/IEC 27001 certificate? When was the most recent audit? Is encryption at rest with customer-managed keys an option?

Sub-processors

Does the vendor disclose its sub-processors? Does it use other AI model providers under the hood? Are we notified when sub-processors change?

Incident response

What is the vendor's commitment to notify us in the event of a breach, and within how many hours? How are model failures (a hallucination causing harm) handled?

If the vendor's marketing page leads with "powered by GPT-4 / Claude / Gemini" but the trust page doesn't say which one or under what terms, that's a flag. The model provider is a sub-processor. You're entitled to know.

!
The "AI wrapper" risk

Many "AI tools" advertised to SMBs are thin wrappers around a major model provider's API. That isn't bad in itself — but it does mean your data flows through two companies: the wrapper, and the underlying model provider. Both need DPAs. Both have terms. Both can change them. If a vendor can't tell you who's at the other end of the API call, they probably shouldn't be processing your customer data.

"My business hasn't got an AI policy"

Most small and medium businesses do not have one yet. That puts you in the position of doing the right thing by judgement rather than by rule.

Things that help, even without a formal policy:

  • One nominated person (usually your manager or IT lead) who knows you're using AI for work and what for.
  • A consistent default — pick one tool per kind of work and stick to it, rather than scattering data across six.
  • Your own log of "I used AI to help with X on Y date", in whatever form works for you (Module 9).
  • The 60-second checklist (Module 11) next to your screen.

If the business eventually does adopt a policy, you'll already be 90% compliant.

§
Where the standards land

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Clause 6 requires an organisation that uses AI to plan and document how it uses it — a policy in everything but the name. Annex A.6 covers the AI system lifecycle, including third-party AI services. EU AI Act Article 26 places "deployer" obligations on any organisation that uses a high-risk AI system in the EU — choice of tool, configuration, monitoring, and instructions to staff are all your business's responsibility. Choosing the right tool isn't just risk management — it's an obligation under the rules now in force.

A note on local / offline AI

You may have heard about "running AI locally" — Ollama, LM Studio, llama.cpp, Apple Intelligence's on-device models, Microsoft's local Copilot+ models. For sensitive work, this is a genuinely powerful option: the data never leaves the device, so most of the never-paste-list concerns evaporate.

Caveats: the models are smaller, often less capable; you still need to make sure backup and disk encryption are sorted; and "local" doesn't help if the laptop itself isn't well managed. But for some kinds of work — summarising sensitive notes, drafting things you'd rather not send anywhere — a local model is a genuine fit.
