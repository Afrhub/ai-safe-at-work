Logging, accountability, and "who used what".

Six months after AI helped you make a decision, somebody may ask "how was this decided?". The honest answer takes ten seconds to write down at the time and becomes near-impossible to reconstruct later.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Know what counts as a "significant AI-assisted decision" worth logging
  • Have a minimum logging format you can actually use without slowing your work down
  • Understand GDPR Article 22 protections for certain automated decisions, and the related transparency obligations, in plain words
  • Be able to tell the difference between "logging" and "surveillance"

Why this matters

Three pressures converge on the same answer:

  1. The law is increasingly asking. GDPR Article 22 provides protections relating to certain automated decision-making, alongside transparency obligations under Articles 13–15, and the EU AI Act's logging obligations for high-risk systems (Article 12) both rest on the assumption that there's a record of how AI contributed to a decision. Where AI processes personal data, that activity may also belong in your Records of Processing Activities (RoPA) under GDPR Article 30.
  2. The standards are increasingly asking. ISO/IEC 42001 requires documented evidence of how AI is used and overseen. ISO/IEC 27001 includes controls relating to logging, monitoring and accountability.
  3. The business cost of "we don't know" is high. When a customer complains that an AI-generated decision was wrong, or a regulator asks how you're using AI, or your insurer asks for evidence of AI controls, "we didn't keep track" is the worst answer.

What counts as "significant"

Not every interaction with AI needs a log entry. "I asked ChatGPT to fix this sentence" doesn't. The line is around four properties:

  • Does the output affect another person? Customer, employee, candidate, supplier, the public.
  • Does the output represent the business? Going to a customer, on the website, in a contract, in a marketing campaign.
  • Was the data sensitive? Anything close to the never-paste list (Module 3), even if handled correctly.
  • Was the decision irreversible or expensive? Hiring, firing, pricing, contract terms, technical changes to production systems.
  • Does the output influence a regulatory, compliance or audit-related activity? Anything that may later be examined by a regulator, auditor, or insurer.

Two or more of those true: log it. Zero true: skip it. Over-logging is its own dysfunction.

The minimum useful log

Nine fields, no more. Designed to be copied into a spreadsheet, a Notion page, a shared doc, whatever your team already uses. The whole point is that this should take 30 seconds and remain useful in six months.

i
A minimum log row
  1. Date.
  2. Who. Your name or initials.
  3. Tool. Which AI, which tier. ("ChatGPT Team", "Copilot for M365", "Claude Pro".)
  4. Task. One sentence. ("Drafted reply to customer complaint about late delivery.")
  5. Inputs. What kind of data was in the prompt, not the data itself. ("Customer's order history, summarised; no names or contact details.")
  6. What the AI produced. One sentence. ("Three reply drafts, varying in tone.")
  7. What you did with it. ("Picked option 2, edited the apology, removed the offer of a refund, added 'we'll investigate' instead.")
  8. Who else reviewed it. If anyone. ("Sent. No further review, within normal authority for me.")
  9. Outcome / status. What happened to the output. ("Used", "Rejected", "Escalated", "Further review required".) This becomes useful evidence during audits and annual reviews.

That's it. For most teams, this can live in a single shared spreadsheet. For larger teams, it eventually moves into a proper logging tool. For an individual, a personal note works.

What you don't need to do

Logging isn't a transcript. You don't need to keep every prompt, every response, every conversation. You don't need to capture screenshots. You don't need to record what other people in your team did with their AI tools (that's their log, not yours).

The goal is "if asked, could I describe what happened?", not "if asked, could I replay it exactly?".

Automated decisions and transparency (GDPR Article 22)

GDPR gives individuals a right not to be subject to a decision "based solely on automated processing... which produces legal effects concerning him or her or similarly significantly affects him or her". And when such decisions are made, the person has the right to "meaningful information about the logic involved".

"Solely automated" is the key phrase. If a human meaningfully reviews the AI's output and is empowered to override it (Module 5, real oversight, not rubber-stamping), the decision isn't "solely automated" and Article 22 doesn't bite in its strongest form. But transparency and accountability obligations may still apply depending on the circumstances.

In practice, for an SMB, this means:

  • If a customer is told no, a candidate is rejected, an employee is disciplined, a price is set, or a service is denied, and AI helped shape that, be prepared to explain the AI's role if asked.
  • "Our AI tool recommended this and we accepted it without thinking" is the answer that gets you in trouble. "We used AI to summarise the application; a human then read the summary, the application, and decided" is the answer that does not.
!
Beware "the AI decided"

It's tempting to deflect difficult decisions by blaming the AI. Legally, this is exactly backwards: deferring to AI on regulated decisions increases liability, because Article 22 specifically targets automated decisions. Saying "the AI flagged this candidate as low-fit, so we didn't interview them" is admitting to the kind of decision the law most carefully restricts. Saying "we read the application, considered the AI's summary, and chose not to progress" is doing the same work more defensibly. Accountability for the decision remains with the organisation and the decision-maker, never with the AI system.

Logging vs. surveillance

Logging your own AI use, as in the nine-field row above, is good practice and protective of you. Your employer logging your AI use without your knowledge or a clear policy is a different thing, it's employee monitoring, and it has its own legal framework.

For employers reading this: monitoring of employees' AI use needs the same things any monitoring needs, a lawful basis, transparency to staff, proportionality, and (usually) consultation with employee representatives where they exist. "We installed it without telling people" doesn't fly under GDPR or under employment law in most EU jurisdictions.

The healthy pattern: AI tool use is logged at the tool level (in the admin console) for security and compliance purposes; employees know this; the logs aren't used for performance scoring; and there's a clear policy.

i
Governance insight: logging is for accountability, not monitoring

The purpose of logging should be accountability, traceability and continuous improvement, not employee performance monitoring. Where AI use is recorded in a Records of Processing Activities (RoPA), that record exists to evidence lawful, overseen processing, not to score individuals. Keep the two purposes clearly separate in policy and in practice.

Annual review

Once a year, look back at the log:

  • What did we actually use AI for, in volume?
  • Were the right tools used at the right tiers?
  • Are there categories of use we should formalise as policy?
  • Are there categories we should stop doing?

This is exactly the kind of evidence ISO 42001 expects, but it's also genuinely useful as a management exercise. Most small businesses, when they do this, discover their AI use is concentrated in two or three task types, which makes choosing the right enterprise tool dramatically easier.

✓
Do this today

Open whatever you use for your day-to-day notes. Create one document called "AI use log". Add a header row with the nine fields above. Next time you use AI for anything that meets the "significant" test, add a row. You'll have a useful artefact within a fortnight and audit-grade evidence within a year.

§
Where the standards land

GDPR Article 22: rights related to automated decision-making. Article 13–14: information to be provided to data subjects, including "the existence of automated decision-making" and "meaningful information about the logic involved". Article 30: records of processing activities (RoPA), for larger businesses, AI uses belong in this register. EU AI Act Article 12: high-risk AI systems must keep automated logs. Article 26(5–6): deployers must retain logs and inform affected persons. ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Clause 7.5: documented information; Annex A.9.1: monitoring AI system performance. ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A.8.15: logging.
