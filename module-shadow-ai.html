Relevant Role · Shadow AI

10 sub-modules · 45 min read · two-persona track

Discovering, containing, replacing shadow AI.

Shadow AI is the number-one operational AI risk in most SMBs. The fix isn't a ban — bans don't work; they push the problem underground. This track is the security lead's playbook and the end user's explanation, in the same place, with the same shared vocabulary.

By the end of this track, you'll
  • Tell apart the four shapes of Shadow AI — consumer chatbots, browser extensions, AI-in-SaaS, personal accounts at work
  • Discover what's already happening in your org without a panic-driven ban
  • Know why a ban doesn't work — and what does
  • Build an acceptable-use posture that gets adoption without compromising
  • Gate procurement on AI-feature inventory so new SaaS doesn't become tomorrow's shadow AI
  • Educate end users in a way that gets compliance instead of resentment
  • Run a Shadow-AI-triggered data-leak incident in the first hour
  • Execute a 90-day containment plan for organisations starting from zero
  • Pass a 10-question assessment on shadow-AI judgement calls

Sub-module 1 of 10

What Shadow AI is — and why it spreads faster than any internal control

Shadow AI is any AI use inside the business that hasn't been approved through the organisation's normal procurement, security, or governance processes. It usually starts because someone wanted to do their job better — not because they were trying to break a rule.

It spreads faster than any internal control for three combining reasons:

  1. The tools are free or nearly free. No procurement form to fill in. No invoice for finance to question.
  2. The results land in seconds. The productivity uplift is visible immediately to the user; the risk is invisible.
  3. Adopters become advocates. "I draft my proposals in ChatGPT now" becomes "you should try it" becomes "the whole team is using it" — without any decision needing to be made.

The internal controls that worked for software-as-a-service in 2014 — IT procurement, single sign-on, security review — work poorly for shadow AI because none of them are triggered. The browser visits a website; no MSI is installed; no SAML assertion is requested; no PO is raised. From the IT estate's perspective, nothing happened.

§
Shadow AI isn't a moral failing

Treating shadow-AI adoption as "staff broke the rules" is the wrong starting frame. Most adopters never saw a rule to break; they saw a tool that helped them do their job. The fix is a posture that channels the energy, not a sanction that suppresses it.

Sub-module 1 takeaway
Free tool + instant uplift + invisible risk + organic spread. The math of shadow AI defeats every old-school control. New controls — cultural, procurement, technical — combined.
Sub-module 2 of 10

The four shapes of Shadow AI

Shadow AI isn't one thing. It's four. Each has a different discovery path, a different risk profile, a different intervention.

Shape 1 — Consumer chatbots

Free or paid-individual ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity, Copilot in Edge (consumer), and the dozens of clones. User opens a browser tab and types. Detection: proxy logs / DNS / network analytics.

Shape 2 — Browser extensions

Grammarly's AI features, Compose AI, browser-sidebar AI assistants, "AI summariser" extensions, AI meeting note-takers as Chrome extensions. Often installed casually because they're free; many transmit page content to a third-party AI service. Detection: Microsoft Edge enterprise / Chrome enterprise extension inventory; Defender for Cloud Apps; browser estate management.

Shape 3 — AI-in-SaaS

AI features bolted onto SaaS tools the business already uses: Notion AI, Slack AI, Zoom AI Companion, Otter.ai, Fireflies.ai, HubSpot AI, Salesforce Einstein, every modern CRM and CMS. The SaaS was approved; the AI feature was added in an update without renewed approval. Detection: SaaS Management Platforms (Productiv, Torii, BetterCloud); your existing SaaS inventory + monthly vendor release notes; Defender for Cloud Apps with SaaS connectors.

Shape 4 — Personal AI accounts used for work

Employee has a paid ChatGPT Plus on their personal Gmail. Uses it for work-related tasks. The corporate identity isn't involved at all; the tool sits entirely outside any control surface. Detection: hardest. Network egress patterns + endpoint monitoring + paper-and-pen surveys + cultural signal (people mentioning "their" ChatGPT).

Sub-module 2 takeaway
Four shapes, four discovery paths, four interventions. Treating all shadow AI as "ChatGPT in the browser" misses three of the four.
Sub-module 3 of 10

Discovery without panic

The discovery question isn't "is shadow AI happening here". It is. The question is "what's the scope and what's the risk". Done badly, discovery turns into a witch-hunt that destroys trust and pushes use underground. Done well, it surfaces enough information to make good decisions.

The discovery toolkit

  1. Proxy / DNS / network analytics — surface traffic to known AI domains. Most enterprise proxies + DNS providers (Zscaler, Cisco Umbrella, Cloudflare Gateway, etc.) have AI-tool taxonomies. Pull a 30-day report.
  2. SaaS Management Platforms — Productiv, Torii, BetterCloud surface what SaaS is actually in use vs what's procured. Cross-reference against AI-feature inventory.
  3. Defender for Cloud Apps (or equivalent CASB) — surfaces sanctioned vs unsanctioned cloud apps; many include AI-app categories.
  4. Browser extension inventory — Microsoft Edge for Business, Chrome Enterprise, Brave Talk to IT give visibility into extensions across managed endpoints.
  5. OS-level endpoint monitoring — surfaces locally-installed AI desktop apps (Cursor, Claude desktop app, etc.).
  6. Anonymous survey — paper-and-pen or anonymised online survey: "what AI tools do you actually use for work, and what for". Surprisingly accurate when staff trust the framing.
  7. Manager 1:1 prompts — managers ask their team in 1:1s. Not as an inquisition. As "are there tools you're using that help".

The discovery framing

How discovery is framed matters more than the toolkit. Two scripts that work:

  • Audit framing: "We need to inventory our AI use so we can document compliance with the EU AI Act Article 4. We're asking everyone to share what they use." Neutral. Compliance-driven. No threat.
  • Enablement framing: "We want to make sure people using AI for work get the right tool — paid, secure, supported. Tell us what you use; we'll buy the right tier." Productive. No threat.
!
Framings that destroy discovery

"We've discovered staff are using unauthorised AI tools and we need to know who." "If you've been using ChatGPT for work, you need to declare it." These framings ensure under-reporting + staff working around the controls. Don't.

Sub-module 3 takeaway
Discovery is enabled by the framing as much as by the tooling. Neutral + compliance-driven + enablement-flavoured beats sanction-flavoured every time.
Sub-module 4 of 10

Why a ban doesn't work

Many organisations' first response to shadow AI discovery is a sitewide ban. Two months later, the ban is widely circumvented; use is underground; the data-exposure risk is unchanged but visibility is gone. The ban has made things worse.

Why bans fail

  1. Bans don't satisfy the underlying need. Staff who started using ChatGPT to draft proposals will keep needing to draft proposals. Without a sanctioned alternative, they'll find an unsanctioned route.
  2. Bans drive use underground. Personal accounts; phone hotspots; mobile devices; lunchtime use; out-of-hours use. Network controls don't reach mobile devices.
  3. Bans hand competitors a hiring advantage. Modern knowledge workers expect AI tools at work. "We banned AI" is a recruiting signal — backwards.
  4. Bans are rarely truly enforced. The CEO writes the policy; the CEO's PA uses ChatGPT to draft the CEO's emails. The exception breaks the rule.
  5. Bans become permission slips. Once "no AI for work" is the policy, every exception becomes a special privilege. Resentment + arbitrage follow.

What works instead

  • A clear permitted-tool list with a tier-appropriate option for every job that needs AI.
  • A clear forbidden-data list — narrower than a tool ban, more enforceable.
  • A path for staff to request approval of a new tool, with a published SLA.
  • A culture where reporting "I just realised I pasted something I shouldn't have" lands in support, not discipline.
  • Periodic review of the forbidden list as risk landscape evolves.
§
"Approved alternatives" beats "no"

The most effective shadow-AI containment in the field is providing M365 Copilot or ChatGPT Enterprise as the alternative, then communicating "use this, not that". Staff get the productivity; the business gets the governance. Cost of the licences is rounding-error vs the cost of an incident.

Sub-module 4 takeaway
Bans don't change behaviour; they change visibility. Approved alternatives + clear forbidden categories + cultural permission to report mistakes is the working stack.
Sub-module 5 of 10

The acceptable-use posture

An AUP that works for shadow AI is short, specific, and signed. The AUP template ships the structure. This sub-module covers the shadow-AI-specific tuning.

Three things the AUP must say specifically about shadow AI

  1. The permitted tool list is in here. Not "see IT for the list". The list is in the AUP. Staff who sign know what they're allowed to use.
  2. The forbidden data list is in here. Not "use good judgement". Specific categories: customer PII, special-category personal data, source code, contracts, financial models, HR records, internal-classified, M&A.
  3. The approval path is in here. "Want to use a tool that isn't on the list? Here's the form. Here's the SLA. Here's who decides." No mystery.

Three things the AUP should NOT say

  • "All AI tools must be approved by IT before use." Too vague. What counts as a tool? Is Grammarly an AI tool? Is Slack with AI Companion enabled? Specificity beats generality.
  • "Staff are personally liable for any incident arising from unapproved AI use." Wrong frame. Personal liability creates a "don't report it" culture; this is the opposite of what works.
  • "Browser extensions are forbidden." Too broad. Most browser extensions are fine. Mean what you say.
Sub-module 5 takeaway
The AUP works when it's specific, short, and pairs every forbidden thing with an approved alternative. Vague AUPs don't change behaviour.
Sub-module 6 of 10

Procurement controls — gating new SaaS by AI-feature inventory

Today's approved SaaS is tomorrow's shadow AI when the vendor ships AI features in an update. The procurement gate has to consider AI-feature posture, not just check-the-box at point of purchase.

Three procurement controls

  1. AI-feature inventory at point of purchase — every new SaaS is asked: does this product include AI features? What model is used? What data is sent where? Is training-data opt-out available? Where's the DPA addendum? Vendor questionnaire covers these in 37 questions.
  2. Material-change clause in contracts — vendor agrees to notify you within 30 days of any material AI-feature addition. Where they don't, AI-tier features default to off until you've reviewed.
  3. Quarterly vendor sweep — for every SaaS on the inventory, check the vendor's release notes for AI feature additions in the past quarter. Re-evaluate.

What "material" means for AI features

  • Any new feature that processes customer data, not just metadata.
  • Any new feature that adds a sub-processor (GDPR Article 28 trigger).
  • Any change in data residency posture.
  • Any change in training-data use policy.
  • Any new integration with an external AI provider.
!
"Vendor X added Vendor Y" is your problem

When your CRM vendor adds an AI feature powered by OpenAI, OpenAI is now a sub-processor of yours. GDPR Article 28(2) requires notification + objection window. Catch this at contract renewal, not via a data subject access request.

Sub-module 6 takeaway
Today's approved SaaS is tomorrow's shadow AI without the procurement gate. Vendor questionnaires + material-change clauses + quarterly sweeps prevent the drift.
Sub-module 7 of 10

End-user education — how to explain "use the procured tool"

The intervention that lands isn't a policy email; it's the manager's 1:1 conversation. End-user education that works is conversational, specific, and offers a better alternative — not a worse one.

Scripts that work

  • "I noticed you've been using ChatGPT for client emails. That's actually really smart — we've procured Copilot for M365 which does the same thing but inside our security boundary. Let me show you the difference."
  • "You mentioned using Otter.ai for meeting notes. Did you know our M365 has the same feature built in? Same productivity, your meeting notes stay in the tenant."
  • "We're not banning AI — we're asking you to use the procured one because we've checked it for data handling. Here's the list of what you can use. If something's missing that would help you, here's the request form."

Scripts that don't work

  • "You're using unapproved tools and that's against policy." Lectures. Resentment. Working-around.
  • "From now on, no AI without explicit approval." See sub-module 4 — bans.
  • "This is a compliance matter and IT has been notified." Frame as audit; staff hide further.

What managers need to teach

  • The forbidden-inputs list — concrete categories, examples, why each one matters.
  • The approved tools — which one for which task; how to log in; where to ask for help.
  • The "if you make a mistake" path — what happens if I realise I pasted something I shouldn't have. (Answer: report it, no discipline for honest reports; we can sometimes contain.)
  • The "what if I think this tool would help" path — request, expected SLA, who decides.
Sub-module 7 takeaway
End-user education is the manager's 1:1, not the all-hands policy email. Conversational + alternative-offering + safe-to-report changes behaviour.
Sub-module 8 of 10

Incident response when Shadow AI causes a data leak

An employee pastes a customer-data file into ChatGPT free. They realise the mistake an hour later. The clock starts now. The first-hour playbook follows the incident form + the MSP-track Sub-module 8, but with shadow-AI-specific tuning.

Shadow-AI first-hour notes

  1. Preserve the artefact. Ask the employee to screenshot what they pasted and what was returned. Don't ask them to "delete the chat" — they may need to, but only after preservation.
  2. Engage the vendor. Most consumer AI vendors have a route to delete data on request. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google all have channels. Use them — but understand that "deleted" doesn't always mean "gone from logs".
  3. Don't make the employee the incident. The incident is the exposure; the employee is the reporter. Make sure HR knows this isn't a disciplinary; it's a reportable.
  4. Trigger the GDPR clock if applicable. Personal data exposed to a non-controlled third party = GDPR Article 33 likely.
  5. Sweep for similar. If this employee paste something, others may have too. MDCA / proxy logs / DLP sweep across the org.
  6. Close the gap. The forbidden-inputs list was either unclear, or the right approved tool wasn't available, or both. Fix the upstream cause within 30 days; communicate the fix.

Common containment errors

  • Disciplining the reporter. The next person never reports. The org becomes blind to incidents.
  • Banning the tool reactively. See sub-module 4. Often the wrong response.
  • Skipping vendor deletion request. Even when the practical effect is small, it's part of the audit trail.
  • Not sweeping for related events. The single incident is rarely the whole story.
Sub-module 8 takeaway
Preserve. Vendor delete. Don't punish the reporter. Sweep for related. Fix the gap. The employee told you because the culture allows them to — protect that.
Sub-module 9 of 10

The 90-day Shadow AI containment plan

For organisations starting from zero — they suspect shadow AI is happening, they have no real inventory, they need a structured 90-day plan. This is it.

Days 0–30 — Discovery without panic

  • Pull 30-day proxy / DNS / SaaS / browser-extension data. Build a one-page inventory of "what's in use".
  • Run the anonymous survey + manager 1:1 framing (Sub-module 3).
  • Don't communicate any new policy yet. Establish baseline first.
  • Engage HR + legal + procurement + IT + one or two representative line managers on the working group.
  • End of week 4: working group has the inventory + scope of what to address.

Days 30–60 — Procure + draft

  • Decide the approved-tool baseline. Typically: M365 Copilot if M365-heavy; ChatGPT Enterprise if M365-light; both if appropriate.
  • Procure the licences. Pilot to the working group + early adopters.
  • Draft the updated AUP with the specific approved-tool list and forbidden-inputs list.
  • Draft the "if you've been using shadow AI, here's how we handle it" amnesty communication.
  • Build manager talking-points (Sub-module 7 scripts).

Days 60–90 — Communicate, train, embed

  • Sponsor sends the amnesty + new-posture email. No-fault period for shadow-AI declarations.
  • Roll out the universal course + this Shadow AI track to all staff (use the 30-day rollout guide as the linear plan, run it in week 8–12).
  • Activate procurement gating (Sub-module 6).
  • Manager 1:1 conversations during weeks 9–12.
  • End of week 12: training register complete, AUP signed, escalation channel proven, incident drill done.

Days 90+ — Steady state

  • Monthly procurement sweep + quarterly approved-tool list review.
  • Quarterly survey to recheck shadow-AI prevalence.
  • Annual AUP restatement.
  • Quarterly incident-drill tabletop.
Sub-module 9 takeaway
90 days takes you from zero to controlled. Skip discovery and you ban the wrong things; skip procurement and you've got nothing to point staff at; skip the amnesty + culture work and you've got compliance theatre.
Sub-module 10 of 10

Final assessment

Ten judgement-call questions. Self-mark. Suggested pass: 8/10. Use it to feel where you're confident and where the gap is.

Question 1

Discovery surfaces 47 different AI tools in use across an SMB of 90 staff. The CEO wants to "ban everything that's not approved by Friday". What do you say?

Answer

Push back. A Friday ban on 47 tools will (a) not be enforceable, (b) push use to personal accounts, (c) destroy goodwill, (d) leave the underlying job-to-be-done unaddressed. Counter-propose: 30 days to procure approved alternatives + draft the specific AUP + identify forbidden-data categories. Then transition staff to the approved set. The ban only works after the alternative is available.

Question 2

You discover via DNS logs that 60% of staff have visited chat.openai.com from a corporate device in the last 30 days. What's the immediate next step?

Answer

Not "block chat.openai.com". The immediate next step is to understand what they were doing — anonymous survey + sample manager 1:1 conversations. If they're drafting client communications, the answer is "procure the approved alternative + transition them". If they're doing personal-time research, that's different. The block is a tool; the wrong tool to lead with.

Question 3

A staff member walks up and says "I've been using ChatGPT for our M&A workstream. I just realised the documents I uploaded included board minutes. Have I broken the rules?" What's your response?

Answer

"Thank you for telling me. The fact you flagged it is exactly what we need. You haven't broken any rule about reporting — but yes, we shouldn't have those documents in a consumer AI. Let's contain: I need the chat link so we can preserve and request deletion from OpenAI. Then we work out whether this triggers any client / regulator notification. You are not in trouble for reporting. We may need to walk what other documents you've used; that conversation is supportive, not disciplinary."

Question 4

HR is using Otter.ai to record and transcribe interviews with candidates. Is this Shadow AI?

Answer

Probably — if Otter wasn't approved through procurement with AI-feature scrutiny. Worse, candidate interviews involve special-category considerations (often health, beliefs, characteristics revealed in interview). Even worse, candidates may not have consented to AI transcription. Fix: stop the use, switch to an approved tool with appropriate DPA + consent flow, and consult on whether the existing interviews need to be addressed under the data subject rights framework.

Question 5

You decide to procure ChatGPT Enterprise. You can roll it out to (a) everyone immediately; (b) IT + finance pilot for 30 days then expand; (c) just managers. Which?

Answer

(b). The pilot lets you validate the data-handling posture, train the working group, refine the AUP, identify the manager talking points before the all-hands rollout. Going straight to everyone (a) skips learning; going to just managers (c) doesn't address the staff who are currently using shadow AI most heavily.

Question 6

Your CRM vendor pushes a release notes update: "AI-powered email assistant now available — automatically enabled for all users in your tenant". What do you do this week?

Answer

(1) Read the AI-assistant terms — what data is sent where, training opt-out, sub-processor list. (2) Check whether this triggers GDPR Article 28(2) sub-processor notification window. (3) Decide whether to keep it on, opt out tenant-wide, or feature-gate. (4) Update the approved-tool inventory entry for the CRM. (5) Communicate to staff what's happening. (6) If it's material, brief the DPO. None of this is panicked; all of it is procedural.

Question 7

Your AUP says "AI tools must be approved before use". An auditor asks "how do staff know what's approved?". You say "it's on the intranet". Auditor finds the intranet page; it was last updated 14 months ago and lists three tools. What's wrong?

Answer

The approved-tool list is stale + hidden. Two issues: (1) the list must be current — monthly review minimum (Sub-module 4 of MSP / IT Admin track + Sub-module 6 here); (2) the list must be in the AUP itself (Sub-module 5 — three things the AUP must say), not somewhere staff have to remember to look. Fix both.

Question 8

Browser extension audit surfaces 12 staff using a Chrome extension called "AI Email Composer" that the team installed individually. What do you do first?

Answer

Check the extension's data handling — does it transmit email contents to a third-party AI? If yes (commonly), this is potentially a personal-data-out-to-an-unauthorised-processor event. (1) Block the extension at the browser-management layer. (2) Engage DPO on whether this triggers Art 33 notification. (3) Identify the underlying need ("I want help composing emails") and direct affected staff at the approved alternative. (4) Update browser-extension allowlist. (5) Add this to the AUP forbidden list with the example named.

Question 9

A senior leader insists on using personal ChatGPT Plus for work because "Enterprise is too slow". What's the right escalation?

Answer

Not enforcement. (1) Surface to their peer (CISO to CIO, CIO to COO). (2) Address the speed complaint — is it real (configuration / region)? Often it's perception, not measured. (3) Document the exception with the named individual + risk + mitigations agreed. (4) Time-bound the exception — "we'll revisit in 60 days". Senior exceptions become culture norms; manage the optics and the precedent carefully.

Question 10

You're an MSP. A customer says "we've discovered shadow AI; what's your 90-day fix?" What's the answer?

Answer

Days 0–30 discovery (proxy + survey + manager 1:1); days 30–60 procure + draft (license M365 Copilot or ChatGPT Enterprise + AUP + amnesty comm); days 60–90 train + embed (rollout guide + manager scripts + procurement gating + drill). The 30-day rollout guide covers the weeks 8–12 training rollout; the MSP / IT Admin track sub-module 5 covers your provider-side onboarding choreography. The 90-day plan in this track's Sub-module 9 is the answer.

Track complete
Shadow AI is mostly a culture and procurement problem with a technical layer. Discovery without panic + approved alternatives + clear AUP + supportive incident posture + procurement gating + steady-state cadence. Record yourself in your training register and run the 90-day plan if you haven't.
