Chairman's verdict

Where the council agrees (high confidence)

1. Range/band pricing is right; FINE bands are wrong. Nobody defended 1–20/21–50/51–100/101–150. Every extra band is invented precision (no customer data), a manual upgrade event at every boundary (19-person firm hires two), and a repricing liability.
2. Publish prices. All five, again: a blank price loses every visitor and burns founder time on unqualified calls. Transparency's real payoff is self-qualification.
3. Both tiers appear at every published row. The Outsider's point stood unchallenged: a missing cell reads as "call us" and kills the tab.
4. Hidden MSP pricing works — channel wholesale is a trade secret, like rebates; one "Partners: contact us" line suffices — subject to two rules: reconcile the RORtech maths before any partner terms go in writing, and contract that partner resale price never exceeds your public list (the only scenario where hidden pricing looks dishonest).

Where the council clashes

How much of the ladder to price publicly. Contrarian/First-Principles: two priced bands, everything bigger gated ("a public £499/mo caps the 150-person org that might pay £15k"). Expansionist/Outsider: the full ladder builds trust and self-serve. Executor: four bands max, top row "contact us". Chairman resolution: price two rows, show three. The grid format wins (legibility, upgrade path) but the top row shows "Contact us" for both tiers instead of a number — big prospects see they're served without being handed a ceiling.
Calculator vs table. Expansionist wants an interactive calculator; Executor says eight cells don't need tooling. Resolution: static table now; calculator only if enquiry volume ever justifies it.

Blind spots the council caught

• The headcount contract mechanics are the real cost of banding (all five missed, four reviewers caught): define who counts (employees incl. part-time; contractors with system access count; group companies don't), measure at purchase, honour-system with annual true-up, and grandfather each customer's band price for 12 months.
• VAT display: state ex-VAT explicitly (UK B2B convention).
• The "why is Platform 3×?" test: a side-by-side grid invites the comparison — each column needs its one-line value statement.
• It's all reversible at zero customers: treat the page as an experiment; log the sizes behind every "talk to us" enquiry and let real demand draw the future band seams.

The recommendation

One grid, three rows, two priced. Bands chosen so boundary events are rare and hand-invoicing stays sane:

Full advisor responses & reviews

The Contrarian (strongest, 3/5 reviews)
Four-plus public bands is a spreadsheet, not a pricing page; every band beyond the first two is fiction with zero customers. Invented numbers lock you in — the 120-person prospect screenshots and negotiates against them forever; fine bands invite reverse headcount gaming with no verification; the grid hands procurement your margin model. Publish entry prices, gate the top ("From £249/mo; larger teams talk to us"). Hidden MSP pricing fails here specifically because RORtech tiers were never reconciled with direct prices — reconcile first, and contract MSP resale ≥ public list.
The Executor (strongest, 2/5 reviews)
Bands are capped by what you can invoice by hand — four max; boundaries are manual upgrade conversations, so ≤25 beats 1–20 (the 19-person firm hiring two never triggers an event). Full grid as one static table, both tiers as columns, top band "Contact us", no calculator. Publish everything — you need quotes you can send as a URL. Partner line: "MSP or reseller? Contact us." Ship this week: one page, one deploy.
The First Principles Thinker
A band's only job is answering "what will this cost me?" Fine bands are price discrimination without data — false precision that forces decisions on visitors and creates repricing liability. Two bands + talk-to-us; widen when customers reveal the seams. Transparency buys self-qualification of founder time, not abstract trust. Hidden channel margin is standard; the only rule is partner resale never exceeds public list; fix the RORtech maths or the grid is a contract already broken.
The Outsider
At 38 staff I find my 21–50 row and I'm done — that's the grid's win. Both tiers must appear at every band (missing cells = "call us" = closed tab). The full ladder reassures rather than spooks and shows my upgrade cost before renewal surprises me. I need a who-counts footnote or I won't email. Hidden MSP pricing is fine — I assume my IT provider marks up — unless I learn identical service was materially cheaper direct. What actually blocks purchase: invoice-only payment, no testimonials, placeholder bios.
The Expansionist (blind spot, 5/5 reviews)
The grid is the growth engine: self-serve procurement, the upgrade ladder as product story, "AI governance pricing UK" SEO, a calculator that works in FR/DE, and public retail making partner margin legible. Reviewers' unanimous caution: upside-only; ignores lock-in, top-band ceiling, gaming, and a solo founder's capacity; proposes tooling that violates the operating constraints; asserts SEO value without evidence.
Peer-review highlights
Strongest split D×3 / B×2 — risk mechanism vs shippability; the chairman merged them (grid format from B, gated top row from D). Blind spot E×5. What all five missed: contractual headcount mechanics (FTE definition, measurement date, true-up, grandfathering) as the true operational cost of banding; VAT display; the Platform-vs-Foundation "why 3×?" comparison a side-by-side grid invites; and that at zero customers the whole page is a reversible experiment whose "talk to us" enquiries generate the missing pricing data.
