### Duration
7–10 minutes
### Learning Outcome

* understand why organisations are introducing AI governance,
* recognise operational risks linked to unmanaged AI usage,
* understand what “shadow AI” means,
* and identify how everyday AI usage can create business exposure.
---
# Introduction
## AI is already inside many organisations.
Employees are increasingly using tools such as:
* ChatGPT,
* Microsoft Copilot,
* Google Gemini,
* Claude,
* AI browser extensions,
* transcription tools,
* and AI-enabled SaaS platforms
to:
* write emails,
* summarise meetings,
* analyse information,
* create content,
* and improve productivity.
In many cases, this adoption happens faster than:
* governance,
* training,
* approval processes,
* or operational oversight.
This is commonly referred to as:
## Shadow AI
Meaning:
> use of AI systems outside approved governance, procurement or security processes.
---
# Why This Matters
AI can provide significant business benefits.
However, unmanaged AI usage may also create:
* confidential data exposure,
* GDPR/privacy concerns,
* intellectual property risks,
* inaccurate outputs,
* reputational damage,
* supplier governance issues,
* and operational risk.
This course is designed to help employees:
* use AI more safely,
* recognise governance boundaries,
* reduce operational risk,
* and support responsible AI adoption.
---

## “It was only for five minutes.”

> “improve the wording before a meeting.”

* customer names,

> “It’s harmless and temporary.”
### Risks Created
* confidentiality exposure

Even well-intentioned AI usage may create business risk if employees do not understand:

# Introduction
## AI tools are not simply search engines.
When information is entered into an AI system, it may be:
* processed,
* transmitted,
* retained,
* logged,
* or reviewed
depending on:
* the provider,
* account type,
* organisational controls,
* and platform settings.
Different AI platforms operate in different ways.
Understanding these differences is essential for safe AI usage.
---
# What Happens When You Type Into AI?
When a prompt is submitted:
1. Information is transmitted to the AI provider.
2. The system processes the request using a large language model.
3. A response is generated.
4. Data handling depends on:
* the platform,
* governance settings,
* contractual protections,
* and organisational controls.
Some AI systems may retain or process submitted data depending on:
* platform configuration,
* enterprise settings,
* and account type.
---

* limited organisational oversight

* hallucination risk,

* or governance failures.

depending on:
* settings,
* account type,
* and platform configuration.

Not always.
Removing a conversation from the user interface does not necessarily mean:
* immediate deletion,

* consumer-focused,

## “Enterprise AI removes all risk.”
Incorrect.
Enterprise AI may reduce certain risks through stronger governance controls, but organisations still remain responsible for:
* oversight,
* permissions,
* configuration,
* and safe usage practices.

# MODULE 05
# Verifying What The AI Tells You
### Duration
10–12 minutes
### Learning Outcome
By the end of this module, learners will:
* understand why AI systems may generate incorrect information,
* recognise hallucinations, bias and false confidence,
* apply practical verification techniques,
* and understand why human oversight remains essential.
---
# Introduction
## AI can sound convincing while still being incorrect.
This is one of the biggest operational risks associated with generative AI.
AI systems are designed to:
* generate human-like responses,
* predict likely language patterns,
* and produce helpful outputs quickly.
They are not:
* guaranteed sources of truth,
* legal authorities,
* compliance specialists,
* or replacements for professional judgement.
Generative AI systems generate outputs based on learned patterns and probabilities rather than verified factual reasoning.
As a result, AI may:
* fabricate information,
* generate inaccurate content,
* misunderstand context,
* present outdated information,
* or produce biased outputs.
This is commonly referred to as:
## hallucination.
---
# The Core Principle
## If the output matters,
it must be verified by a human.
Especially where outputs affect:
* customers,
* finance,
* legal matters,
* compliance,
* HR,
* operational decisions,
* or reputational risk.
---
# What Is A Hallucination?
A hallucination occurs when an AI system generates:
* fabricated,
* inaccurate,
* or misleading information
while presenting it confidently.
Examples include:
* invented references,
* incorrect statistics,
* fabricated case law,
* or inaccurate policy guidance.
The risk is that:
> the response may still appear highly professional and believable.
---
# Real-World Scenario 1
## “The cases didn’t exist.”
A lawyer uses AI to help prepare legal arguments.
The AI generates:
* legal case references,
* quotations,
* and supporting explanations.
The output:
* appears authoritative,
* sounds professional,
* and includes detailed citations.
However:
the legal cases were fabricated by the AI system.
### Risks Created
* reputational damage
* operational embarrassment
* legal exposure
* professional conduct concerns
### Key Lesson
AI-generated confidence is not proof of accuracy.
---

# Why AI Gets Things Wrong
Generative AI systems:
* predict patterns,
* not verified truth.
Outputs are influenced by:
* training data,
* prompt quality,
* context,
* and probability-based language generation.
This means AI may:
* misunderstand prompts,
* combine unrelated information,
* oversimplify issues,
* or fill gaps with inaccurate detail.
---
# Common AI Failure Types
# 1. Fabricated Information (“Hallucinations”)
Examples:
* fake references
* invented statistics
* fabricated case law
* inaccurate policies
---
# 2. Outdated Information
AI may:
* rely on historical training data,
* misunderstand recent changes,
* or provide outdated guidance.
---
# 3. False Confidence
AI often presents uncertain information using:
* authoritative tone,

* fabricated references or summaries

---
# 3. Governance Violations
Examples:
* unapproved AI usage

---
# 4. Security Incidents
Examples:
* AI-assisted phishing
* credential exposure
* infrastructure information entered into AI systems
---
# 5. Reputational Incidents
Examples:
* biased AI-generated content
* inappropriate outputs
* customer complaints linked to AI usage
---
# When To Escalate
Escalation may be appropriate if:
* sensitive information was entered into AI,
* customer impact exists,
* the output appears incorrect or harmful,
* an unapproved AI tool is discovered,
* security concerns exist,
* or organisational policy may have been breached.
If unsure:
> escalate and ask.
---
# What Good Escalation Looks Like
Good escalation is:
* prompt,
* factual,
* transparent,
* and calm.
The objective is:
* containment,
* risk reduction,
* and appropriate response.
---
# What To Include In An Escalation
Where possible include:
* what happened,
* what AI tool was involved,
* what information may have been affected,
* when it occurred,
* who has been informed,
* and any immediate containment actions already taken.
Do not:
* hide information,
* attempt to quietly delete evidence,
* or continue using the affected system without guidance.
---

Many incidents become worse because:
* employees panic,
* delay reporting,
* or try to fix issues alone.
Good governance culture encourages:
* transparency,
* escalation,
* and learning.

# The 60-Second AI Safety Checklist
Before using AI, pause and ask:
---
# 1. Is The Tool Approved?
## Ask Yourself:
* Is this AI platform approved by the organisation?
* Am I using a personal account or approved enterprise environment?
* Has this tool gone through governance or security review?
### Higher-Risk Examples
* personal ChatGPT accounts
* AI browser extensions
* unapproved SaaS tools
* AI plugins installed without review
### Safer Practice
Use approved enterprise AI platforms wherever possible.
---
# 2. What Information Am I Entering?
## Ask Yourself:
Does this contain:
* customer data?
* confidential information?
* pricing?
* HR records?
* source code?
* contracts?
* security information?
If yes:
* additional controls,
* approval,
* or escalation
may be required.
---
# 3. Would I Be Comfortable Explaining This AI Usage?
## Ask Yourself:
Would I be comfortable explaining this usage:
* to leadership,
* compliance,
* a customer,
* or an auditor?
Would the organisation expect this information to be processed externally?
If unsure:
> stop and ask.
---
# 4. Does The Output Need Verification?
## Ask Yourself:
Could this output affect:
* customers,
* finance,
* legal matters,
* HR,
* operations,
* or reputation?
If yes:
* human review,
* validation,
* and oversight
are essential.
---
# 5. Could This Create Bias, Harm Or Embarrassment?
## Ask Yourself:
Could this:
* create reputational risk?
* misrepresent someone?
* introduce bias?
* create offensive or inappropriate content?
* cause customer concern?
AI-generated outputs should always be reviewed for:
* fairness,
* tone,
* and context.
---
# 6. Would I Know What To Do If Something Went Wrong?
## Ask Yourself:
If this created:
* an incident,
* data exposure,
* customer issue,
* or governance concern,
would I know:
* who to contact,
* how to escalate,
* and what process to follow?
If not:
review organisational guidance before proceeding.
