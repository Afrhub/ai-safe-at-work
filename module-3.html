The never-paste list.

If one module sticks, make it this one. The nine kinds of information below should never go into a free or paid AI tool, no matter how you dress up the task. "Just to format it" and "just to sum it up" still count.

By the end of this module, you will
  • Know the nine kinds of data you must never paste into a free AI tool
  • See which rules (GDPR, ISO 27001) back each one up
  • Know what to do when the AI would help but the data is off-limits
i
"Never" with one footnote

"Never paste this" means never into a free or paid (just-you) AI tool. The same thing may be fine in a proper work tool your company has set up (see Module 4). The rule is not "no AI". The rule is "not this kind of tool for this kind of data".

§
First, classify the data

Before you decide where data can go, decide what it is. Most organisations sort information into a few levels:

  • Public — already published; safe to share.
  • Internal — for staff, not the outside world.
  • Confidential — limited to those who need it; client, commercial or personal data lives here.
  • Restricted — the most sensitive; tight access, special handling.

This is the data-classification idea behind ISO/IEC 27001 (A.5.12 & A.5.13), and it is exactly the first question in Module 2's five-question test. Everything on the never-paste list below is Confidential or Restricted — which is why a free public tool is the wrong place for it.

1. Customer and contact details

Names, emails, phone numbers, home addresses, birth dates, account numbers, customer IDs. Even one row. Even "I'll hide the names first". You usually don't, and even when you try, the AI's reply often fills in enough to spot the person.

Why it's a problem: this is personal data under GDPR. Paste it into a tool with no proper deal in place, and that may result in a personal data breach or unauthorised disclosure.

What to do instead: describe the shape, not the data. "I have a spreadsheet with three columns: name, email, last order date." Ask for the steps, then run them on your own computer.

2. Sensitive personal data

Health, race, religion, who someone loves, politics, union membership, fingerprints or face scans. Anything that could be used to treat someone unfairly.

Why it's a problem: GDPR Article 9 says this kind of data is almost always off-limits unless you have one of nine special reasons. "I pasted it into a chatbot to save time" is not one of them.

What to do instead: if you work with HR files, medical notes, or diversity data, the only safe AI is one your company has set up properly, with records turned on.

3. Contracts and legal papers you did not write

Draft or signed contracts, NDAs, term sheets, settlements. Other people's lawyers wrote them, often under a promise to keep them secret.

Why it's a problem: most contracts say you must not share them with outsiders without permission. The AI company is an outsider. Pasting the other side's secret terms breaks that promise, and may break professional rules if you are a lawyer.

What to do instead: take out the names, paste only the one clause you want to understand, and ask in general ("what does this kind of clause usually mean?"). Better still, use a contract tool your firm has set up.

4. Code from systems you do not own

Internal apps, your company's code, a client's code, secret formulas, anything from a private code store.

Why it's a problem: code is valuable property. Big companies (Samsung is the famous one) have had staff paste secret code into ChatGPT. Once code goes to an outside tool you do not control, it may reduce legal and commercial protections around confidential information.

What to do instead: rewrite the snippet so it is generic. Strip out the business bits, the function names and the variable names, until it is just a plain coding puzzle. Or use a coding helper set up for your company, like GitHub Copilot Business.

5. Money figures that are not public

Internal profit and loss, draft accounts, pricing, forecasts, special customer prices, supplier costs, pay, share details.

Why it's a problem: price-sensitive numbers from a private company are commercial secrets. For a publicly traded company, in specific circumstances they may count as "inside information", and disclosing or acting on it before it is announced can be a criminal offence.

What to do instead: work with the shape, not the numbers. "I have monthly sales for the last two years. How should I build a chart that shows growth and seasons?" needs no real numbers.

6. Passwords and keys

Passwords. API keys. SSH keys. Database links. Login tokens. Anything that opens a door.

Why it's a problem: any secret you paste into a chat should be treated as potentially compromised. Change it straight away.

What to do instead: to fix a login bug, describe it without the secret. "My API gives a 401 error when I send the token in the header" is enough.

!
If a secret has been pasted

Do not panic, but do not wait. Change the password or key in the system it belongs to before you finish this module. Delete the chat. Tell your IT or security team. Most can be changed in minutes. That is far cheaper than leaving the secret inside a company's AI.

7. Internal papers marked confidential

Board papers, strategy docs, takeover talks, layoff plans, restructure memos, anything stamped "Confidential".

Why it's a problem: the stamp is there for a reason. If your company sorts documents by how secret they are, the top level means "do not send outside". If it has no system, the word "Confidential" on the page is the system.

What to do instead: ask the AI for the shape, not the content. "How should I lay out a board memo about a big change of plan?" is fine. Pasting the actual plan is not.

8. HR and staff records

Reviews, warnings, pay letters, sickness notes, references, exit chats, complaints.

Why it's a problem: staff data is personal data, and HR data is some of the most sensitive there is. Getting it wrong hurts real people. A wrong tone in a review, a leaked salary, or an unfair AI judgement all add legal risk on top of the privacy risk.

What to do instead: write people stuff in your own words first, then ask AI to tidy the wording, not to write the substance. And do not paste the person's real data.

9. Anything from a client's job

If you are a consultant, agency, accountant, lawyer or contractor, your client's information is theirs to protect. Briefs, work you deliver, papers they showed you, anything you would not show another client.

Why it's a problem: you are not the owner of that data, your client is. They never agreed for it to go to an AI company. It is not yours to paste. On top of the data-protection angle, most client work sits under contractual confidentiality obligations — an NDA or a confidentiality clause in your engagement terms — and breaching those is often a bigger and faster problem than the privacy breach itself.

What to do instead: ask the client. Many will say yes. Some will name a tool they want you to use. Some will say no, and then you simply cannot use AI for that part. Get used to that chat.

!
This is where Shadow AI bites

Using unapproved AI tools to process any of the information above is often referred to as Shadow AI, and it creates significant governance and compliance risks. The data leaves through a door no one is watching: no contract, no record, and no way for your organisation to know what was sent or to get it back.

G
Governance Insight

ISO/IEC 42001 encourages organisations to understand the AI systems they use, assess supplier risks and implement appropriate controls over AI-related activities. Knowing which tool a given class of data may go into — and which suppliers stand behind it — is a control, not just good manners.

The "but I'm in a rush" answer

Most of the pressure to break these rules comes from a deadline. A deadline has three honest exits: (1) push the deadline, (2) use the AI on the general shape of the problem, (3) get the proper work tool that is allowed for this job. "Paste it anyway and hope" is not on the list.

§
Where the rules land

GDPR Articles 5, 6, 9, 32: how to handle data, having a lawful reason, sensitive data, and keeping data safe. The never-paste list is mostly these rules turned into plain steps. ISO 27001:2022 (A.5.10): use information and tools properly. Your company should have an acceptable-use policy, and AI belongs in it. (A.5.13): label information. The "Confidential" stamp means something. (A.8.12): stop data leaks.

✓
The "describe the shape" trick

Most useful AI work does not need the real data. Swap it like this:

Instead of: "Here is our customer list, sort by region."

Try: "I have about 400 customers in a spreadsheet. The columns are name, email, country, last order date. Give me the Excel formula to sort by country, then by last order date, newest first."

You got the answer. The data never left your laptop. This works for code, writing, analysis, summaries and plans, almost anything. Learn this and most of the never-paste list stops being a problem.
