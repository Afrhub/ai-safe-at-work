AI in healthcare.

A sector overlay on top of the universal course. Healthcare data is special-category under GDPR Article 9 by default; AI that touches clinical decisions becomes a medical device under EU MDR; UK practices answer to NHS DSPT + DCB0129 + DCB0160. The clocks are tighter than other sectors — and the patient is always closer than the regulator.

By the end of this overlay, you'll
  • Understand GDPR Article 9 + national derogations as the floor for any AI processing patient data
  • Know when an AI tool crosses the EU MDR / MHRA / FDA SaMD threshold and becomes a medical device
  • Map AI procurement to NHS DSP Toolkit, DCB0129 (manufacturer) + DCB0160 (deployer) clinical-safety obligations
  • Identify the extended healthcare "never-paste" list (NHS number, clinical-record extracts, prescription data, diagnostic images, genetics)
  • Apply patient-safety reporting clocks: Yellow Card · MHRA · MDR Article 87 · AI Act Article 73 — and which fires first
  • Manage AI receptionist / triage chatbot risk under MHRA + Consumer-facing AI rules
  • Handle cross-border patient data flows (UK / EU / US / HIPAA)
  • Know which regulator you tell, in what order, in an AI incident affecting patients
  • Pass a 10-question assessment on sector-specific judgement calls

The regulatory stack — read top to bottom

GDPR — applies sitewide. Article 9 special-category data (Article 9(1) prohibition + Article 9(2) lawful-basis conditions, of which 9(2)(h) "preventive or occupational medicine" is the workhorse for clinical use). Article 22 (automated decisions) bites in clinical triage. Recital 35 defines "data concerning health" widely. National derogations under Article 9(4) — UK DPA 2018 Schedule 1, French Code de la santé publique, German BDSG §22 — add local conditions.

EU AI Act — applies. Article 4 literacy obligation now. Article 26 deployer obligations August 2026. Annex III §5(a) high-risk where AI is used to dispatch / triage emergency services. Annex I lists AI integrated into medical devices: high-risk by default. Article 50 transparency for AI users interacting with patients (chatbots).

EU MDR (Regulation 2017/745) — applies if AI is a medical device. Articles 2(1) definition + Annex VIII classification rules + Rule 11 (software) — most AI clinical software is at least Class IIa. CE marking required. Notified body involvement scales with class. Vigilance reporting per Article 87 (serious incident → competent authority within 15 days; immediate or 2-day windows for serious public-health threats).

UK regulators:

  • MHRA — UK medical device authority; AI as a Medical Device programme; UKCA / UKCA(NI) marking; Yellow Card incident reporting
  • NHS England + NHS Digital — DSP Toolkit annual self-assessment (mandatory for organisations connected to NHS systems); Network and Information Systems (NIS) Regulations 2018 for NHS organisations
  • NICE — Evidence Standards Framework for Digital Health Technologies (ESF) — informs procurement; not strictly statutory but de facto required for NHS purchase
  • CQC — registers + inspects health and social care providers; AI-related concerns surface in well-led + safe-domain assessments

UK clinical-safety standards — DCB0129 (manufacturer of health IT — clinical safety case + clinical-safety officer named); DCB0160 (deployer — same disciplines applied locally). Both are mandatory under the Health and Social Care Act 2012 for NHS organisations.

US cross-reference — HIPAA Privacy + Security + Breach Notification Rules (45 CFR Parts 160 + 164); FDA SaMD framework; HHS OCR breach reporting clocks (60 days). Cross-border telehealth + US-served patients trigger these.

NIS2 — covers healthcare providers as "essential" entities for those above relevant thresholds. Article 21 risk-management measures apply.

This overlay sits on top of the universal course. Universal course = baseline. Healthcare overlay = the additional clinical-safety + special-category layer.

The healthcare governance layer — how GDPR Art 9 + EU MDR + EU AI Act + DCB0129 combine

Healthcare AI sits at the intersection of three regulatory frameworks that were written by three different communities, in three different decades, with three different definitions of "device" and "data". They don't always align. They all apply.

For most SMB healthcare providers the priority sequence is: (1) GDPR Article 9 lawful basis — must exist before any AI processes a patient record; (2) DCB0160 deployer clinical-safety case for any AI affecting clinical decisions; (3) NHS DSPT compliance if connected; (4) check whether the AI is a medical device under MDR/MHRA — if yes, only buy CE / UKCA-marked; (5) EU AI Act Article 26 if a deployer of a high-risk AI system.

Sub-module 1 takeaway
Healthcare AI lives at the intersection of data-protection, AI-system, and medical-device regulators. Map your firm to each before you procure; one regulator doesn't override another's clock.
Sub-module 2 of 9

The healthcare never-paste list

Universal Module 3 holds. Healthcare extends it because the data is special-category by default, the consequence of leak is patient harm, and several categories carry criminal-not-civil penalties.

Categories to add to the universal never-paste list

  • NHS number / health-system identifier — strong identifier; combination with a postcode often re-identifies. Avoid even partial in any non-approved AI tool.
  • Clinical record extracts — even single SOAP notes are special-category. Pasting one note into a consumer LLM to "tidy the wording" is an Article 9 event.
  • Prescription data — drug + dose + patient + date often identifies the patient + the condition.
  • Diagnostic images — radiology, dermatology, fundus images. EXIF + DICOM tags often contain identifiers. Don't drop into image-handling AI tools without scrubbing AND lawful basis.
  • Genetic + genomic data — special-category and highly identifying. Even partial genotype string is sensitive.
  • Mental-health record content — same category, with social-consequence asymmetry that elevates risk.
  • Safeguarding-investigation records — child / vulnerable-adult protection notes. Disclosure outside the controlled environment may be a criminal offence under safeguarding law.
  • Sexual-health / HIV / GUM records — additional national restrictions in many jurisdictions (UK NHS Act 1977, etc.).
  • Reproductive-health records in restrictive jurisdictions — varies; relevant for cross-border practices.
  • Death-by-suicide records + coroner-related notes — restricted handling under multiple national regimes.
  • Donor / transplant records — highly identifying.
  • Insurance + claims notes — may identify diagnosis indirectly via covered-procedure codes.
!
Pseudonymised ≠ anonymised

Removing a name doesn't make a record safe to paste. Pseudonymised data is still personal data under GDPR. Re-identification via combination of free-text features + AI is straightforward. Only properly-anonymised data falls outside GDPR — and "properly" is a high bar (ICO Anonymisation Code).

Sub-module 2 takeaway
Twelve additional categories on top of the universal never-paste list. Special-category by default; pseudonymisation isn't a shield; the consequence of leak is patient harm, not policy violation.
Sub-module 3 of 9

AI as a Medical Device — when an AI tool crosses the SaMD threshold

Some AI tools are medical devices in the eyes of EU MDR + MHRA + FDA. Many SMB healthcare providers don't realise this; they procure a "decision-support" tool that is, regulatorily, an unauthorised Class IIa medical device. The fine + clinical-safety consequence is significant.

The MDR definition (Article 2(1))

"Medical device" — any instrument, apparatus, software, etc. intended by the manufacturer to be used for human beings for: diagnosis, prevention, monitoring, prediction, prognosis, treatment, or alleviation of disease — among other purposes. "Intended by the manufacturer" is the key phrase: intended-purpose statements drive classification.

Rule 11 (Annex VIII) — software classification

Software intended to provide information for decisions for diagnosis or therapeutic purposes is at least Class IIa. Where the decisions could cause serious deterioration / surgical intervention / death — Class IIb or III.

Worked examples

  • AI that summarises a consultation transcript for the clinician's review — usually NOT a medical device (administrative use). Document the intended purpose.
  • AI that flags an X-ray for radiologist review — YES, medical device, Class IIa. CE/UKCA required.
  • AI receptionist that triages "is this an emergency?" — likely YES, medical device + EU AI Act Annex III §5(a) high-risk. Both apply.
  • AI that drafts a referral letter from notes — borderline; depends on whether the AI selects clinical content or just rewords human-authored content. Document intended purpose carefully.
  • AI mental-health chatbot for self-help — YES, medical device in most regulators' interpretation; some recent enforcement.
  • AI tool that predicts patient no-show risk — NOT a medical device (administrative); still GDPR + ICO scrutiny.

What to do as an SMB deployer

  1. For any procured AI tool, ask: "is this CE-marked or UKCA-marked as a medical device, and at what class?"
  2. If the vendor says "we are not a medical device", get the documented intended-purpose statement; verify it doesn't match Rule 11.
  3. If the answer is unclear, treat as suspected medical device and engage MHRA / Notified Body before deployment.
  4. If CE/UKCA-marked, request the technical documentation + clinical evaluation summary; deployer-side DCB0160 case file references it.
  5. Vigilance reporting routes are in the user manual; deployer is responsible for setting up the route.
!
"Wellness" labels are not exempt

Vendors sometimes ship a clinical tool labelled "wellness" or "lifestyle" to avoid MDR. If the intended purpose under any reasonable reading is diagnosis / monitoring / treatment, MHRA will look through the label. Don't accept "wellness" framing for a tool you intend to use clinically.

Sub-module 3 takeaway
If the AI helps with diagnosis, prognosis, monitoring, or treatment, it's a medical device. CE/UKCA-marked or it doesn't get deployed. Intended-purpose document is the artefact that decides — get it.
Sub-module 4 of 9

Clinical-safety case files — DCB0129 + DCB0160 in practice

Two UK clinical-safety standards govern any digital health system that could affect patient safety. DCB0129 applies to the manufacturer of the system; DCB0160 applies to the deployer (your practice / trust). Both require a named clinical-safety officer + a documented clinical-safety case + ongoing surveillance.

DCB0129 — manufacturer (your AI vendor)

  • Named clinical-safety officer (clinically qualified + appropriately trained).
  • Hazard analysis covering reasonably foreseeable misuse.
  • Clinical-safety case report covering the system's clinical use.
  • Hazard log maintained throughout the product lifecycle.
  • Post-market clinical-safety surveillance.

Ask the vendor for: their DCB0129 case file; the name of their clinical-safety officer; their hazard log summary; their last review date.

DCB0160 — deployer (you)

  • Named clinical-safety officer at your practice / trust.
  • Local hazard analysis covering how the system will be used by your staff in your workflows.
  • Clinical-safety case report covering local clinical use.
  • Local hazard log; updates with each material change.
  • Staff training records demonstrating safe use.

SMB practices commonly don't have a clinical-safety officer. The Health and Social Care Act 2012 makes DCB0160 compliance mandatory for NHS-connected organisations; not having a CSO is a finding. MSPs serving healthcare often help by sub-contracting a clinical-safety officer service.

Practical SMB workflow

  1. Identify whether any AI tool in scope is a clinical decision-support system (sub-module 3). If yes, both DCB0129 (vendor) and DCB0160 (you) apply.
  2. Name the local CSO. Brief them.
  3. Run a local hazard analysis with the CSO + a representative clinician + the IG lead.
  4. Document the safety case; sign off.
  5. Train staff; record the training.
  6. Maintain the hazard log; review quarterly.
Sub-module 4 takeaway
DCB0129 + DCB0160 are not optional for NHS-connected organisations using clinical AI. Vendor brings 0129; you bring 0160. Both need a named CSO and a documented safety case. No CSO is the audit finding.
Sub-module 5 of 9

Triage chatbots, AI receptionists — consumer-facing healthcare AI

"AI receptionist" tools that screen incoming calls + classify urgency are increasingly marketed to SMB practices. Most are partially or fully medical devices. Most are also EU AI Act Annex III §5(a) high-risk where they perform triage / emergency dispatch. The combination is heavy regulatory load that vendors often understate.

The combined regulatory load

  • EU MDR — Class IIa or higher under Rule 11 if the system informs clinical urgency decisions.
  • EU AI Act — Annex III §5(a) high-risk where used for triage / dispatch of emergency services; Article 26 deployer duties bite.
  • GDPR — Article 9 special-category (health data); Article 22 if the patient experience is materially shaped by the AI decision; Article 50 transparency to interacting natural persons (the patient must know they're talking to an AI).
  • CQC + clinical-safety — patient-safety case under DCB0160; CQC inspection considers AI handling under "safe" and "well-led" domains.
  • Equality + accessibility — Equality Act 2010 obligations: AI receptionist must be accessible to disabled patients (deaf, speech-difference, English-as-additional-language); reasonable adjustments required.

Procurement red flags

  • Vendor cannot produce CE/UKCA marking + class.
  • Vendor cannot produce DCB0129 case file.
  • Vendor will not commit to specific accuracy / sensitivity / specificity metrics on triage decisions.
  • "Wellness" labelling on a tool that triages clinical urgency.
  • No documented human-oversight mechanism (Art 14 AI Act).
  • No Article 50 transparency mechanism (the bot doesn't disclose it's an AI).
  • No equality / accessibility statement.
  • No documented bias / disparate-impact assessment (especially for AAVE, regional accents, non-native English).
!
The "111-equivalent" trap

Vendors sometimes market triage AI as "equivalent to NHS 111". NHS 111 operates under specific clinical-governance arrangements + clinical staff supervision. A vendor product is not "equivalent" unless it meets the same clinical governance standard. Don't accept the comparison without evidence.

Sub-module 5 takeaway
AI receptionists / triage chatbots are usually medical devices + Annex III high-risk + Article 9 special-category processors + CQC concern + equality-obligation surfaces all at once. If you procure one, satisfy every layer.

Cross-border patient data — UK / EU / US flows

Healthcare data crosses borders the moment a UK practice uses a US-hosted EHR, an EU radiology service uses a US-hosted AI, or telehealth platform serves patients in another jurisdiction. The legal stack changes depending on direction.

FlowPrimary legal stackKey controls
UK → EUUK GDPR + EU GDPR; adequacy decision in forceDPA + Art 9 lawful basis; no SCCs required pending adequacy renewal
EU → UKEU GDPR + UK adequacySame; monitor adequacy review cycle
UK → USUK GDPR + UK-US Data Bridge (extension of EU-US Data Privacy Framework)Vendor must be Data Bridge certified; otherwise SCCs + TIA; HIPAA likely also applies
EU → USEU GDPR + EU-US Data Privacy FrameworkVendor must be DPF certified; otherwise SCCs + Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA); HIPAA likely also applies
US → UK/EUHIPAA + UK/EU GDPR for inboundBAA with vendor; GDPR controllership analysis; potentially DPA in addition
Within EU (multi-country)EU GDPR; national health-data derogations under Art 9(4)Map each member-state derogation; processing in one MS may need basis in another

When AI is in the loop

AI hosting is itself a cross-border event. Common scenarios:

  • UK practice → AI tool whose backend is Azure OpenAI in West Europe (Netherlands). UK→EU flow; covered by adequacy.
  • UK practice → AI tool whose backend is US-hosted. UK→US; Data Bridge or SCC required + TIA + HIPAA BAA likely.
  • EU practice → AI tool whose backend uses Bing grounding (public web). Public-web exposure of patient data — usually unacceptable for Article 9 data.
  • NHS-connected practice → any AI: NHS DSPT statement of compliance must reflect any cross-border processing.
§
Adequacy is a live thing

Adequacy decisions are reviewed periodically and can be paused or revoked. Schrems II (2020) invalidated the previous EU-US privacy regime overnight. Your transfer-impact assessment + alternative SCC pathway must be maintained even when an adequacy decision is in force. Plan for the worst case.

Sub-module 6 takeaway
Cross-border patient data is six different legal stacks depending on direction. Pin down the AI vendor's hosting + sub-processor list + adequacy status before procuring; maintain TIA + SCC fallback as live documents.
Sub-module 7 of 9

Patient-safety reporting — Yellow Card · MHRA · MDR Art 87 · AI Act Art 73

A patient-safety incident involving an AI tool fires multiple reporting clocks. Each has a different recipient, a different definition, and a different window. The fastest clock starts first; the slowest finishes last; all must be filed.

The reporting clock matrix

MechanismWho reportsRecipientWindow
Yellow Card (UK)Manufacturer, deployer, clinician, or patientMHRA"As soon as possible"; serious incidents within days, immediate for life-threatening
MDR Article 87 — vigilanceManufacturer primarily; deployer cooperatesCompetent authority of the MS where the incident occurredSerious incident: 15 days; serious public-health threat: 2 days; death / unanticipated serious deterioration: immediate
EU AI Act Article 73 — serious incidentProvider of high-risk AI systemMarket surveillance authority of the MS where incident occurred15 days from awareness; 2 days if widespread infringement or fatal outcome
GDPR Article 33 — controller breachControllerSupervisory authority72 hours from awareness
GDPR Article 34 — data subjectsControllerAffected data subjectsWithout undue delay where high risk
HIPAA Breach NotificationCovered entity / business associateHHS OCR + affected individuals + (sometimes) media60 days max for individuals; 60 days for HHS OCR if < 500 affected, immediately if 500+
NHS DSPT (UK)NHS-connected organisationNHS England + ICOInitial report immediately; full report per DSPT timeline
CQC notificationRegistered providerCQC"Without delay" for notifiable events under Regulation 18
NIS2 (where in scope)Essential / important entityCSIRTEarly warning 24h, notification 72h, final report 1 month

Practical workflow when a clinical AI incident occurs

  1. Stabilise patient (always first; never delay clinical action for reporting).
  2. Preserve evidence (the AI chat, the audit log, the screenshot, the time-of-event).
  3. Internal escalation to CSO + DPO + IG lead + clinical lead within 15 minutes.
  4. Determine which clocks have started: medical device (MDR/Yellow Card)? AI Act (provider you)? GDPR (data breach)? CQC notification? DSPT? NIS2?
  5. Start the fastest clock first; document each report's reference number.
  6. If you are also the AI provider (rare for SMB), separate provider-side from deployer-side reports.
  7. Maintain a single timeline document showing each clock + filing time + outcome.
!
Don't conflate reports

A combined "all-in-one" report to multiple authorities fails most regulators' formal requirements. Each authority wants its own report on its own template within its own window. Use the same factual base but produce separate filings.

Sub-module 7 takeaway
Nine clocks for a clinical AI incident. Three within hours, three within days. Stabilise patient first; preserve evidence; file each clock separately on its own template.
Sub-module 8 of 9

Incident escalation — which regulator hears, in what order

The incident form covers the universal flow. This sub-module adds the healthcare-specific sequencing. The first hour is patient-care-first; the first 24 hours is reporting-first.

First 15 minutes

  1. Clinician on duty manages immediate patient care; no AI tool delays clinical action.
  2. Practice manager + named CSO notified.
  3. Preserve evidence (chat thread, audit log, screenshots, before-and-after).
  4. Open the incident-form workflow.

First 2 hours

  • CSO assesses clinical safety; halts the AI tool if patient-safety risk continues.
  • IG lead + DPO assess data-protection implications.
  • Clinical lead notifies affected patient(s) where appropriate (subject to clinical judgement).
  • Internal incident-management framework activated.

First 24 hours

  • MHRA vigilance / Yellow Card — serious incident filed.
  • CQC notification — Regulation 18 "without delay" filing where applicable.
  • NHS England + DSPT — initial notification.
  • ICO / supervisory authority — GDPR Article 33 72-hour clock running.
  • NIS2 CSIRT — early warning if in scope.
  • Insurer — E&O / cyber / clinical-negligence policy notification.
  • Defence Union / Medical Protection — for clinicians involved.

First 7 days

  • EU AI Act Article 73 — if you are also provider of high-risk AI system, 15-day filing.
  • MDR Article 87 — 15-day filing if device manufacturer.
  • HIPAA Breach Notification — if US patient data involved.
  • Affected patient notification — GDPR Article 34 where high risk; clinical duty of candour parallel obligation.
  • Updated clinical-safety case + hazard log entry.
  • External counsel engagement on patient / regulator comms.
  • Board / Practice partners briefed.

NHS-specific routing notes

  • For NHS England-commissioned services, the Strategic Executive Information System (StEIS) is the reporting platform for serious incidents involving NHS care.
  • Local Safety Standards for Invasive Procedures (LocSSIPs) may engage if the AI affected a procedural step.
  • National-Patient-Safety reporting via NHS England Learn from Patient Safety Events (LFPSE) where applicable.
Sub-module 8 takeaway
Patient-care first; reporting first thereafter. Five-to-nine regulators in the first 24 hours depending on scope; multiple clocks running in parallel. Build the routing matrix during onboarding, not during the incident.
Sub-module 9 of 9

Final assessment

Ten judgement questions exercising the healthcare overlay. Self-mark. Suggested pass: 8/10.

Question 1

A GP wants to paste a single consultation note into ChatGPT free to "tidy the wording". The patient name has been removed. Is this OK?

Answer

No. The note is still personal data under GDPR (pseudonymised); it's special-category (health) under Article 9. Pasting it into a consumer LLM without a lawful basis + appropriate data-handling agreement is an Article 9 + Article 5 + likely Article 6 breach. Fix: use an approved, contractually-protected AI inside the controlled environment; train staff that pseudonymisation isn't a shield; treat historical pastes as reportable.

Question 2

A vendor sells an "AI receptionist" that does urgency triage on incoming calls. They say it's not a medical device. Should you accept the framing?

Answer

No without evidence. Urgency triage is decision-making that informs clinical care; under MDR Rule 11 this is at least Class IIa. Also Annex III §5(a) high-risk under EU AI Act. Ask for the documented intended-purpose statement; verify it doesn't match Rule 11; if it does, require CE/UKCA marking before procuring. "Wellness" or "administrative" labels don't override regulator interpretation.

Question 3

Your practice deploys an AI tool that summarises consultation transcripts for clinician review. Is DCB0160 required?

Answer

Likely yes. Even an administrative summarisation tool affects clinical workflow + decisions are made from its output. If the practice is NHS-connected, DCB0160 deployer clinical-safety case is mandatory. Required: named CSO, local hazard analysis, safety case report, hazard log, staff training records. Not having a CSO is the most common DSPT audit finding.

Question 4

An AI tool you procured is hosted in US. Patient data goes to US for processing. What's the legal stack?

Answer

UK GDPR (controller obligations) + UK-US Data Bridge OR Standard Contractual Clauses + Transfer Impact Assessment if vendor not Data Bridge certified + Article 9 lawful basis + HIPAA Business Associate Agreement (if vendor is a US covered-entity-adjacent processor) + adequacy-review-cycle monitoring. Document each layer. Don't proceed without the BAA + transfer mechanism.

Question 5

An AI tool gives a clinically-significant wrong answer to a clinician question; the clinician follows it; a patient suffers harm. How many clocks fire?

Answer

Up to nine depending on context: Yellow Card (MHRA), MDR Article 87 (if device), EU AI Act Article 73 (if provider), GDPR Article 33 (if personal data exposed), GDPR Article 34 (data subject notification), CQC Regulation 18 notification, NHS DSPT initial + StEIS / LFPSE, NIS2 CSIRT (if essential entity), HIPAA Breach Notification (if US data involved). Plus insurer + defence union. Stabilise patient first; preserve evidence; file each clock separately.

Question 6

An AI mental-health support chatbot is being procured for a primary-care practice. Patient self-help. What's the regulatory analysis?

Answer

(a) Almost certainly a medical device under MDR (Rule 11) — mental-health intervention software has been treated as Class IIa or higher in recent regulator decisions; (b) Annex I integration if it is part of a regulated patient pathway; (c) Article 9 GDPR (health + mental-health); (d) Article 22 if any decision is materially made by the AI; (e) Article 50 transparency to interacting patients; (f) DCB0160 clinical-safety case; (g) Equality Act accessibility obligations; (h) safeguarding considerations especially for under-18 use. Only procure CE/UKCA-marked; require vendor's DCB0129 + clinical-safety officer details.

Question 7

Your vendor refuses to provide the DCB0129 clinical-safety case file. They say it's "commercial-confidential". Do you proceed?

Answer

No. DCB0129 case files exist precisely to be shared with deployers so deployer-side DCB0160 can reference them. A vendor refusing to share is either (a) doesn't have one (procurement disqualifier), or (b) chooses not to (effective disqualifier for NHS-connected procurement). Walk away or escalate to MHRA via the unsatisfactory-product channel.

Question 8

A clinician uses a non-approved consumer AI to draft a referral letter from notes; they realise mid-draft the AI has fabricated a clinical finding. What do you do?

Answer

(1) Discard the AI output; do not include any AI-generated clinical statement in the referral. (2) Re-draft manually. (3) Report the use as an AUP violation but as a learning event, not disciplinary. (4) If the referral was sent: notify the receiving clinician immediately and amend. (5) If patient was affected: clinical duty of candour applies; report. (6) Open the incident-form workflow; the fabricated finding is a near-miss patient-safety event even if not actualised. (7) Tighten the AUP + training; this user found the failure mode safely — protect that.

Question 9

NHS DSPT renewal is in 8 weeks. Your practice has deployed Copilot for M365 in the last month. What's the DSPT impact?

Answer

The DSPT self-assessment must reflect the M365 Copilot deployment including: data-processor relationship + DPA with Microsoft; tenant region + EU Data Boundary commitment for the workload; data-flow diagram including Copilot grounding; access-control posture for SharePoint + groups that Copilot can read; staff training reflecting the additional AI literacy; updated incident-response runbook. Don't tick "no change" if Copilot landed in the period.

Question 10

An MSP wants to deliver a "DSPT + Copilot + AI Safe@Work" rollout for an SMB GP practice. What's the standard deliverable?

Answer

(a) 30-day rollout guide + Microsoft Copilot track + this healthcare overlay; (b) Named CSO under DCB0160 (sub-contractor where needed); (c) AUP extended with the healthcare never-paste list (Sub-module 2); (d) DPIA covering the Article 9 processing + Copilot specifics; (e) DSPT update covering the deployment; (f) DCB0160 clinical-safety case for the affected clinical workflow(s); (g) incident form healthcare-extended (Sub-module 8 multi-regulator routing); (h) training register for all clinical + admin staff; (i) Vendor-due-diligence pack covering Microsoft as ICT third party + CE/UKCA status. End state: an audit binder satisfying CQC + ICO + NHS England + MHRA + practice partners.
