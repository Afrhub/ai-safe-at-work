Post-market monitoring

AI Post-Market Monitoring Plan for [System Name]

The living record of how you watch a high-risk AI system once it is in use, what you measure, the thresholds that trigger action, who reviews it, and how serious incidents get reported. Aligns with EU AI Act Art 72 (post-market monitoring system), Art 73 (reporting of serious incidents), Art 26(5) (deployer monitoring duty), and ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 9 (performance evaluation) & Clause 10 (improvement).

Keep this plan alongside the AI System Card. Review on the cadence set below, and after every model, data, or usage change. Update the corrective-action log the same day as any breach.
Purpose and scope

Why post-market monitoring is required

Governance done at procurement and deployment tells you a system was safe on the day it went live. It does not tell you it stayed that way. Models drift, data shifts, people use tools in ways nobody designed for, and harms often only show up in the aggregate over weeks. A Post-Market Monitoring Plan is:

  • A legal obligation, the EU AI Act requires providers to operate a post-market monitoring system (Art 72) and to report serious incidents (Art 73). Deployers of high-risk systems must monitor operation and inform the provider or authority when they spot a risk or a serious incident (Art 26(5)).
  • An early-warning system, defined thresholds turn a vague "something feels off" into a specific, actionable breach with an owner and a clock.
  • The improvement loop, ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 9 (performance evaluation) and Clause 10 (improvement) expect measured performance to feed corrective action.
  • Audit evidence, it shows a regulator that oversight is continuous, not front-loaded.

Scope

Complete one plan per high-risk AI system. This plan covers:

  1. The AI system named above, in its production deployment (not development or test instances).
  2. All the ways it can degrade or cause harm, drift, accuracy or quality loss, safety failures, bias, misuse, and loss of availability.
  3. The full monitoring loop, from raw telemetry through thresholds, review, escalation, incident reporting, and corrective action.
  4. The triggers that force this plan (and the underlying risk assessment) to be re-reviewed.

4. Escalation path when a threshold is breached

  1. Detect & log. Whoever spots the breach records it (date, signal, observed value vs threshold) and notifies the plan owner.
  2. Triage. The plan owner assesses severity: routine degradation, material risk, or potential serious incident.
  3. Contain. Where user harm is plausible, apply mitigations, tighten guardrails, restrict scope, or take the system offline pending review.
  4. Report up. Notify [risk lead / DPO / senior owner]. For deployers, inform the provider of any risk identified (Art 26(5)).
  5. Serious incident? If it meets the definition below, start the Art 73 reporting clock and complete the AI Incident Form.
  6. Record corrective action. Log the action, owner, and outcome in section 6.

5. Serious incidents & the reporting clock

A serious incident (EU AI Act Art 3(49) / Art 73) is any incident or malfunction of an AI system that directly or indirectly leads to, or could lead to, one of the following:

  • The death of a person, or serious harm to a person's health.
  • A serious and irreversible disruption of critical infrastructure.
  • An infringement of obligations under Union law intended to protect fundamental rights.
  • Serious harm to property or the environment.

The Art 73 reporting clock (report to the relevant market surveillance authority):

SituationReporting deadline (from awareness)
Serious incident (general)Without undue delay, and no later than 15 days
Widespread infringement or serious & irreversible disruption of critical infrastructureImmediately, no later than 2 days
Death of a personImmediately once the link is established, no later than 10 days

Do not wait for full facts to file, an initial report followed by a complete one is expected. Capture every detail on the AI Incident Form and start the clock from the moment you become aware.

7. Re-review triggers

Re-run this plan and the underlying risk assessment whenever any of the following occurs, do not wait for the scheduled cadence:

  • Model change, provider ships a new model version, you retrain, or you change providers.
  • Data change, the input data source, schema, population, or labelling approach shifts.
  • Usage change, a new user group, use case, jurisdiction, or volume step-change.
  • Any serious incident, or a cluster of lower-severity breaches pointing at a systemic issue.
  • Regulatory change, new guidance, standard, or authority requirement.

Rolling this out
  1. Complete one plan per high-risk system. Start from the AI System Card so system details stay consistent.
  2. Define your signals, sources, and thresholds in section 2. Make thresholds specific and measurable, so a breach is unambiguous.
  3. Wire the signals into your Monitoring & KPI dashboard where you can, and name a human reviewer for the rest.
  4. Rehearse the escalation and Art 73 reporting clock before you need it. Confirm who files, and their deputy.
  5. Review on cadence, and re-run the plan on any model, data, or usage change. Log every corrective action the same day.
