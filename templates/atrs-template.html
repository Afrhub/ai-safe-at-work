Public-sector algorithmic transparency record

Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard.

A starter ATRS record for any UK public-sector body deploying an algorithmic tool that affects citizens. Tier 1 (short, public-facing) plus Tier 2 (detailed, also public). Draft both, internal-review, publish before deployment. Mandatory for central government; recommended + de facto expected for local authorities, NDPBs, NHS, FE colleges, MATs.

Print A4. File alongside FRIA + DPIA + PSED s.149 assessment. Publish to your authority's ATRS register or the Cabinet Office repository.
Statement of purpose

ATRS record for [Tool name] at [Organisation name]

Record version: 1.0 · Date published: [YYYY-MM-DD] · Owner organisation: [Organisation full name] · Owning team / SRO: [Team + Senior Responsible Officer] · Review cycle: Annual, or on material change to the algorithm / data / oversight

What ATRS is and why this record exists

The Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard (ATRS) is the UK Government's standardised disclosure format for algorithmic tools used in public-sector decision-making. It was developed by the Cabinet Office Central Digital and Data Office (CDDO) with the Centre for Data Ethics and Innovation (now part of DSIT). The standard exists so citizens, journalists, regulators, and elected representatives can find, understand, and challenge the algorithmic tools that affect decisions about them.

ATRS is mandatory for central government departments and many arm's-length bodies. It is recommended and de facto expected for local authorities, NHS bodies, NDPBs, multi-academy trusts and FE colleges where algorithmic tools influence decisions affecting citizens. Publishing an ATRS record before deployment is the public-trust posture; explaining the absence of one when a journalist asks is the alternative.

Tier 1 begins below, short, public-facing summary, written for citizen comprehension. Aim for under 400 words. The Cabinet Office repository renders this fields-only.
Tier 1 · Public-facing summary

1. Tool name + summary description

Tool name: [Public-facing tool name, e.g. "Housing register prioritisation assistant"]

One-paragraph plain-English description: [Two-to-four sentences. What the tool does, in language a non-specialist citizen understands. Avoid jargon. Avoid hedging.]

2. How the tool affects decisions about people

Describe in plain language how the tool participates in a decision that affects a citizen. State whether the tool's output is:

  • Informing a decision made by a named human (most common).
  • Recommending a decision that a named human reviews + accepts or overrides.
  • Determining a decision with no human in the loop (rare; engages GDPR Article 22 + AI Act high-risk).

This tool's role: [Informing / Recommending / Determining] · What a citizen sees: [e.g. "Their housing-priority band, communicated in a written letter, with the named decision-maker's contact details."]

3. Why the tool is being used

Problem the tool addresses: [Plain-language statement of the citizen-affecting or service-delivery problem.]

Expected benefit: [E.g. faster decisions, more consistent assessments, identification of vulnerable applicants.]

Alternative considered + why this is preferred: [At least one alternative considered + named reason for selecting this option.]

4. Owner + contact

  • Owner organisation: [Organisation full legal name + abbreviation where used.]
  • Department / unit: [Department + service area.]
  • Senior Responsible Officer: [Name + role title.]
  • Citizen-facing contact: [E.g. "AI algorithm queries: feedback@[org].gov.uk; complaints route: complaints@[org].gov.uk; LGSCO / PHSO escalation route published at ."]
Tier 1 ends. Anything below is Tier 2 detail, also public, but typically more technical. Tier 2 supports auditor, journalist, and academic scrutiny; Tier 1 supports citizen comprehension.
Tier 2 · Detail

5. Detailed description of what the algorithm does

Describe the algorithm in enough detail that a technically literate reader can understand the approach without seeing the source code.

  • Algorithm type: [Rule-based decision system / statistical model (regression, decision tree) / machine-learning classifier / large language model / hybrid / etc.]
  • Inputs: [Categories of input data the algorithm uses.]
  • Outputs: [Categories of output the algorithm produces.]
  • Decision logic at a high level: [E.g. "The algorithm applies a weighted score across eight criteria, producing a priority band of A-D. Citizens scoring above 8.5 are placed in band A."]
  • Confidence / uncertainty information: [How the algorithm communicates confidence to the human decision-maker.]

6.1 Input data

  • Categories of personal data: [Standard, special-category (Art 9), criminal-offence (Art 10).]
  • Categories of non-personal data: [E.g. service-level statistics, demographic aggregates.]
  • Source(s): [Internal system names / external feeds with provenance.]
  • Refresh cadence: [Real-time / daily / weekly / per-decision / etc.]

6.2 Training data (if applicable)

  • Training-data scope: [E.g. historical decisions 2020-2024 from the same authority; n=ɸ records.]

6.3 Lawful basis (UK GDPR)

  • Article 6 condition: [6(1)(e) public task is most common; cite specific statutory function.]
  • Article 9 condition (if special-category): [E.g. 9(2)(g) substantial public interest with appropriate Schedule 1 condition.]

7. Human oversight arrangements

Describe how a named human exercises meaningful oversight per EU AI Act Article 14 and UK AI Playbook expectations.

  • Oversight role(s): [Named role + reporting line + training required.]
  • What the human sees: [Algorithm output + input data summary + confidence + alternative options.]
  • Authority to override: [Yes; documented; reason for override recorded.]
  • Automation-bias mitigation: [E.g. mandatory second-opinion above threshold; sampled audit of acceptance decisions.]
  • Override rate monitored: [Cadence + threshold for review.]
  • Article 86 right-to-explanation operationalisation: [Process by which an affected citizen receives a clear and meaningful explanation of the AI's role in the decision.]

Publish ATRS before deploying, not after. The audit story when a citizen FOIs your authority's AI use is "yes, here is the published record" or "we are not yet at published-record maturity but here is the in-flight inventory". The third option, "we don't know", is the one that costs trust.
