AI System Inventory & Model Card for [Company Name]

Track every AI system in use, not just the tools: the models behind them, the datasets they touch, the vendors involved, a named owner, and where each system sits in its lifecycle. Aligns with EU AI Act Art 26 (deployer obligations), ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 8 (operational planning and control), GDPR Art 30 (records of processing).

Maintain the inventory in a shared spreadsheet and keep one model card per system alongside it. Review monthly; full review quarterly.
Purpose and scope

Why an inventory, not just a tool list

A tool register tells you what software is installed. A system inventory tells you what your organisation actually does with AI. The difference matters:

  • One tool, many systems. A single tool (say, ChatGPT Enterprise) can power a CV-screening workflow, a customer-email drafter, and a contract summariser. Same tool, three systems, three very different risk profiles.
  • Obligations attach to systems in context. The EU AI Act classifies risk by what the system is used for, not by the underlying model. CV screening is high-risk under Annex III; drafting a newsletter with the same model is not.
  • Accountability needs a name. Every system gets a named owner who answers for its data, its oversight, and its review dates. "IT owns the tool" is not the same as "someone owns the system".
  • Audit evidence. Regulators and auditors ask "show me what AI systems you deploy and how you control them" (EU AI Act Art 26, ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 8). This inventory, plus its model cards, is that answer.

Keep your AI Tool Register for the tools themselves. This inventory sits one level up: the workflows and use cases built on those tools.

Column notes

  • Lifecycle status: Proposed (idea or pilot, not approved) · Approved (signed off, not yet in use) · Live (in production use) · Retired (switched off; keep the row for audit history).
  • EU AI Act risk tier: Prohibited · High-risk (Annex III use cases such as employment, credit, education) · Limited risk (transparency duties) · Minimal risk. Classify by the use, not the model.
  • Data categories touched: Use the same categories as your Tool Register: public-only, internal non-sensitive, confidential business, personal data, special category.
Section 2 · Model card

Model card (one per system)

Complete one card for every system on the inventory. Keep it to a single page; it is the "passport" a manager, auditor, or new starter reads to understand the system in two minutes. Print or copy this blank card for each system.

System name[System name]
What it does[One or two sentences: purpose, users, and where it sits in the workflow]
Model + version[e.g. GPT-5, accessed via ChatGPT Enterprise; record the version or date]
Training-data provenance[What the vendor states about training data; link to the vendor statement or DPA]
Known limitations[Hallucination, bias, knowledge cutoff, weak languages or domains]
Human oversight in place (Art 14)[Who reviews outputs, at what point, and with what authority to override or stop]
Performance checks run[What you tested, when, sample size, results; e.g. accuracy spot-check on 50 outputs]
Approved uses[Explicit list of what this system may be used for]
Prohibited uses[Explicit list of what it must not be used for, e.g. final decisions about individuals]
Owner + review date[Named owner] · next review [date]
Section 3 · Review cadence

Review cadence

  1. Monthly light check (15 minutes, by the inventory owner): any new systems appeared? Any owners changed roles? Any vendor notices received? Update rows; no deep review.
  2. Quarterly full review (owner plus each system owner): re-confirm every row, re-read each model card, check performance-check results are current, and confirm lifecycle status and risk tier still hold.
  3. Re-review on any material change, within one week of: a model swap or major version change, a new data category being fed to the system, a new use for an existing system, or a vendor terms change. A material change can move a system's risk tier, so treat it as a new approval, not a footnote.

Standards referenced

  • EU AI Act 2024/1689, Article 26 (deployer obligations: oversight, monitoring, input-data control), Article 14 (human oversight), Annex III (high-risk use cases).
  • ISO/IEC 42001:2023, Clause 8 (operational planning and control, including AI system impact assessment and lifecycle management).
  • GDPR 2016/679, Article 30 (records of processing activities), Article 35 (DPIA where processing is likely high-risk).
i
Rolling this out
  1. Start from your AI Tool Register: for each tool, ask "what workflows actually run on this?" Each answer is a system row.
  2. Name an owner for every system before it goes on the inventory. No owner, no approval.
  3. Complete a model card per system, starting with anything touching personal data or decisions about people.
  4. Classify each system's EU AI Act risk tier by use case; escalate anything that looks Annex III (employment, credit, education) to your DPO before it goes live.
  5. Put the monthly check and quarterly review in the diary now, owned by a named person.
