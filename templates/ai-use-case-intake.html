Approval workflow

AI Use Case Intake & Approval Form for [Company Name]

The gate before any new AI use goes live: someone requests it, someone assesses it, someone approves or refuses it, and the sign-off is recorded. Aligns with ISO/IEC 42001 Clause 6.1 (risk and opportunity assessment), EU AI Act Art 4 (AI literacy) and Art 26 (deployer obligations), and the GDPR Art 35 DPIA trigger check.

Use one copy per request. File completed forms with your AI Tool Register. Keep refused requests too, they are audit evidence of governance working.
Purpose and scope

Why approval comes before use

Governing AI means deciding before deployment, not recording after. If the first time your DPO hears about a tool is when a customer complains, you are not governing, you are firefighting. This form is your anti-shadow-AI mechanism:

  • A clear front door. Staff know exactly how to get a new AI use approved, so they do not quietly start using tools on personal accounts.
  • A consistent assessment. Every request gets the same data, risk, and legal checks, whoever asks and whoever assesses.
  • A recorded decision. Approve or refuse, with named sign-off and a date. That record is what an auditor, insurer, or regulator asks to see.
  • A feed into your registers. Approved use cases go straight onto your AI Tool Register and AI Risk Register, so nothing goes live untracked.

Use this form for any new AI use: a new tool, a new use of an approved tool, or a change that moves an existing use into a new data category (for example, starting to put customer data into a tool that was approved for internal drafting only).

How to run the process

  1. Publish the front door. Put this form (or a shared-drive / intranet version of it) where staff will find it, and say plainly: no new AI use goes live without it.
  2. Keep the request light. Part A should take a requester ten minutes. If the form is painful, people will route around it, and you are back to shadow AI.
  3. Set a service standard. Commit to a decision within [e.g. 5 working days] for routine requests. Slow approval is the biggest driver of unapproved use.
  4. Record everything. File every completed form, including refusals, with your AI Tool Register. Refusals are some of your best audit evidence: they show the gate actually stops things.
  5. Feed the registers. On approval, update the AI Tool Register (tool, tier, data categories, conditions) and add any notable risks to the AI Risk Register.
  6. Review on schedule. Re-check approved use cases at their review date, or immediately if the vendor changes terms, the data categories change, or an incident occurs.

Standards referenced

  • ISO/IEC 42001:2023, Clause 6.1 (actions to address risks and opportunities, including AI risk assessment and AI system impact assessment before deployment).
  • EU AI Act 2024/1689, Article 4 (AI literacy for staff using AI systems), Article 26 (deployer obligations: use in accordance with instructions, human oversight, monitoring).
  • GDPR 2016/679, Article 35 (Data Protection Impact Assessment where processing is likely to result in high risk to individuals).
i
Rolling this out
  1. Name one owner for intake decisions (e.g. DPO or IT lead) and one deputy, so requests never sit waiting for a single person.
  2. Announce the process to all staff: "Want to use AI for something new? Fill in Part A, decision within [X] days."
  3. Run your existing AI uses through the form retrospectively, it is the fastest way to surface shadow AI and build the habit.
  4. Keep a simple log of requests and decisions (date, requester, use case, decision), and review it quarterly for patterns.
  5. Link the form from your Acceptable Use Policy and your AI Tool Register so the three documents reference each other.
