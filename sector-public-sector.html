AI in the public sector.

A sector overlay on top of the universal course. Public-sector AI carries an audience the private sector doesn't have: the citizens whose lives the decisions affect. Annex III makes many public-services AI uses high-risk by default; Article 27 FRIA is mandatory; the UK AI Playbook and ATRS add their own discipline; G-Cloud procurement runs the table on how you buy. Different rules; same patient.

By the end of this overlay, you'll
  • Know which Annex III categories your service is likely to engage
  • Apply Article 27 FRIA in practice for public-body deployers
  • Publish an Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard (ATRS) record
  • Map the UK AI Playbook (Cabinet Office / DSIT / GDS) to your AI use cases
  • Identify the extended public-sector "never-paste" list (citizen identifiers, casework, welfare, education records)
  • Procure AI under government frameworks (G-Cloud, Crown Commercial Service, EU public procurement)
  • Understand judicial-review and ombudsman risk where AI decisions affect citizens
  • Know which regulator + ombudsman + select committee hears about an AI incident, in what order
  • Pass a 10-question final assessment on public-sector judgement calls

The regulatory stack, read top to bottom

EU AI Act (Reg 2024/1689), applies. Annex III lists public-services AI as high-risk including: §1 biometrics; §2 critical infrastructure; §3 education and vocational training (admission, scoring, classification); §4 employment and worker management; §5(a) emergency triage / dispatch; §5(b) creditworthiness scoring of natural persons; §6 law enforcement (risk assessment, profiling, evidence reliability); §7 migration, asylum, border control; §8 administration of justice and democratic processes. Article 27 FRIA is mandatory for public-sector deployers + private entities providing public services. Article 26 deployer duties bite. Article 50 transparency to citizens interacting with AI.

UK regulatory + policy stack:

  • UK AI Playbook (Cabinet Office / DSIT / GDS, 2024+), government's own approach to AI; recommended controls; public-sector usage expectations
  • Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard (ATRS), mandatory for central government and many ALBs; standardised disclosure of algorithmic tool use including purpose, decision impact, technical detail, oversight, contact route
  • UK GDPR + DPA 2018, Article 22 + Schedule 1 conditions for public-task processing
  • Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile Applications) Accessibility Regulations 2018, WCAG 2.2 AA conformance for citizen-facing services
  • European Accessibility Act (Directive 2019/882), effective 28 June 2025; broader accessibility scope
  • NIS Regulations 2018 / NIS2, public-sector essential entities
  • FOI Act 2000 + EIR 2004, algorithmic transparency obligations via citizen FOI requests
  • Equality Act 2010 + Public Sector Equality Duty (s.149), proactive duty to consider equality impact of AI decisions
  • Judicial review, citizens can challenge AI-affected administrative decisions on Wednesbury / proportionality / procedural grounds
  • Public Services Ombudsman + Local Government Ombudsman, maladministration complaints regarding AI use

National EU member-state stacks: France (CNIL, Conseil d'État, Défenseur des droits); Germany (BfDI, BNetzA, Bundesrechnungshof); Netherlands (CBP, ARK); Italy (Garante per la protezione dei dati personali, Corte dei Conti); Spain (AEPD, Defensor del Pueblo). Each adds local guidance + accountability venues.

US cross-reference, OMB Memos M-24-10 (federal AI use) + M-25-21 (federal procurement of AI) + Executive Orders on AI in government; FedRAMP for cloud; state-level (NYC Local Law 144 for automated employment decisions; Colorado SB169; etc.).

This overlay sits on top of the universal course. Universal course = baseline. Public Sector overlay = accountability + transparency + procurement-framework layer.

The public-sector governance layer, Annex III + UK AI Playbook + national-regulator overlays

The universal AI Act + GDPR baseline is the floor. Public-sector AI adds three things the private sector doesn't have: (a) explicit Annex III high-risk categorisation for most public-services use cases; (b) mandatory Article 27 FRIA before deployment; (c) public-accountability venues that don't exist for private-sector AI failures, ombudsmen, judicial review, select committees, the FOI Act, the press.

For most public-sector deployers the priority sequence is: (1) Article 26 + 27 FRIA if AI use falls under Annex III; (2) ATRS record published before public-facing use; (3) UK AI Playbook controls applied where the body is in scope; (4) Article 9 + Article 22 GDPR assessment if special-category or automated-decisions involved; (5) procurement framework discipline; (6) Equality Act / PSED assessment of disparate impact.

Sub-module 1 takeaway
Public-sector AI carries a regulatory stack the private sector doesn't have to face. Annex III is most likely to apply; FRIA is mandatory; the public is the audience and the venue. Map first; deploy second.
Sub-module 2 of 9

The public-sector never-paste list

Universal Module 3 holds. Public-sector adds categories where the data is statutorily protected, where leakage causes citizen harm, or where the act of pasting itself is a public-trust breach.

Categories to add to the universal never-paste list

  • National Insurance numbers, NHS numbers, Unique Reference Numbers (UPN, LSP, etc.), strong citizen identifiers.
  • Casework records, welfare, social-services, child-protection, housing, asylum, immigration files.
  • Education records of named pupils, attainment, attendance, SEN, behavioural records covered by DfE statutory guidance.
  • Welfare-benefit assessments, UC, PIP, ESA, housing benefit files; criminal injuries; bereavement support.
  • Council-tax + revenue collection notes, debt status, vulnerable-customer flags.
  • Police, prosecution, and offender-management records, even partial information often re-identifies; Schedule 8 DPA 2018 + Law Enforcement Directive restrictions apply.
  • Court-listed information + restrictions of reporting orders, pasting restricted-reporting case detail can be contempt of court.
  • Confidential Cabinet / Council / Committee papers + reserve-deliberation notes, public-trust + statutory confidentiality.
  • Citizens' letters / complaints / freedom-of-information requests pre-response, the citizen has a reasonable expectation of authority-of-handling.
  • Procurement evaluation notes + bidder confidential information, disclosure prejudices bidder + market trust.
  • Personnel and complaint-investigation records, internal HR + grievance + whistleblowing files.
  • Election / electoral-roll restricted data, under Representation of the People Act restrictions.
!
FOI + leak = published

Anything pasted into a consumer AI is at risk of vendor-side retention. If a citizen FOIs your authority's AI usage and you cannot demonstrate where pasted content went, the default story for a regulator + journalist is "it ended up in a model". Defending an FOI response that hand-waves at "we don't know" is publicly damaging; defending one that says "approved tool only, here's the audit" is fine.

Sub-module 2 takeaway
Twelve additional categories on top of the universal list. Statutory protection + public-trust + FOI-exposability + criminal-contempt risk all sit on this list. The audit story has to be "approved tool only".
Sub-module 3 of 9

FRIA in practice, when it is required, how to scope it

Article 27 of the EU AI Act mandates a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment for deployers of high-risk AI in defined scenarios. Public-sector use cases are the central target. The FRIA template covers the structure; this sub-module covers public-sector-specific application.

When FRIA is mandatory under Article 27

Public-sector deployers (bodies governed by public law + private entities providing public services) must conduct a FRIA before first use of a high-risk AI system used to:

  • Evaluate the creditworthiness of natural persons or set credit scores (excludes detection of financial fraud).
  • Carry out risk assessment and pricing for natural persons in life and health insurance.
  • Evaluate eligibility of natural persons for essential public assistance benefits and services (Annex III §5(a)).
  • Other Annex III high-risk areas where the body falls within scope.

How to scope a FRIA for a public-sector deployment

Article 27(1) lists what the FRIA must contain. Adapted for public-sector context:

  1. Process description, what the AI is doing inside which administrative or service-delivery process.
  2. Period and frequency, how long; how often.
  3. Categories of natural persons + groups, which citizens are affected; pay particular attention to protected characteristics (Equality Act s.149); marginalised groups; vulnerable adults; children.
  4. Specific risks of harm, Charter of Fundamental Rights mapping: dignity, privacy, data protection, non-discrimination, fair trial / effective remedy, rights of the child, freedom of expression. For each protected group, articulate at least one credible harm scenario.
  5. Human-oversight measures, who reviews; can they override; how is that recorded; what's the training for that role.
  6. Risk-mitigation measures + governance + complaint mechanisms, including a citizen-facing complaint route specific to AI-affected decisions.

Public-sector specific quality bar

  • The FRIA should reference the body's Public Sector Equality Duty assessment (s.149), they are companion documents.
  • Where the AI affects benefits / housing / education / social care, the FRIA should map specific Charter rights to the specific service-area citizen profile.
  • The FRIA should be published proactively where the body operates under transparency duties, ATRS record will reference the FRIA.
  • Article 27(3) notification to the market-surveillance authority + template form from the AI Office must be honoured.
  • Where the body is also subject to FOI, the FRIA is FOI-disclosable subject to standard exemptions; draft accordingly.
§
FRIA + DPIA + PSED, three companion documents

Public-sector deployments usually need all three: FRIA (Article 27, rights of natural persons affected), DPIA (GDPR Article 35, processing risks), and PSED s.149 assessment (equality impact). Don't combine into one document, each regulator wants its own structure. Cross-reference instead.

Sub-module 3 takeaway
FRIA is mandatory for public-sector deployers of high-risk AI. Six elements per Article 27(1); cross-reference DPIA + PSED; notify market-surveillance authority. The FRIA is also FOI-disclosable, draft to be defensible publicly.
Sub-module 4 of 9

UK Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard, publishing an ATRS record

ATRS is the Cabinet Office's standardised disclosure format for algorithmic tools used in public-sector decision-making. Mandatory for central government departments and many ALBs; voluntary but expected for local authorities, NDPBs, and other public bodies. The standard exists so citizens, journalists, and regulators can find + understand what algorithms a public body is using.

What ATRS requires (Tier 1 and Tier 2)

Tier 1 (high-level summary, public-facing):

  • Tool name + summary description of what it does.
  • How the tool affects decisions about people.
  • Why the tool is being used.
  • Owner organisation + contact point.

Tier 2 (more detailed, also public):

  • Detailed description of what the algorithm does.
  • Data used (input data, training data, processing).
  • Human oversight arrangements.
  • Risks identified + mitigations applied (companion to FRIA / DPIA / PSED).
  • Supplier name (if procured).
  • Date of last review.

When ATRS is mandatory + when it's recommended

  • Mandatory, central government departments + arms-length bodies named in the Cabinet Office requirement (check the current list; it expands periodically).
  • Recommended + de facto expected, local authorities, NHS bodies (in addition to DSPT), NDPBs, NHS trusts, FE colleges, multi-academy trusts where algorithmic decision-making affects pupils.
  • Out of scope, algorithms used purely for internal admin without citizen impact (e.g. internal staff scheduling without performance consequences). Be conservative; "purely internal" is a high bar.

How to publish

  1. Decide whether the use case is in scope. Default: if it affects a citizen or determines a service outcome, it's in scope.
  2. Complete Tier 1 + Tier 2 fields. Draft for public reading, assume a journalist will read it.
  3. Internal review: comms, legal, DPO, FRIA author.
  4. Submit via the Cabinet Office repository (or organisation's published register).
  5. Maintain, update on material change to the algorithm, data, or oversight.
!
"Not yet published" is the worst answer

If a journalist asks "is your ATRS record published?" and the answer is "we're working on it", the story is already partially adverse. Publish before deploying, or accept the procurement-and-deployment risk that the disclosure comes from elsewhere.

Sub-module 4 takeaway
ATRS = the public-sector "show your working". Tier 1 + Tier 2 fields, published before deploying. Mandatory for central government, recommended for everyone else, expected by journalists. Plan publication before deployment.
Sub-module 5 of 9

AI-assisted decisions affecting citizens, Article 22 GDPR + EU AI Act + judicial review

When AI helps make a decision that affects a citizen, benefit eligibility, school admission, housing allocation, planning permission, council-tax band, immigration claim, multiple frameworks engage simultaneously. The decision must be defensible against each.

The four lenses

  1. GDPR Article 22, decisions based solely on automated processing that produce legal or similarly significant effects are prohibited unless one of three exceptions applies; even then, safeguards (human review, contestation right, explanation) are required.
  2. EU AI Act Article 14, meaningful human oversight; the human must be able to override; automation bias must be mitigated.
  3. EU AI Act Article 26(7), workers + their representatives informed of high-risk AI use in employment.
  4. Judicial review, citizens can challenge administrative decisions on three traditional grounds: illegality (ultra vires), irrationality (Wednesbury), procedural impropriety; AI-affected decisions add transparency + explainability + equality questions.

Defending an AI-assisted decision

  • The decision-maker is a named human, not the algorithm. Algorithm is an input.
  • The human has access to the algorithm's reasoning, at least the input data, the relevant features, the recommendation, the confidence level.
  • The human has the authority + the time + the training to override.
  • The human's reasoning is recorded, including where it diverged from the algorithm recommendation.
  • The citizen has a route to challenge, internal review, ombudsman, judicial review.
  • The decision letter explains the basis in citizen-understandable terms, even where the algorithm details are complex.
  • The equality-impact assessment shows the algorithm doesn't disparately disadvantage any protected group.

Article 86 right to explanation

EU AI Act Article 86 (in force August 2026) gives data subjects the right to obtain a clear and meaningful explanation of the role of the AI system in the decision-making procedure + the main elements of the decision taken, for high-risk AI decisions. Build the explanation infrastructure before deployment.

Sub-module 5 takeaway
Citizen-affecting AI decisions defend against four frameworks: Article 22 + Article 14 + Article 86 + judicial review. Named human + meaningful oversight + recorded reasoning + accessible challenge route + understandable explanation, or the decision falls.
Sub-module 6 of 9

Procurement under government frameworks

Public-sector AI procurement runs through framework agreements. G-Cloud (UK), DOS (Digital Outcomes and Specialists), and Crown Commercial Service AI frameworks dominate UK. EU public procurement Directive 2014/24/EU + national equivalents govern EU. Off-framework procurement is possible but requires specific justification + risk acceptance. Buyers should expect to defend the procurement route after the fact.

UK framework landscape

  • G-Cloud (currently iteration 14), Crown Commercial Service framework for cloud + cloud-adjacent services; includes most SaaS AI products. Three lots: Cloud Hosting, Cloud Software, Cloud Support.
  • Digital Outcomes (DOS), for outcomes-based purchases of digital services including AI development + implementation work.
  • AI Dynamic Purchasing System (DPS), newer framework specifically for AI products + services.
  • Wider CCS frameworks, Technology Services 3, Data Centre Services, Vertical Application Solutions, etc.

EU framework landscape

  • Directive 2014/24/EU, main public-procurement directive; thresholds determine OJEU advertisement.
  • EU AI Act Article 25, requires public procurers to demand CE/UKCA-equivalent + Article 16 / 17 compliance from providers of high-risk AI.
  • National framework implementations vary.

AI-specific procurement clauses

  • Provider AI Act compliance, confirmation provider has discharged Article 8-22 obligations (where applicable) before delivery.
  • Risk classification disclosure, provider asserts the AI's risk classification under EU AI Act + provides supporting documentation.
  • FRIA / DPIA artefacts, provider supplies inputs to buyer's FRIA + DPIA.
  • Bias + accuracy reporting, provider commits to specific bias-evaluation reports + accuracy metrics, including disaggregated by protected characteristic where relevant.
  • Data residency + processing, provider confirms data residency + sub-processor list + change-notification mechanism.
  • Audit rights, buyer has explicit audit + inspection rights.
  • Termination + data return, explicit termination conditions; data return + deletion obligations.
  • Material change notification, provider must notify buyer in advance of material changes to the model, training data, or behaviour.
  • Incident notification, provider's incident-notification clock for AI-related incidents (including Article 73 serious incidents).
  • UK / EU jurisdiction + dispute resolution, keep the venue local to the buyer.

Off-framework AI procurement

Off-framework procurement is possible under specific exemptions (small-value, urgency, single-supplier). For AI, expect post-hoc audit, the National Audit Office, the Comptroller and Auditor General, and select committees will all look. Document the exemption justification at the moment of decision.

Sub-module 6 takeaway
Use the framework. Insert AI-specific clauses. Document the route. The procurement route survives audit when the rationale is documented at decision-time, not reconstructed later.
Sub-module 7 of 9

Public accountability and transparency reporting on AI usage

Public bodies report on AI usage to multiple audiences: citizens (via published documents + ATRS), elected members (cabinet, council, executive), regulators (ICO, FRIA-recipient market-surveillance authority), parliament + audit bodies (NAO, C&AG, select committees), and the public via FOI. Build the reporting cadence before it's demanded.

Inherent tensions

  • Commercial confidentiality of the AI vendor vs FOI / ATRS transparency. Negotiate at procurement; flag in contract.
  • Security exemption (likely + harm-test) vs citizen transparency. Don't over-claim security exemption.
  • Personal-data exemption (Section 40 FOI) vs FOI requests about AI use affecting named individuals. Handle case by case.
  • Pre-decisional / policy-development exemption (Section 35/36 FOI) vs ATRS pro-active publication. ATRS sits in the public domain regardless.
Sub-module 7 takeaway
Nine audiences for the same AI deployment. Build the reporting cadence before it's demanded; the public-trust position holds when you arrived at transparency first.
Sub-module 8 of 9

Incident escalation, which regulator + ombudsman + select committee hears, in what order

A public-sector AI incident fires multiple escalation paths. The first hour is internal-containment; the first day is regulator-and-elected-member; the first week is citizen-and-public. The universal incident form is the structure; this sub-module covers the public-sector-specific sequencing.

First hour

  1. Stop the AI usage on the affected service.
  2. Preserve evidence, chat logs, audit trails, screenshots, before-and-after of the affected decision.
  3. Internal incident team + DPO + chief executive + Monitoring Officer (local authority) + Head of Legal.
  4. If a citizen suffered a loss / detriment / wrong decision, identify them; brief frontline service team to handle queries supportively.

First 24 hours

  • ICO / supervisory authority, GDPR Article 33 72-hour clock running; assess whether notification is required.
  • FRIA market-surveillance authority, if FRIA notification has been filed, the material-change clause may be triggered.
  • Internal elected-member / executive briefing, Council Leader / Cabinet Member / equivalent; before press contact.
  • Local Authority Monitoring Officer, has duties under Local Government and Housing Act 1989 to report unlawful + maladministrative behaviour.
  • External counsel, for citizen-affecting incidents, expect to need it.
  • Insurance, local-authority insurers + government risk pools (e.g. Risk Management Partners for English councils) within their notification windows.

First 7 days

  • Affected citizens, direct contact (where identifiable); citizen-facing explanation; complaint route.
  • EU AI Act Article 73 (if provider of high-risk AI), 15-day notification.
  • Public Sector Equality Duty, assess whether the incident has differential equality impact requiring formal review.
  • Press handling, prepare statements; assume FOI requests will follow.
  • ATRS record, update to reflect the incident + the remediation.
  • NAO / C&AG, for material spending implications, anticipate audit interest.

First month + onward

  • Local Government Ombudsman + Public Services Ombudsman complaints, typically arrive within 30-60 days; respond fully.
  • Select-committee letter or hearing, for high-profile incidents; prepare evidence pack.
  • FOI requests, high volume likely; have responses prepared.
  • Internal lessons-learned report; public summary where appropriate.
  • Permanent update to FRIA + DPIA + ATRS + risk register.

Specific UK escalation routes

  • Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman (LGSCO, England), for maladministration by local authorities.
  • Parliamentary and Health Service Ombudsman (PHSO), for central government + NHS.
  • Public Services Ombudsman for Wales / Scotland / Northern Ireland, devolved equivalents.
  • Information Commissioner's Office (ICO), data-protection + FOI.
  • Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC), PSED + protected-characteristics impact.
  • National Audit Office (NAO), value-for-money + procurement audit.
  • Local Government Association (LGA), sector body for shared lessons.
Sub-module 8 takeaway
Public-sector AI incidents have more reporting venues than private sector + add ombudsman, NAO, select committee, FOI, EHRC. Stop usage first; preserve evidence; brief electeds before press; publish update to ATRS. The audit story has to be defensible publicly.
