Module 04 of 11 · 8–12 minutes

Picking the right tool for the job.

By the end of this module you will
  • Understand that different AI tools carry different levels of risk
  • Recognise when enterprise-approved tools should be used
  • Identify unsafe or unapproved AI usage
  • Make safer decisions when selecting AI tools for work

Not all AI tools operate in the same way.

Some AI platforms are designed with:

  • enterprise governance
  • security controls
  • auditability
  • organisational management in mind.

Others are:

  • consumer-focused
  • lightly governed
  • or completely outside organisational oversight.

One of the largest operational AI risks facing organisations today is:

using the wrong AI tool for the wrong task.

This module explains how to:

  • choose safer AI tools
  • recognise governance boundaries
  • reduce unnecessary operational risk.

The Core Principle

The more sensitive the task,

the stronger the governance requirement. Tool selection should not be based only on:

  • convenience
  • popularity
  • or speed.

It should also consider:

  • governance
  • data sensitivity
  • organisational approval
  • operational accountability.

AI Tool Categories

1. Consumer AI Tools

Examples:

  • free ChatGPT
  • personal Claude accounts
  • public Gemini apps
  • AI browser extensions
  • consumer AI writing assistants

Typical Use Cases

  • brainstorming
  • generic drafting
  • low-risk productivity support
  • public content creation

Typical Risks

  • limited governance controls
  • personal account ownership
  • uncertain retention settings
  • reduced organisational oversight
  • unknown data residency

These tools should generally not be used for:

  • confidential business information
  • regulated data
  • customer information
  • or commercially sensitive material

unless explicitly approved by the organisation.

Real-World Scenario

Scenario

"It was quicker on my phone."

A manager uses a personal AI app to summarise:

  • internal strategy discussions
  • staffing concerns
  • financial updates.

The organisation already provides an approved enterprise AI platform, but the employee chooses the personal app because:

"it was faster."

Risks Created

  • shadow AI usage
  • governance bypass
  • loss of auditability
  • unmanaged data exposure

Key Lesson

Convenience should not override governance and approval processes.

2. Enterprise AI Platforms

Examples:

  • ChatGPT Enterprise
  • Microsoft Copilot for M365
  • approved enterprise AI environments

These platforms may provide:

  • stronger administrative controls
  • auditability
  • retention management
  • contractual protections
  • governance tooling.

However:

organisations still remain responsible for configuration, permissions, oversight and safe usage.

Enterprise AI does not remove:

  • hallucination risk
  • human error
  • oversharing
  • or governance failures.

Real-World Scenario

Scenario

"Copilot already had access."

An employee uses Microsoft Copilot to summarise project documentation. The employee assumes:

"it must be safe because it's part of Microsoft 365."

However:

  • SharePoint permissions were poorly managed
  • historical folders contained sensitive information
  • Copilot surfaced information more broadly than expected.

Risks Created

  • oversharing
  • permissions exposure
  • governance weaknesses
  • information leakage

Key Lesson

Enterprise AI security depends heavily on:

  • governance maturity
  • permissions management
  • organisational controls.

3. AI Browser Extensions & Plugins

Examples:

  • AI writing assistants
  • browser summarisation tools
  • AI email plugins
  • AI note-taking extensions

Common Risk

Employees may not realise:

  • the extension uses AI
  • information is processed externally
  • or governance approval has not taken place.

Real-World Scenario

Scenario

"Nobody approved the plugin."

A marketing employee installs a browser extension that:

  • rewrites customer emails
  • summarises webpages
  • drafts social media posts.

No vendor review or governance approval occurs. The extension automatically sends:

  • webpage content
  • customer communications
  • internal text

to external AI services.

Risks Created

  • supplier governance concerns
  • third-party exposure
  • privacy risk
  • shadow AI growth

Key Lesson

AI risk increasingly exists inside:

  • browser plugins
  • SaaS integrations
  • embedded productivity tools.

Not only standalone AI chatbots.

Choosing The Right Tool

Before using AI, ask:

1. Is the tool approved?

Use organisation-approved AI tools wherever possible.

2. What type of information is being processed?

The more sensitive the information:

  • the stronger the governance requirement.

3. Is this a personal or enterprise-managed account?

Personal AI accounts generally create higher operational risk.

4. Is the output business-critical?

If outputs affect:

  • customers
  • finance
  • legal matters
  • HR
  • or compliance

human oversight becomes essential.

5. Does the tool support governance?

Can the organisation:

  • manage usage
  • apply controls
  • or review activity?

If not: risk increases significantly.

Red / Amber / Green Exercise

GREEN — Generally Lower Risk

Approved enterprise AI used for:

  • brainstorming
  • non-sensitive drafting
  • productivity support
  • generic summarisation

AMBER — Requires Governance & Human Review

AI used for:

  • customer communications
  • operational reporting
  • supplier discussions
  • internal business summaries

Requires:

  • approved tools
  • governance controls
  • human oversight.

RED — Higher Risk / Unapproved Usage

  • personal AI accounts used for confidential work
  • unapproved browser extensions
  • free AI tools processing sensitive information
  • unmanaged AI SaaS platforms
  • AI-generated outputs used without review

Tool Selection Decision Tree

LOW-RISK TASK?

Use:

  • approved enterprise AI
  • generic prompts
  • non-sensitive information.

SENSITIVE BUSINESS INFORMATION?

Use:

  • approved enterprise AI only
  • minimum necessary information
  • human oversight.

HIGHLY CONFIDENTIAL OR REGULATED DATA?

Do not use AI unless:

  • explicitly approved
  • operationally governed
  • authorised by the organisation.

Practical Workplace Rule

Before using any AI tool:

  • check whether it is approved
  • understand what information it processes
  • avoid using personal AI accounts for business work
  • apply human judgement at all times.

If unsure:

stop and escalate before using the tool.

Key Takeaways

  • Different AI tools create different levels of risk.
  • Convenience should not override governance.
  • Enterprise AI may reduce risk but still requires oversight.
  • Browser extensions and SaaS integrations may create hidden AI exposure.
  • Tool approval and governance are critical operational controls.
  • Human oversight remains essential.

Module Completion Outcome

You now understand:

  • how to choose safer AI tools
  • why governance matters when selecting AI platforms
  • how poor tool selection may create operational risk.
