The never-paste list.

By the end of this module you will
  • Understand which information should not be entered into unapproved AI systems
  • Recognise high-risk business data
  • Understand why even small snippets of information may create exposure
  • Apply safer decision-making before using AI tools

Most AI-related incidents begin with convenience.

Usually:

  • someone wanted to save time
  • improve wording
  • summarise information
  • or work more efficiently.

The issue is often not malicious intent. The issue is:

  • misunderstanding what information is sensitive
  • misunderstanding how AI tools process data
  • or using unapproved platforms without governance.

This module explains the categories of information that should not be entered into unapproved or unauthorised AI systems.

The Golden Rule

If information is:

  • confidential
  • commercially sensitive
  • regulated
  • personal
  • or security-related

do not assume it is safe to enter into AI tools. Especially:

  • consumer AI platforms
  • free AI accounts
  • browser extensions
  • or unapproved SaaS tools.

Only use approved enterprise AI platforms for permitted organisational data classifications.

1. Customer Personal Data

Examples:

  • names
  • addresses
  • phone numbers
  • email addresses
  • payroll details
  • identification numbers
  • medical information
  • customer tickets
  • CVs
  • account information

Why This Matters

This may create:

  • GDPR/privacy concerns
  • customer trust issues
  • regulatory exposure
  • governance risk.

Real-World Scenario

Scenario

"It was just a customer complaint."

A support employee pastes a customer complaint email into a public AI chatbot to:

"generate a more professional response."

The email contains:

  • names
  • account details
  • personal information.

The employee believes:

"It's only one email."

Risks Created

  • personal data exposure
  • customer trust concerns
  • governance failures
  • potential privacy breaches

Key Lesson

Even small amounts of personal data may create operational and regulatory exposure.

2. Confidential Business Information

Examples:

  • pricing models
  • commercial proposals
  • supplier negotiations
  • acquisition discussions
  • internal strategy
  • financial reporting
  • sales forecasts
  • commercial agreements

Why This Matters

Confidential business information may create:

  • competitive exposure
  • contractual risk
  • reputational harm
  • governance concerns.

Real-World Scenario

Scenario

"The AI improved the proposal."

A salesperson uploads a draft proposal containing:

  • customer pricing
  • commercial margins
  • supplier comparisons

into a free AI tool. The goal:

"make it sound more executive."

Risks Created

  • commercial confidentiality exposure
  • supplier governance concerns
  • reputational risk
  • operational governance failures

Key Lesson

Commercially sensitive information requires governance just as much as personal data.

3. Passwords & Security Information

Do not enter:

  • passwords
  • MFA codes
  • API keys
  • VPN details
  • certificates
  • firewall configurations
  • penetration test results
  • infrastructure diagrams
  • vulnerability reports

into unapproved AI systems.

Why This Matters

This information may directly increase:

  • cyber security risk
  • infrastructure exposure
  • operational vulnerability.

Real-World Scenario

Scenario

"The AI helped troubleshoot the firewall."

An IT administrator pastes:

  • firewall logs
  • internal IP ranges
  • VPN configuration details

into an AI chatbot to:

"help diagnose a network issue."

Risks Created

  • infrastructure exposure
  • attack surface disclosure
  • governance failures
  • operational cyber risk

Key Lesson

AI tools should not become uncontrolled support channels for sensitive infrastructure information.

4. HR & Employment Information

Examples:

  • disciplinary records
  • salaries
  • grievances
  • sickness records
  • redundancy plans
  • interview notes
  • performance reviews
  • recruitment assessments

Why This Matters

This information may involve:

  • privacy obligations
  • discrimination concerns
  • legal exposure
  • employee trust issues.

Real-World Scenario

Scenario

"The AI scored the candidates."

A hiring manager uploads:

  • CVs
  • interview notes
  • candidate rankings

into an AI tool to:

"speed up shortlisting."

The manager does not realise:

  • bias may be introduced
  • candidate data may be processed externally
  • governance approval has not taken place.

Risks Created

  • discrimination concerns
  • privacy exposure
  • governance failures
  • reputational harm

Key Lesson

AI-assisted recruitment activities may require governance, oversight and organisational approval.

5. Intellectual Property & Source Code

Examples:

  • source code
  • engineering drawings
  • product designs
  • proprietary methods
  • unreleased content
  • internal documentation
  • customer deliverables

Why This Matters

This may expose:

  • trade secrets
  • commercially valuable intellectual property
  • or sensitive technical information.

Real-World Scenario

Scenario

"The AI fixed the code."

A developer pastes proprietary source code into a public AI assistant to:

"debug a performance issue."

The code includes:

  • authentication logic
  • customer integrations
  • internal security controls.

Risks Created

  • intellectual property exposure
  • security concerns
  • supplier governance issues
  • contractual risk

Key Lesson

AI coding assistants require governance and approval just like any other enterprise technology platform.

The "Small Snippet" Problem

Many employees assume:

"It's only a small amount of information."

However:

  • fragments of information may still be sensitive
  • multiple prompts may create larger exposure
  • context may reveal more than expected.

Examples:

  • partial pricing
  • project references
  • screenshots
  • internal acronyms
  • snippets of contracts
  • customer references

Even incomplete information may still create risk.

Red / Amber / Green Exercise

GREEN — Generally Lower Risk

  • brainstorming ideas
  • rewriting public content
  • grammar improvements
  • generic learning support

AMBER — Requires Approved Enterprise AI & Oversight

  • internal discussions
  • supplier communications
  • operational summaries
  • customer-related content

Requires:

  • approved tools
  • governance controls
  • human review.

RED — Do Not Enter Into Unapproved AI Systems

  • customer personal data
  • passwords
  • source code
  • contracts
  • pricing
  • HR information
  • regulated data
  • security configurations

Practical Workplace Rule

Before entering information into AI, ask:

1.

Is this information confidential or commercially sensitive?

2.

Does it belong to:

  • a customer
  • employee
  • supplier
  • or partner?

3.

Is the AI platform approved for this type of information?

4.

Would the organisation expect this information to be processed externally? If unsure:

stop and escalate before proceeding.

Key Takeaways

  • Most AI-related risk comes from oversharing information.
  • Confidential information includes more than passwords and personal data.
  • Commercial information may be highly sensitive.
  • Small snippets of information may still create exposure.
  • Governance and human judgement remain essential.

Module Completion Outcome

You now understand:

  • what information should not be entered into unapproved AI systems
  • why sensitive data creates operational risk
  • how to make safer decisions before using AI tools.
