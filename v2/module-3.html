Module 03 of 11 · 8–10 min

The never-paste list.

Most AI incidents start with convenience, not malice. This module names the five categories of information that do not belong in unapproved AI tools.

By the end of this module you will
  • Understand which information should not be entered into unapproved AI systems
  • Recognise high-risk business data
  • Understand why even small snippets of information may create exposure
  • Apply safer decision-making before using AI tools

Most AI-related incidents begin with convenience

Someone wanted to save time, improve wording, summarise information or work more efficiently. The issue is rarely malicious intent. The issue is misunderstanding what information is sensitive, misunderstanding how AI tools process data, or using unapproved platforms without governance.

The golden rule

If information is confidential, commercially sensitive, regulated, personal or security-related, do not assume it is safe to enter into AI tools. Especially consumer AI platforms, free AI accounts, browser extensions or unapproved SaaS tools. Only use approved enterprise AI platforms for permitted organisational data classifications.

1. Customer personal data

Examples: names · addresses · phone numbers · email addresses · payroll details · identification numbers · medical information · customer tickets · CVs · account information.

Why this matters: may create GDPR or privacy concerns, customer trust issues, regulatory exposure, governance risk.

Scenario — "It was just a customer complaint"

A support employee pastes a customer complaint email into a public AI chatbot to "generate a more professional response." The email contains names, account details and personal information. The employee believes "it's only one email."

Key lesson

Even small amounts of personal data may create operational and regulatory exposure.

2. Confidential business information

Examples: pricing models · commercial proposals · supplier negotiations · acquisition discussions · internal strategy · financial reporting · sales forecasts · commercial agreements.

Why this matters: may create competitive exposure, contractual risk, reputational harm, governance concerns.

Scenario — "The AI improved the proposal"

A salesperson uploads a draft proposal containing customer pricing, commercial margins and supplier comparisons into a free AI tool to "make it sound more executive."

Key lesson

Commercially sensitive information requires governance just as much as personal data.

3. Passwords & security information

Do not enter: passwords · MFA codes · API keys · VPN details · certificates · firewall configurations · penetration test results · infrastructure diagrams · vulnerability reports.

Why this matters: may directly increase cyber security risk, infrastructure exposure, operational vulnerability.

Scenario — "The AI helped troubleshoot the firewall"

An IT administrator pastes firewall logs, internal IP ranges and VPN configuration details into an AI chatbot to "help diagnose a network issue."

Key lesson

AI tools should not become uncontrolled support channels for sensitive infrastructure information.

4. HR & employment information

Examples: disciplinary records · salaries · grievances · sickness records · redundancy plans · interview notes · performance reviews · recruitment assessments.

Why this matters: involves privacy obligations, discrimination concerns, legal exposure, employee trust issues.

Scenario — "The AI scored the candidates"

A hiring manager uploads CVs, interview notes and candidate rankings into an AI tool to "speed up shortlisting." The manager does not realise bias may be introduced, candidate data may be processed externally, and governance approval has not taken place.

Key lesson

AI-assisted recruitment activities may require governance, oversight and organisational approval.

5. Intellectual property & source code

Examples: source code · engineering drawings · product designs · proprietary methods · unreleased content · internal documentation · customer deliverables.

Why this matters: may expose trade secrets, commercially valuable IP or sensitive technical information.

Scenario — "The AI fixed the code"

A developer pastes proprietary source code into a public AI assistant to "debug a performance issue." The code includes authentication logic, customer integrations and internal security controls.

Key lesson

AI coding assistants require governance and approval just like any other enterprise technology platform.

The "small snippet" problem

Many employees assume "it's only a small amount of information." However: fragments of information may still be sensitive · multiple prompts may create larger exposure · context may reveal more than expected.

Examples that still create exposure: partial pricing · project references · screenshots · internal acronyms · snippets of contracts · customer references.

Practical workplace rule

Before entering information into AI, ask:

  1. Is this information confidential or commercially sensitive?
  2. Does it belong to a customer, employee, supplier or partner?
  3. Is the AI platform approved for this type of information?
  4. Would the organisation expect this information to be processed externally?

If unsure, stop and escalate before proceeding.

Key takeaways

  • Most AI-related risk comes from oversharing information
  • Confidential information includes more than passwords and personal data
  • Commercial information may be highly sensitive
  • Small snippets of information may still create exposure
  • Governance and human judgement remain essential
