Verifying what the AI tells you.

By the end of this module you will
  • Understand why AI systems may generate incorrect information
  • Recognise hallucinations, bias and false confidence
  • Apply practical verification techniques
  • Understand why human oversight remains essential

AI can sound convincing while still being incorrect.

This is one of the biggest operational risks associated with generative AI. AI systems are designed to:

  • generate human-like responses
  • predict likely language patterns
  • produce helpful outputs quickly.

They are not:

  • guaranteed sources of truth
  • legal authorities
  • compliance specialists
  • or replacements for professional judgement.

Generative AI systems generate outputs based on learned patterns and probabilities rather than verified factual reasoning. As a result, AI may:

  • fabricate information
  • generate inaccurate content
  • misunderstand context
  • present outdated information
  • or produce biased outputs.

This is commonly referred to as:

hallucination.

The Core Principle

If the output matters,

it must be verified by a human. Especially where outputs affect:

  • customers
  • finance
  • legal matters
  • compliance
  • HR
  • operational decisions
  • or reputational risk.

What Is A Hallucination?

A hallucination occurs when an AI system generates:

  • fabricated
  • inaccurate
  • or misleading information

while presenting it confidently. Examples include:

  • invented references
  • incorrect statistics
  • fabricated case law
  • or inaccurate policy guidance.

The risk is that:

the response may still appear highly professional and believable.

Real-World Scenario 1

Scenario

"The cases didn't exist."

A lawyer uses AI to help prepare legal arguments. The AI generates:

  • legal case references
  • quotations
  • supporting explanations.

The output:

  • appears authoritative
  • sounds professional
  • includes detailed citations.

However: the legal cases were fabricated by the AI system.

Risks Created

  • reputational damage
  • operational embarrassment
  • legal exposure
  • professional conduct concerns

Key Lesson

AI-generated confidence is not proof of accuracy.

Real-World Scenario 2

Scenario

"The financial summary was wrong."

A manager uses AI to summarise financial reporting for a leadership meeting. The AI:

  • misinterprets spreadsheet context
  • swaps percentages
  • generates inaccurate conclusions.

The manager trusts the output without verification.

Risks Created

  • inaccurate reporting
  • poor decision making
  • governance concerns
  • operational disruption

Key Lesson

AI-generated summaries should support human review, not replace it.

Real-World Scenario 3

Scenario

"The AI made up the policy."

An employee asks AI:

"What does our company policy say about customer refunds?"

The AI generates a convincing response using:

  • formal language
  • realistic wording
  • fabricated policy details.

The employee acts on the incorrect guidance.

Risks Created

  • misinformation
  • customer disputes
  • inconsistent decision making
  • governance failures

Key Lesson

AI should not replace:

  • official documentation
  • approved policies
  • or authoritative organisational guidance.

Why AI Gets Things Wrong

Generative AI systems:

  • predict patterns
  • not verified truth.

Outputs are influenced by:

  • training data
  • prompt quality
  • context
  • probability-based language generation.

This means AI may:

  • misunderstand prompts
  • combine unrelated information
  • oversimplify issues
  • or fill gaps with inaccurate detail.

Common AI Failure Types

1. Fabricated Information ("Hallucinations")

Examples:

  • fake references
  • invented statistics
  • fabricated case law
  • inaccurate policies

2. Outdated Information

AI may:

  • rely on historical training data
  • misunderstand recent changes
  • or provide outdated guidance.

3. False Confidence

AI often presents uncertain information using:

  • authoritative tone
  • professional structure
  • convincing wording.

This can make inaccuracies harder to identify.

4. Bias & Context Errors

Outputs may reflect:

  • incomplete context
  • training bias
  • flawed assumptions
  • or missing information.

5. Oversimplification

AI may remove:

  • nuance
  • exceptions
  • or important operational context.

This is particularly risky in:

  • legal
  • compliance
  • HR
  • financial environments.

Verification Rules

Rule 1 — Verify Important Outputs

The greater the business impact the greater the verification requirement. Always verify:

  • customer communications
  • legal content
  • financial summaries
  • policy wording
  • compliance advice
  • HR-related outputs

Rule 2 — Check Trusted Sources

Where possible:

  • verify against official documentation
  • organisational policy
  • approved systems
  • or authoritative sources.

Rule 3 — Challenge The Output

Do not assume:

"the AI must be correct."

Ask:

  • Does this make sense?
  • Is anything missing?
  • Does this match official guidance?
  • Would I trust this without AI involvement?

Rule 4 — Understand AI's Role

AI may support:

  • drafting
  • summarisation
  • brainstorming
  • productivity.

Humans remain accountable for:

  • decisions
  • approvals
  • compliance
  • operational outcomes.

Verification Workflow

Before Using AI Output

1.

Check:

  • factual accuracy
  • names
  • dates
  • references
  • calculations
  • links

2.

Validate:

  • business context
  • organisational policy
  • legal/compliance implications

3.

Apply:

  • human judgement
  • operational oversight
  • professional review

4.

Escalate if:

  • unsure
  • customer-impacting
  • high-risk
  • or governance-sensitive.

Red / Amber / Green Exercise

GREEN — Lower Risk Usage

  • brainstorming
  • grammar improvements
  • generic drafting
  • idea generation

Still requires review.

AMBER — Requires Careful Verification

  • customer communications
  • operational summaries
  • policy drafts
  • reporting outputs

Requires:

  • source validation
  • human oversight
  • organisational review.

RED — Do Not Rely On Without Expert Validation

  • legal advice
  • regulatory interpretation
  • disciplinary decisions
  • financial reporting
  • medical guidance

AI may assist but qualified humans remain responsible.

Practical Exercise

Scenario

An AI system generates the following statement:

"Under GDPR, businesses can freely use customer data in AI systems if the information is anonymised afterwards."

Learner Question

What should you do next?

  • Trust the answer immediately
  • Verify against official GDPR guidance
  • Escalate to compliance if unsure
  • Check organisational policy

Correct Answer

Everything except: "Trust the answer immediately."

Practical Workplace Rule

Treat AI outputs as:

draft assistance,

not:

final authority.

Always apply:

  • verification
  • oversight
  • professional judgement.

Key Takeaways

  • AI systems may generate convincing but inaccurate information.
  • Hallucinations are a genuine operational risk.
  • Human oversight remains essential.
  • Important outputs should always be verified.
  • AI should support judgement, not replace it.
  • Accountability remains with the human user and organisation.

You now understand:

  • why AI systems may generate inaccurate information
  • how to identify hallucinations and misinformation
  • how to safely verify AI-generated outputs.
