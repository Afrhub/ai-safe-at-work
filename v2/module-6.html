Module 06 of 11 · 10–12 minutes

AI-powered scams aimed at you.

By the end of this module you will
  • Understand how AI is changing cyber crime and social engineering
  • Recognise AI-enhanced phishing, impersonation and fraud
  • Identify common manipulation techniques
  • Apply safer behaviours when handling suspicious communications

AI is not only helping businesses.

It is also being used by cyber criminals and fraudsters. Attackers increasingly use AI to:

  • write convincing phishing emails
  • imitate writing styles
  • generate fake documents
  • automate scams
  • create realistic impersonation attempts
  • support social engineering attacks at scale.

This means scams are becoming:

  • more believable
  • more personalised
  • more difficult to identify using traditional warning signs alone.

The greatest risk is often not:

"obviously fake emails."

The risk is:

realistic, convincing and highly targeted deception.

The New Reality

Historically, phishing messages often contained:

  • spelling mistakes
  • poor grammar
  • awkward language
  • or obvious warning signs.

AI now allows attackers to generate:

  • professional language
  • realistic tone
  • contextual messaging
  • targeted communications quickly and cheaply.

This significantly lowers the barrier for cyber crime.

Real-World Scenario 1

Scenario

"The CEO asked urgently."

A finance employee receives a Teams message appearing to come from a senior executive. The message says:

"Need this payment processed urgently before the meeting. I'm travelling and can't call."

The wording:

  • sounds natural
  • references real projects
  • matches the executive's communication style.

The employee later discovers:

  • the account was compromised
  • the message formed part of a social engineering attack.

AI-assisted techniques may have been used to:

  • improve wording
  • imitate communication style
  • or personalise the request.

Risks Created

  • financial fraud
  • business email compromise
  • operational disruption
  • reputational damage

Key Lesson

Urgency and familiarity should not replace verification and process.

Real-World Scenario 2

Scenario

"The voice sounded real."

An employee receives a phone call from someone sounding very similar to their manager. The caller requests:

  • password resets
  • MFA approvals
  • urgent access changes.

The employee complies because:

"it sounded like them."

The organisation later suspects the call may have involved AI-generated voice impersonation.

Risks Created

  • credential compromise
  • account takeover
  • operational security failures
  • major incident escalation

Key Lesson

Voice and video should no longer be treated as standalone proof of identity. Verification processes remain essential.

Real-World Scenario 3

Scenario

"The phishing email looked perfect."

A staff member receives an email appearing to come from a supplier. The message:

  • contains accurate branding
  • references real projects
  • uses highly professional language.

The employee clicks the link because:

"it looked legitimate."

The site captures login credentials.

Risks Created

  • account compromise
  • supplier impersonation
  • operational disruption
  • potential ransomware exposure

Key Lesson

Professional language and branding do not guarantee legitimacy.

Common AI-Enhanced Threats

1. AI-Generated Phishing Emails

AI can help attackers:

  • personalise emails
  • imitate writing styles
  • improve language quality
  • generate convincing communications rapidly.

2. Voice Impersonation

Attackers may use AI-assisted tooling to generate realistic voice impersonations using:

  • publicly available audio
  • social media clips
  • meeting recordings
  • or voicemail samples.

3. Deepfake Video

AI-generated video may be used to:

  • impersonate executives
  • support fraud attempts
  • create fake meetings
  • or manipulate trust.

4. Fake Documents & Content

AI can generate convincing but inaccurate or fabricated:

  • invoices
  • contracts
  • reports
  • policies
  • customer communications

Often using professional formatting and language.

5. AI-Assisted Social Engineering

Attackers increasingly use AI to:

  • research organisations
  • profile employees
  • craft believable messages
  • automate deception at scale.

Why Humans Are Still The Target

Even with advanced technology most attacks still succeed because:

  • someone trusts the request
  • bypasses process
  • or acts under pressure.

AI increases realism. Human judgement and operational controls remain critical defences.

Warning Signs Still Matter

Even AI-enhanced scams often rely on:

  • urgency
  • emotional pressure
  • secrecy
  • authority
  • or requests to bypass process.

Examples:

  • "Do this urgently."
  • "Don't tell anyone yet."
  • "I need this immediately."
  • "You're the only person available."

These should always trigger caution.

Verification Rules

Rule 1 — Slow Down

Urgency is a common manipulation tactic. Pause before:

  • clicking
  • approving
  • paying
  • or sharing information.

Rule 2 — Verify Through Another Channel

If unsure:

  • call the individual directly
  • message them separately
  • or use approved contact methods.

Do not rely solely on:

  • incoming email
  • voice
  • or video.

Rule 3 — Follow Process

Security and financial controls exist for a reason. Do not bypass:

  • approval workflows
  • MFA controls
  • payment verification
  • or identity checks

because a request appears urgent or senior.

Rule 4 — Escalate Suspicious Activity

If something appears unusual:

  • stop
  • verify
  • escalate.

It is always safer to check.

Red / Amber / Green Exercise

GREEN — Safe Behaviour

  • independently verifying requests
  • following approval processes
  • reporting suspicious communications
  • slowing down under pressure

AMBER — Requires Additional Verification

  • urgent executive requests
  • supplier payment changes
  • unexpected MFA prompts
  • unusual file-sharing requests

Requires:

  • verification
  • oversight
  • escalation if unsure.

RED — High-Risk Behaviour

  • sharing passwords
  • approving MFA requests without verification
  • bypassing controls due to urgency
  • trusting voice/video automatically
  • processing payments without following process

Practical Exercise

Scenario

You receive:

  • a Teams message appearing to come from a senior executive
  • requesting urgent payment approval
  • while travelling overseas.

The message:

  • sounds realistic
  • references genuine projects
  • pressures immediate action.

What should you do?

  • approve immediately because the request appears urgent
  • verify using another approved communication method
  • follow payment approval procedures
  • escalate if the request appears suspicious

Correct Answer

Everything except: "approve immediately because the request appears urgent."

Practical Workplace Rule

Treat:

  • urgent requests
  • unusual behaviour
  • payment changes
  • MFA prompts
  • identity-based requests

with caution. AI-assisted attacks increase realism. Verification protects the organisation.

Key Takeaways

  • AI is making scams more convincing and scalable.
  • Professional language does not prove legitimacy.
  • Voice and video impersonation risks are increasing.
  • Human judgement and process remain critical controls.
  • Verification and escalation are essential operational behaviours.
  • Slowing down reduces risk.

You now understand:

  • how AI is changing phishing and social engineering
  • how attackers may use AI-assisted impersonation techniques
