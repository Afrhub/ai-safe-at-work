AI-powered scams aimed at you.

Attackers use AI to write better phishing, clone voices and impersonate at scale. Traditional warning signs no longer save you.

By the end of this module you will
  • Understand how AI is changing cyber crime and social engineering
  • Recognise AI-enhanced phishing, impersonation and fraud
  • Identify common manipulation techniques
  • Apply safer behaviours when handling suspicious communications

AI is not only helping businesses

Attackers use AI to write convincing phishing emails, imitate writing styles, generate fake documents, automate scams, create realistic impersonation attempts and support social engineering attacks at scale. Scams are becoming more believable, more personalised and more difficult to identify using traditional warning signs alone.

The new reality

Historically, phishing messages often contained spelling mistakes, poor grammar, awkward language or obvious warning signs. AI now allows attackers to generate professional language, realistic tone, contextual messaging and targeted communications quickly and cheaply. This significantly lowers the barrier for cyber crime.

Real-world scenario 1 — "The CEO asked urgently"

Scenario

A finance employee receives a Teams message appearing to come from a senior executive. The message says "need this payment processed urgently before the meeting. I'm travelling and can't call." The wording sounds natural, references real projects, and matches the executive's communication style. The account was compromised; the message formed part of a social engineering attack. AI-assisted techniques may have been used to improve wording, imitate communication style or personalise the request.

Key lesson

Urgency and familiarity should not replace verification and process.

Real-world scenario 2 — "The voice sounded real"

Scenario

An employee receives a phone call from someone sounding very similar to their manager. The caller requests password resets, MFA approvals and urgent access changes. The employee complies because "it sounded like them." The organisation later suspects the call may have involved AI-generated voice impersonation.

Key lesson

Voice and video should no longer be treated as standalone proof of identity. Verification processes remain essential.

Real-world scenario 3 — "The phishing email looked perfect"

Scenario

A staff member receives an email appearing to come from a supplier. The message contains accurate branding, references real projects and uses highly professional language. The employee clicks the link because "it looked legitimate." The site captures login credentials.

Key lesson

Professional language and branding do not guarantee legitimacy.

Common AI-enhanced threats

1. AI-generated phishing emails

Personalise emails · imitate writing styles · improve language quality · generate convincing communications rapidly.

2. Voice impersonation

Attackers use AI-assisted tooling to generate realistic voice impersonations using publicly available audio, social media clips, meeting recordings or voicemail samples.

3. Deepfake video

AI-generated video may be used to impersonate executives, support fraud attempts, create fake meetings or manipulate trust.

4. Fake documents & content

Convincing but inaccurate or fabricated invoices, contracts, reports, policies, customer communications. Often using professional formatting and language.

5. AI-assisted social engineering

Attackers use AI to research organisations, profile employees, craft believable messages and automate deception at scale.

Why humans are still the target

Most attacks still succeed because someone trusts the request, bypasses process or acts under pressure. AI increases realism. Human judgement and operational controls remain critical defences.

Warning signs still matter

Even AI-enhanced scams often rely on urgency, emotional pressure, secrecy, authority or requests to bypass process. Phrases to treat with caution:

  • "Do this urgently."
  • "Don't tell anyone yet."
  • "I need this immediately."
  • "You're the only person available."

The four verification rules

Rule 1 — Slow down

Urgency is a common manipulation tactic. Pause before clicking, approving, paying or sharing information.

Rule 2 — Verify through another channel

If unsure, call the individual directly, message them separately or use approved contact methods. Do not rely solely on incoming email, voice or video.

Rule 3 — Follow process

Security and financial controls exist for a reason. Do not bypass approval workflows, MFA controls, payment verification or identity checks because a request appears urgent or senior.

Rule 4 — Escalate suspicious activity

If something appears unusual, stop, verify and escalate. It is always safer to check.

Red / Amber / Green exercise

Green · safe behaviour

  • independently verifying requests
  • following approval processes
  • reporting suspicious communications
  • slowing down under pressure

Amber · extra verification

  • urgent executive requests
  • supplier payment changes
  • unexpected MFA prompts
  • unusual file-sharing requests

Red · high-risk behaviour

  • sharing passwords
  • approving MFA requests without verification
  • bypassing controls due to urgency
  • trusting voice or video automatically
  • processing payments without following process

Practical workplace rule

Treat urgent requests, unusual behaviour, payment changes, MFA prompts and identity-based requests with caution. AI-assisted attacks increase realism. Verification protects the organisation.

Key takeaways

  • AI is making scams more convincing and scalable
  • Professional language does not prove legitimacy
  • Voice and video impersonation risks are increasing
  • Human judgement and process remain critical controls
  • Verification and escalation are essential operational behaviours
  • Slowing down reduces risk
