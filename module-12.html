The standards behind this course.

Four sources of rules sit behind this whole course. They overlap and approach similar risks from different perspectives. This page says what each one means, why it exists, and what it asks of you on a normal Tuesday.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Know the difference between a regulation (you must), a standard (you can choose to demonstrate you meet it), and a framework
  • Understand who each of GDPR, the EU AI Act, ISO 27001, and ISO 42001 actually applies to
  • Be able to answer "we follow X" without panicking
i
Regulation vs. standard

GDPR and the EU AI Act are regulations: laws. If they apply to you, you must comply. Non-compliance can bring regulatory penalties and enforcement actions, including significant fines. ISO 27001 and ISO 42001 are standards: voluntary frameworks. Following them is good practice and increasingly demanded by customers, insurers, and procurement teams, but there's no fine for not following them. (You can be certified against them, which is a separate exercise.)

Regulation · In force since 2018

GDPR

General Data Protection Regulation (EU) 2016/679 — and the UK GDPR, which is the post-Brexit British equivalent. The defining European law on processing personal data.

Applies to
Any organisation processing the personal data of people in the EU or UK — regardless of where the organisation is. A US software vendor with EU customers is covered. So is a one-person business in Lyon with an email list.
Maximum fine
€20 million or 4% of annual global turnover, whichever is higher.
Enforced by
National supervisory authorities — ICO (UK), CNIL (France), Datatilsynet (Denmark), Garante (Italy), etc.

The articles that come up most in AI use

  • Article 5 — principles: lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, data minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity and confidentiality, accountability. Most "is this OK?" questions in AI use can be answered by checking these seven principles.
  • Article 6 — lawful basis for processing. You need one of six: consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests. "It seemed useful" is not on the list.
  • Article 9 — special-category data (health, ethnicity, religion, etc.) is forbidden to process except in nine specific circumstances. Pasting it into ChatGPT isn't one.
  • Article 22 — rights relating to certain automated decision-making, alongside transparency obligations. It concerns decisions made solely by automated means that produce legal or similarly significant effects — not all AI-assisted decisions. Module 9.
  • Articles 28 and 44–50 — when you give personal data to a third party, there must be a contract; transfers outside the EU/EEA need extra safeguards. Module 2.
  • Article 32 — security of processing. You must implement appropriate technical and organisational measures.
  • Articles 33–34 — breach notification: 72 hours to the supervisory authority, and to data subjects where the risk is high. Module 10.

What it asks of you on a Tuesday

Don't paste personal data into tools you don't have a contract with. Have a lawful basis for what you do with personal data. Know who to tell, and how fast, if something goes wrong.

Regulation · Phased application 2025–2027

EU AI Act

Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 — the first comprehensive AI law in any major jurisdiction. Adopted 2024, in force August 2024, with obligations phased in over three years.

Applies to
Providers placing AI systems on the EU market; deployers (anyone using AI in a professional capacity inside the EU); importers and distributors. Most SMBs are deployers.
Maximum fine
€35 million or 7% of annual global turnover, whichever is higher (for the most serious breaches — prohibited AI practices).
Enforced by
National AI authorities (member states had until August 2025 to designate them) plus the EU AI Office.

The four-tier risk framework

  • Unacceptable risk — banned outright. Social scoring of citizens, real-time biometric surveillance of public spaces (with narrow exceptions), emotion recognition in workplaces and schools, dark-pattern manipulation that causes harm. These have been illegal since February 2025.
  • High risk — heavy obligations on both providers and deployers. Listed in Annex III: employment, education, essential services, law enforcement, migration, justice, biometric identification, and certain critical infrastructure. This is the category most likely to touch an SMB — particularly if you use AI in hiring.
  • Limited risk — transparency obligations only. Chatbots must tell users they're chatting with an AI. Synthetic content such as deepfakes may need to be labelled. Certain marketing, chatbot and synthetic-content use cases may attract transparency obligations.
  • Minimal risk — most AI use. No specific obligations beyond general law. But voluntary codes of conduct are encouraged.

Articles that come up most in this course

  • Article 4 — AI literacy: providers and deployers must ensure their staff have an appropriate level of AI literacy. This course is, in part, that literacy.
  • Article 12 — high-risk systems must keep logs.
  • Article 14 — human oversight: the human in the loop has to actually oversee.
  • Article 26 — deployer obligations: use the AI according to instructions, monitor it, inform staff, keep logs, inform affected persons.
  • Article 50 — transparency obligations for certain AI systems and synthetic content: for example, chatbots identifying themselves and the labelling of some AI-generated or manipulated content. The precise requirements are more nuanced and continue to evolve through implementation guidance.
  • Article 73 — providers must report serious incidents.

What it asks of you on a Tuesday

Know which tier your AI use falls into. If it's high-risk, expect proper procurement, contracts, instructions, logging, and human oversight — not a free consumer tool. If it's limited-risk, label the AI's involvement. If you're not sure, ask before you ship.

Standard · Voluntary · 2022 revision

ISO/IEC 27001:2022

The international standard for information security management systems (ISMS). The most widely-adopted security standard in the world. If your business has ever been asked "are you ISO 27001 certified?", this is what they meant.

Applies to
Voluntary — but increasingly expected by enterprise customers, regulators, and insurers. If your business sells to large customers, you'll be asked.
Certification
An accredited certification body audits your business; if it passes, you get a certificate valid for three years (with annual surveillance audits).
Structure
Main clauses (4–10) describe the management system — leadership, planning, risk assessment, operation, evaluation, improvement. Annex A lists 93 specific controls grouped into four themes: organisational, people, physical, technological.

Annex A controls that come up most in AI use

  • A.5.10 — acceptable use of information and assets. Your AI policy lives under this.
  • A.5.13 — labelling of information. The "confidential" stamp means something.
  • A.5.23 — information security for use of cloud services. AI vendors are cloud services.
  • A.5.34 — privacy and protection of PII. Where ISO 27001 meets GDPR.
  • A.6.3 — information security awareness, education and training. This course meets a lot of the spirit of this control.
  • A.8.5 — secure authentication, including MFA.
  • A.8.12 — data leakage prevention. Module 3 in one control.
  • A.8.15 — logging.

What it asks of you on a Tuesday

Treat AI tools as cloud services like any other — with the same expectations on contracts, controls, and acceptable use. If your business is certified or pursuing certification, your AI use is in scope.

Standard · Voluntary · Published December 2023

ISO/IEC 42001:2023

The world's first international standard for AI management systems. Published December 2023. Designed to do for AI governance what ISO 27001 did for information security — give organisations a framework, and an opportunity to be certified against it.

Applies to
Voluntary. Any organisation that provides or uses AI systems, regardless of size or sector.
Why it exists
Because regulations (like the EU AI Act) tell you what outcomes to achieve, but not how to organise yourself to achieve them. ISO 42001 fills that gap with a management-system structure.
Relationship to ISO 27001
Deliberately structured to integrate with ISO 27001 and other "management system standards". Many organisations will pursue both together.

What an AI management system covers

  • Clause 4 — context: understand the organisation's AI use, the interested parties, the scope of the management system.
  • Clause 5 — leadership: top management commits, defines the AI policy, assigns roles.
  • Clause 6 — planning: identify AI risks and opportunities, set objectives, plan changes.
  • Clause 7 — support: resources, competence, awareness, communication, documented information.
  • Clause 8 — operation: actually run the AI system lifecycle, including risk treatment.
  • Clause 9 — performance evaluation: monitor, measure, internal audit, management review.
  • Clause 10 — improvement: continual improvement, dealing with non-conformities.

Annex A — the AI-specific controls

Annex A of ISO 42001 includes AI-specific controls covering areas such as:

  • Policies for AI use
  • Internal organisation and roles
  • Resources for AI systems
  • Impact assessments for AI systems on individuals and society
  • AI system lifecycle (design, development, verification, deployment, operation)
  • Data for AI systems (data quality, data management)
  • Information for stakeholders of AI systems
  • Use of AI systems (operation, monitoring, incident response)
  • Third-party AI relationships
  • Customer involvement

What it asks of you on a Tuesday

For an individual user, ISO 42001 mostly shows up indirectly — through the policies, training, and tools your business adopts because of it. Your role is to follow the policies (Module 4), keep the logs (Module 9), and report incidents (Module 10). For a business owner: 42001 is the first credible framework for saying "we govern our AI use" — increasingly being asked about by customers, partners, and insurers.

How they fit together

The four standards aren't competing. They cover different surfaces of the same problem:

GDPR

What you can and can't do with personal data. The data layer.

EU AI Act

What you can and can't do with AI systems, by risk tier. The use-case layer.

ISO 27001

How to organise yourself to protect information generally. The security management layer.

ISO 42001

How to organise yourself to govern AI specifically. The AI management layer.

A small business using AI for customer-facing work might find itself touching all four: GDPR because customer data is involved, the EU AI Act because the use is in a regulated category, ISO 27001 because their enterprise customers ask, and ISO 42001 because their insurer or board has asked them to demonstrate AI governance.

i
Governance insight: one framework, four surfaces

The standards are not separate compliance exercises. Together they provide a framework for managing data, security, risk, accountability and AI governance. Treating them as one connected system — rather than four boxes to tick — is what turns compliance effort into something the business actually benefits from.

Other frameworks worth knowing exist

This course covers the four that come up most for European SMBs. Three more deserve a name-check:

  • NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) — the US National Institute of Standards and Technology's voluntary framework. Different style from ISO 42001 (less management-system, more risk function), but broadly compatible. Heavily used in the US.
  • SOC 2 — an audit framework from the American Institute of CPAs, covering security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy. Often demanded by US enterprise customers in parallel with ISO 27001.
  • Sector-specific rules — DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act) for financial services in the EU, NIS2 for essential services, HIPAA for healthcare in the US. If your business is in a regulated sector, sector rules will sit on top of everything in this course.
  • Cyber Essentials / Cyber Essentials Plus and NCSC guidance — UK government-backed schemes and National Cyber Security Centre advice that many UK SMEs already recognise. Often a more familiar starting point than ISO 42001, and a sensible baseline that complements the AI-specific governance above.

One final perspective

The standards exist because the alternative is worse. Without them, each business invents its own approach, nobody can name what good looks like, and customers, regulators and insurers cannot tell the difference between a business that takes this seriously and one that does not.

You do not need to learn the standards in detail. You need to know they exist, know what they expect at a high level, and follow the practices in modules 1–11 that satisfy most of them in the background. If your business pursues certification one day, your habits will already align. You will be the person who turns out to have been doing it right.
