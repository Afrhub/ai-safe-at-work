Relevant Role · DPO / compliance lead

11 min read

AI through the compliance lens.

For the person paid to know what could go wrong, who needs to be told, and within how many hours, before everyone else does. This module turns the standards from background reading into a weekly calendar.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Know when AI use triggers a DPIA, a FRIA, or both, and how to scope each in under a day
  • Know what an AI entry in the Records of Processing Activities should actually contain
  • Run the 72-hour breach-notification clock with confidence, including the awkward "are we even sure it's a breach?" hours
  • Know which conversations to have with procurement, IT, and the board before a tool is bought
  • Have a defensible answer when a data subject asks "how was this decision about me made?"

What's actually changed for you

The substantive shift since 2024 is small and large at once. GDPR was not rewritten. ISO 27001 was not rewritten. The EU AI Act layered on top of both. The change for you: every existing obligation now has an AI-specific edge case, and the business expects you to recognise it before it commits.

Three buckets of work, each cross-cutting:

  • Before, assessments (DPIA, FRIA, vendor diligence), policies (AUP, RoPA entries).
  • During, oversight of staff use (Module 9 logging), training (this course is one such measure under Art 4), incident readiness.
  • After, incident response, breach notification, regulator engagement, lessons captured into the next cycle.

DPIA, FRIA, or both?

The first conversation a business should have with you about a proposed AI use case: do we need a Data Protection Impact Assessment under GDPR Article 35, a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment under EU AI Act Article 27, or both?

DPIA (GDPR Art 35)

Trigger: processing is likely to result in high risk to rights and freedoms of natural persons. AI use almost always meets this where personal data is involved.

Output: documented assessment, mitigations, prior consultation if residual risk remains.

FRIA (EU AI Act Art 27)

Trigger: deployer of a high-risk AI system that is a body governed by public law, a private body providing public services, or one using high-risk AI for credit-worthiness / life and health insurance pricing.

Output: documented assessment of fundamental-rights impact, complementing the DPIA.

In practice, for most EU SMBs that deploy AI in HR, customer-facing decisions, or credit/insurance: both. Run them together, output a combined document, keep two cross-referenced sections. Saves repetition, saves audit time.

The scoping conversation

Before either assessment starts, six questions extracted from a fifteen-minute conversation with the proposing business unit will determine the shape of the work:

  1. What problem is this AI solving? Not the feature, the underlying decision being made.
  2. Whose data, whose decisions? Categories of data subjects, categories of decisions affecting them.
  3. What's the volume? Pilot, departmental rollout, organisation-wide.
  4. What does refusal look like? Can a person opt out? Can they ask for human review?
  5. Where does the AI sit? SaaS, on-premise, in your existing cloud, on the developer's laptop.
  6. What's the worst-case incident? If this misbehaves in front of a regulator, what's the harm to a real person?

Those six answers shape the DPIA's depth and tell you whether to escalate to a FRIA, to prior consultation with the supervisory authority (Art 36), or to refuse the use case altogether.

Records of Processing Activities, the AI entry

For each AI use case, your RoPA (Art 30) entry needs five things many entries skip:

The model behind the product

Not "ChatGPT", "OpenAI GPT-4o via ChatGPT Enterprise tenant ABC-123, configured EU region, training opt-out on." Sub-processors disclosed.

The data flow

What categories go in, what comes out, what is retained where, for how long. Include any vector store, prompt cache, conversation history.

Legal basis

Specific Art 6 basis, plus Art 9 ground if special-category. "Legitimate interests" requires a documented balancing test, link to it.

Decision boundary

Where the AI's output ends and the human decision begins. Names of the humans, their authority to override.

Incident contact

Who at the vendor you call within thirty minutes if something goes wrong. SLA on notification. Vendor's own incident page URL.

Review cadence

Quarterly check, has the vendor changed terms, changed sub-processors, changed model behaviour, changed retention? Default cadence quarterly; finance / health monthly.

The 72-hour breach clock

Article 33 starts the clock when the controller has reasonable awareness, not certainty, that a personal-data breach has occurred. For AI-related incidents (the most common: data pasted into a free-tier tool, vendor security incident, model output disclosing data it shouldn't), the clock is the hardest part of the job.

Three principles get most teams through:

  1. The clock starts at "reasonable awareness", not "certainty". If your gut says this could be a breach, the clock is running. Use the hours to find out, not to wait.
  2. If unsure at 60 hours, notify. A precautionary notification you later refine or withdraw is much smaller cost than a missed deadline. Supervisory authorities recognise the distinction.
  3. Notify in writing, even when the regulator accepts a phone call. Audit trail. The phone call confirms it; the written notification is the record.
!
The most common AI breach scenarios
  • An employee pasted a customer file into a free-tier AI tool. The tool's terms permit training. The data is now in the vendor's pipeline.
  • An AI vendor announced a security incident. Your team had been sending sensitive data through that vendor for six months. You don't know yet what was exposed.
  • An AI chatbot deployed on your site disclosed another customer's order details to a third party who asked the right question.
  • An AI-generated decision was based on the wrong data category, and a customer complains. Investigation reveals the model had been hallucinating personal data the customer had never shared.

Each of these is a personal data breach. The clock starts when you have enough information to suspect it.

The conversation with procurement before a tool is bought

Most AI incidents at an SMB start with a tool that procurement bought before the DPO got involved. The cheapest insurance is to be the first call. Five questions to send back when a new AI tool is proposed:

  1. What categories of personal data will flow through it, in what volume?
  2. Where is the data processed (which country, whose servers, with what sub-processors)?
  3. What does the vendor's DPA actually say about training, retention, and breach notification?
  4. What's the lawful basis for this processing, and have we documented it?
  5. What happens if we need to extract our data and exit?

Three of the five tend to come back blank. Those blanks are the diligence finding. Use the vendor questionnaire template to structure the answers.

The data-subject request you'll get sooner than you think

Article 15 (right of access) plus Article 22 (automated decisions) plus Article 13–14 (information about automated decision-making) combine into a single emerging pattern: a customer asks "how was the decision about me made, and what role did AI play?"

The defensible response has three parts:

  1. The factual sequence: AI tool X produced output Y; human Z reviewed it; the decision was made on the basis of A, B, and C.
  2. The mitigation: human Z had the authority to override; here's evidence of the review.
  3. The right: the data subject can ask for re-review without the AI's involvement; here's how.

If you cannot produce all three from your records, fix the process. The response itself is fine.

The DPO's first 90 days on AI governance

Week 1, inventory

Across the org: which teams are using which AI tools, at which tiers, on which data. Walk floor. Five-minute conversations. Spreadsheet.

Week 2, gap

Match the inventory against existing DPAs, RoPA entries, AUP. Identify the shadow tools, the missing assessments, the unclear data flows.

Week 3, triage

Highest-risk uses first. Decide which to formalise (negotiate DPA, run DPIA), which to migrate to a procured alternative, which to stop.

Week 4, policy

Draft or update AUP for AI. Use the AUP template. Circulate, get sign-off, communicate.

Weeks 5–8, DPIAs

Run DPIA + FRIA on top three use cases. Document, mitigate, where needed escalate to prior consultation.

Weeks 9–12, train + log

Roll out AI literacy training (this course). Establish AI-use logging cadence (Module 9). Calendar the quarterly review.

Where the standards land

EU AI Act Art 4 (literacy), Art 14 (oversight, your spec), Art 26 (deployer obligations), Art 27 (FRIA), Art 73 (serious incidents). ISO/IEC 42001 Cl 6.1 (risks & opportunities), A.5.2 (AI impact assessment), A.7 (data), A.9 (use of AI). ISO/IEC 27001 A.5.34 (PII protection), A.5.23 (cloud use), A.5.24–26 (incidents).
