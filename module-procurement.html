Buying AI without inheriting the risk.

Procurement decides what the rest of the organisation lives with for years. AI vendor selection done badly creates risks no AUP can fix downstream. This track gives the procurement persona the language, the framework, and the contractual levers to buy AI tools the organisation can defend.

  • Know why AI procurement differs from traditional SaaS procurement, and which traditional templates fail
  • Use the 37-question vendor questionnaire as a buying instrument, not a tick-box form
  • Review an AI vendor's DPA against four clauses that determine downstream risk
  • Demand and verify sub-processor disclosures, including the AI provider's AI providers
  • Lean on EU AI Act Article 13 provider obligations at buying time
  • Understand the Article 26 obligations you take on the moment you sign
  • Map AI vendors to your sector framework (DORA, NYDFS, NHS DCB0129, etc.)

  • Apply the buy-vs-build-vs-defer decision tree

Sub-module 1 of 10

Why AI procurement is different

Traditional SaaS procurement templates were written for tools that did one thing in one place to one set of data. AI vendors don't fit. The model changes; the sub-processors change; the training data is opaque; the output is non-deterministic; the regulatory classification can shift mid-contract. Procurement frameworks that assume static behaviour generate static answers to a dynamic question.

Six properties that break traditional templates

  1. Model swap, your CRM doesn't change its database engine quarterly. AI vendors update underlying models with no contractual notice in most agreements.
  2. Sub-processor chain, your CRM uses AWS. Your AI CRM-add-on uses AWS + OpenAI + a vector DB + an embedding provider + a logging tool. The chain isn't transparent unless you require it.
  3. Training data opacity, you can't audit the training data. The compensating control is contractual representation + warranty + indemnification.
  4. Output non-determinism, bias evaluation has to happen empirically, not by code review.
  5. Classification drift, the same tool can become Annex III high-risk if you use it differently next year. Your contract needs to flex.
  6. Vendor-of-vendor, your AI vendor's main IP may be another AI vendor's API. Schrems II + Article 28 sub-processor + DORA Article 28 concentration risk all stack.
§
The buyer is the regulator's accountable party

EU AI Act Article 26 deployer obligations land on you the buyer. You can't outsource them to the vendor. The vendor's "we comply with AI Act" statement doesn't discharge your deployer duties. Procurement is the moment to inherit the right amount of risk, not all of it, not none.

Sub-module 1 takeaway
Traditional SaaS templates produce static answers to a dynamic question. AI procurement needs change-clauses, sub-processor transparency, and an honest deployer-obligation map. The buyer is the accountable party, design the contract for that.
Sub-module 2 of 10

The 37-question vendor questionnaire, as a buying instrument

The AI vendor questionnaire ships with this course. Eight sections, 37 questions, CSA CAIQ-AI-shaped. Most procurement teams treat it as a compliance form to file. The right use is upstream, as a buying instrument that scores vendors, surfaces material gaps, and produces follow-up actions before signing.

How to use it as a buying instrument

  1. Send before pricing, the questionnaire is a filter, not a confirmation. Pricing conversations after a vendor fails procurement filter waste time.
  2. Score each section, RAG (red / amber / green) per section; an amber-on-data-handling vendor is different from amber-on-incident-response.
  3. Demand evidence, not assertion, for the questions that affect risk (sub-processors, training data, incident response), ask for the supporting document, not a yes/no.
  4. Compare across shortlist, completed questionnaires are most useful as a comparative artefact. Side-by-side reveals what the market actually offers.
  5. Follow up on amber, a vendor whose answer is "we don't currently but will by [date]" is a viable vendor; one whose answer is "that's not how we think about it" is a different conversation.
  6. Lock the answers in the contract, material answers (data residency, training-data opt-out, sub-processor change notification) become contractual representations. Annex A of the master agreement.

The 5 questions that typically reveal the most

  • Q on sub-processors, Will you publish your sub-processor list and notify on change?
  • Q on data residency, Where exactly is prompt data + response data + training data + log data stored?
  • Q on training, Is our data used to train your foundation models? In any tier? Now or in the future?
  • Q on incident response, What is your notification window for an AI-system serious incident under Article 73?
  • Q on model change, How do you notify customers of material model updates, and what review rights do we have?
Sub-module 2 takeaway
The questionnaire is a buying instrument when sent early, scored RAG, demanded with evidence, compared across shortlist, and locked into the contract. Treat answers as procurement-grade evidence; treat gaps as red flags.
Sub-module 3 of 10

DPA review for AI vendors, the four clauses that matter most

The Data Processing Agreement under GDPR Article 28 governs how the vendor handles personal data on your behalf. AI vendor DPAs add specific concerns; many off-the-shelf DPAs miss them. Review every AI vendor's DPA, not just sign.

Clause 1, Scope of processing + lawful purposes

The DPA must state the categories of data + categories of data subjects + nature + purpose of processing + duration. For AI vendors specifically: confirm whether the data is also used for model improvement / training (this is usually a separate purpose that requires a separate lawful basis).

Clause 2, Sub-processor approval + notification

Article 28(2) gives the controller specific rights when the processor engages a sub-processor. For AI vendors, the chain is typically deep (model provider + hosting provider + vector store + logging). Insist on:

  • A complete published sub-processor list at signing.
  • 30-day prior notification of any addition or change.
  • A right to object that has teeth (not "you may object but we'll proceed anyway").
  • Termination rights if the change is material.

Clause 3, Data return / deletion on termination

AI vendor outputs + prompts + logs persist across many systems. The DPA must specify:

  • What is returned (raw prompts, generated outputs, fine-tuned model weights if applicable, logs).
  • The format of return.
  • The deletion timeline + verification mechanism.
  • Backup-retention treatment.
  • Aggregated / model-improvement use after termination (most vendors retain "anonymised" derived data; clarify what this means).

Clause 4, Schrems II + transfer-impact assessment

For non-EU AI vendors or EU vendors with non-EU sub-processors, the DPA must support the international-transfer mechanism. Specifically:

  • Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) attached or incorporated by reference.
  • Supplementary measures named (encryption + access controls + processing-location restrictions).
  • Cooperation in your TIA, vendor commits to providing transfer-impact-assessment supporting documentation.
  • Government-access disclosure, what the vendor will tell you about lawful-access requests it receives.
!
"Standard DPA" is rarely AI-fit

A DPA written in 2020 for a CRM doesn't anticipate model swaps, training-data use, sub-processor depth, or Article 73 incident windows. Insist on AI-specific addenda. Many AI vendors now offer them; ask before you sign.

Sub-module 3 takeaway
Four clauses on every AI vendor DPA: scope including training use, sub-processor with teeth, data return + deletion verification, Schrems II + TIA support. Review every time; sign nothing standard.
Sub-module 4 of 10

Sub-processor disclosure, what to demand, how to verify

AI vendor sub-processor chains are often opaque by default. The vendor uses a model provider + hosting + vector DB + logging + analytics; you signed with one company but your data touches five. Bringing this into daylight is a procurement responsibility.

What to demand at procurement

  1. The complete sub-processor list, name, role, country of incorporation, country of processing, sub-processor's own sub-processors if material.
  2. A public sub-processor page, vendor maintains a published page (Snyk, OpenAI, Microsoft all do this) so you can subscribe to changes.
  3. Material-change-notification commitment, vendor commits to email notification at least 30 days before adding a sub-processor.
  4. Your right to object, written into the DPA + master agreement.
  5. Termination right on material change, if the new sub-processor is unacceptable (data residency, security posture, regulatory concern), you can terminate with pro-rata refund.

How to verify

  • Cross-reference the vendor's published list against their DPA + master agreement (consistency check).
  • Check the vendor's sub-processors' own posted sub-processors (depth-2 check).
  • Search the vendor's website + Trust Centre for recent sub-processor announcements (recency check).
  • Spot-check by SOC 2 / ISO 27001 audit report references (third-party-evidence check).
  • For high-risk deployments, request an attestation letter from the vendor's auditor regarding the sub-processor list at a specific date.

What changes when the sub-processor is itself an AI provider

Many SaaS AI tools are built on a foundation-model API (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google). The foundation-model provider is the sub-processor. Material implications:

  • The sub-processor has its own data-handling terms, what does the foundation-model provider keep, for how long, and is it used for training?
  • Foundation-model API outages cascade to your tool.
  • Foundation-model provider's policy changes can change your tool's behaviour without your tool vendor doing anything.
  • DORA Article 28 + similar concentration-risk frameworks apply to indirect dependency.
Sub-module 4 takeaway
Sub-processor chains for AI are 3-5 deep typically. Demand the complete list + published page + 30-day notification + objection right + termination right. Verify on cadence. Treat the foundation-model provider as a sub-processor in its own right.
Sub-module 5 of 10

EU AI Act Article 13, provider obligations you can lean on at buying time

Article 13 of the EU AI Act requires providers of high-risk AI systems to supply specific information to deployers. As a procurement persona, this is exactly the documentation you can demand as part of procurement, and exactly the documentation a serious vendor should be ready to provide.

What Article 13 requires the provider to supply

  1. Instructions for use, clear, complete, accessible documentation covering intended purpose, performance, limitations, and conditions of use.
  2. Identity + contact, provider name, authorised representative in the EU, contact for compliance questions.
  3. Characteristics + capabilities + limitations, including known biases, performance metrics, accuracy, robustness.
  4. Any pre-determined changes to performance, for systems designed to learn or adapt over time.
  5. Human-oversight measures, the technical measures that enable Article 14 oversight, including the interpretation tools the deployer needs.
  6. Computational + hardware resources needed, relevant for deployment planning.
  7. Expected lifetime + maintenance + cybersecurity measures, including update mechanisms.

How to use this at procurement

  • Demand the Article 13 documentation as part of the procurement evidence pack.
  • Read it before signing. If it doesn't address the intended-purpose statement you need, that's a sign the vendor didn't understand your use case.
  • Compare across vendors, sufficient Article 13 documentation is a maturity signal.
  • File it with your contract. Your future FRIA / DPIA / clinical-safety case file will reference it.
  • If the AI is not high-risk under Article 6, Article 13 doesn't strictly apply, but a serious vendor still produces equivalent documentation. Lower-risk doesn't mean no documentation.
!
"We're not regulated as a provider" is suspicious

If a vendor of an AI tool says "we don't see ourselves as a provider under the AI Act" without explaining why, that's a red flag. Either they're a provider and don't realise it (immature vendor), or they've taken a defensible classification you should examine. Get the analysis in writing.

Sub-module 5 takeaway
Article 13 is your procurement-evidence shopping list for high-risk AI. Demand the documentation; read it; compare across shortlist; file it with the contract. A vendor that can't or won't supply it is telling you something.
Sub-module 6 of 10

EU AI Act Article 26, deployer obligations you take on when you buy

The moment you sign for a high-risk AI system, Article 26 lands on you as deployer. The MSP / IT Admin track Sub-module 2 covers this from the operations side. From the procurement side, the lesson is: design the procurement so the deployer obligations are discharged-able.

Article 26 obligations that procurement can shape

Art 26 sub-clauseDeployer obligationWhat procurement does about it
26(1)Use system in accordance with instructions for useDemand Article 13 documentation at procurement; verify it covers your intended use
26(2)Assign human oversight to competent natural personsBuild training + role-designation into rollout plan; budget for it
26(3)Where deployer controls input data, ensure relevant + representativeMap the data-flow; build the input-control responsibility into the deployment plan
26(5)Monitor operation; suspend + inform provider on identified risksBuild monitoring requirement into SLAs; specify provider incident channel
26(6)Retain auto-generated logs ≥ 6 monthsNegotiate log retention + access + export into the contract
26(7)Inform workers + representatives of high-risk AI use in employmentBuild employee + works-council communication into rollout
26(11)Cooperate with competent authoritiesBuild vendor cooperation into contract; specify response timelines

The procurement red-flag for deployer obligations

When a vendor's standard contract makes any of these difficult or impossible, log retention is 30 days, sub-processor changes can't be vetoed, provider won't cooperate with authorities, that's a procurement decision, not an operational decision. Procurement either negotiates the change or rejects the vendor.

Sub-module 6 takeaway
Article 26 lands on you the moment you sign. Procurement shapes which obligations are discharge-able. Vendor contracts that prevent log retention, monitoring, or authority cooperation are procurement rejections, not operations problems.

Sub-module 8 of 10

Contracting for change, model swaps, term changes, exit + data clauses

An AI contract written for the model the vendor is using today fails the moment the vendor updates the model. Contract for change explicitly, model swap clauses, term-change notification, exit-data clauses, or accept that you'll be renegotiating every quarter.

Model swap clause, what to demand

  • Notification window, minimum 60 days notice of material model change.
  • Definition of "material", the clause specifies what counts. "Underlying foundation model changes" is one trigger; "performance metric changes outside agreed range" is another.
  • Review period, you have a documented window to test the change against your critical workflows.
  • Pause right, you can ask the vendor to delay the rollout to your tenant if the test reveals regression.
  • Termination right, if you cannot accept the change, you can terminate without penalty.

Term-change notification clause

AI vendors update terms of service frequently, training-data clauses, retention windows, sub-processor lists. The clause should require email notification at least 30 days before any change, with a documented objection + termination route.

Exit + data return clause

An AI vendor contract you can't exit is one that has all the leverage. Specify:

  • Exit notice period, typically 60-90 days; longer for material deployments.
  • Data return format, machine-readable; specify the schema if necessary.
  • Data deletion, verified deletion timeline; backup-retention treatment; audit trail.
  • Transition support, vendor will support migration for a defined period.
  • Fine-tuning + model weights, if any fine-tuning has occurred, the clause specifies whether weights are returned, destroyed, or whether they remain with the vendor with continuation restrictions.
  • No hostage, pricing during the exit period is no worse than pricing during the contract.

Indemnification + warranty for IP + bias

For high-risk AI deployments, demand:

  • Indemnification for third-party IP claims arising from training-data use.
  • Warranty that the model has been tested for the specific protected-characteristic disparate-impact issues relevant to your sector.
  • Limitation-of-liability caps that aren't risible (a £1k cap on a £500k contract is a procurement reject).
Sub-module 8 takeaway
Contract for change explicitly: model-swap notice + review + pause + termination; term-change notification; exit + data return with no hostage; IP + bias indemnification. The contract that assumes the future will look like the present fails fastest.
Sub-module 9 of 10

The buy-vs-build-vs-defer decision tree for AI

The temptation is to buy. The right answer is sometimes to defer, sometimes to build, sometimes to compose. A decision-tree procurement persona owns brings rigor to a conversation often shaped by vendor demos.

Step 1, Is the use case clearly defined?

If "we want to use AI to help with customer service" is the goal, defer until the goal is specific. "We want to reduce our average response time on Tier-1 tickets by 30% with AI-assisted drafts under human review" is procurement-able. Vague goals produce vague procurements.

Step 2, Is the risk classification known?

Run the use case through the Annex III list. If it's high-risk, the procurement bar is materially higher. If it's not, lighter due diligence applies. Don't procure unknown-classification AI.

Step 3, Does an approved-tool already cover it?

Before procuring net-new, check whether your approved AI estate already covers the use case. M365 Copilot covers many SaaS use cases. ChatGPT Enterprise covers others. Net-new AI procurement adds sub-processor + concentration + governance overhead, only do it if existing tooling genuinely can't.

Step 4, Buy / build / compose

ChoiceWhen it's rightWhen it's wrong
Buy from a specialist vendorUse case is commodity; vendor has scale + maturity; sector framework supports themUse case is competitive differentiator; vendor lock-in too high; sub-processor chain too deep
Compose (use foundation-model API directly)You have engineering capability; the workflow is bespoke; you want lower vendor concentrationYou don't have engineering capability; you don't want to operate the inference; sector demands certified tooling
Build (train a custom model)Use case is core differentiator; data exists in volume; engineering + ML expertise existsOff-the-shelf would work; data doesn't exist; ML expertise is mid-market-typical
DeferTech / regulation / vendor market not mature enough for the use case; competing investment prioritiesDefer is hiding indecision; use case is genuinely high-value + ready

Step 5, Total cost of ownership

AI TCO is the licence + integration + governance + monitoring + retraining + audit + incident-handling. Don't compare vendors on licence cost alone. Build the TCO model with operational input.

§
"Defer" is a legitimate procurement decision

A common procurement failure is procuring AI to satisfy a CEO mandate before the use case is mature. Defer + invest in the use-case definition first. A six-month defer that produces a clear specification is more valuable than a six-month procurement of the wrong thing.

Sub-module 9 takeaway
Define the use case before procuring. Check existing tooling first. Choose buy / build / compose / defer deliberately. Compare on TCO, not licence cost. Defer is a legitimate answer, sometimes the best one.
Sub-module 10 of 10

Final assessment

Ten procurement-judgement questions. Self-mark. Suggested pass: 8/10.

Question 1

An AI vendor's standard DPA doesn't address training-data use. Their sales rep says "we don't use customer data for training, it's just not written down". Proceed?

Answer

No. Material assertions about training-data use must be contractual. A vendor that won't write it down may change practice without breaching contract. Require the assertion to land as a representation + warranty in the DPA or addendum. If they refuse, that's the answer.

Question 2

Three AI vendors in your shortlist. Vendor A scores all-green on the questionnaire. Vendor B scores green-on-data-handling, amber-on-incident-response. Vendor C scores amber-on-data-handling, green-on-incident-response. Which is closer to procurement-ready?

Answer

Vendor B, data-handling material gaps are harder to remediate post-signing. Amber-on-incident-response is typically negotiable (better SLAs, contractual notification windows). Amber-on-data-handling can mean architectural decisions baked into the product. Score severity-weighted, not count-weighted.

Question 3

Your AI vendor's sub-processor list includes "OpenAI". You're a financial-services firm. What additional due diligence applies?

Answer

(a) DORA Article 28 concentration risk, multiple of your AI vendors may all depend on OpenAI; map the indirect dependency. (b) Schrems II transfer-impact assessment for US processing. (c) OpenAI's own DPA + sub-processor + data-handling terms apply downstream. (d) Material change to OpenAI's terms can affect you via your vendor. Your DPA needs to flow these through.

Question 4

A vendor refuses to commit to a 30-day sub-processor notification window. They offer 7 days. Acceptable?

Answer

7 days is rarely enough to do meaningful due diligence + DPO consultation + objection process + alternative-vendor evaluation. Push back to 30 days; settle at 21 if compromise needed; reject 7 unless your operational tolerance is genuinely high. Document the negotiation outcome in case auditor asks why you accepted.

Question 5

An AI vendor's product is sometimes used in a high-risk context (Annex III) and sometimes for low-risk admin. Is Article 26 deployer obligation engaged universally?

Answer

It depends on your actual use. If you procure it for both contexts, the high-risk use triggers Article 26 obligations for that use; the low-risk use doesn't. Your AUP should specify use scope. Where the same procurement covers both, document the scope split clearly so deployer obligations track the actual use.

Question 6

You're choosing between two AI vendors. A is £15k/year, ships in 2 weeks, has weak Article 13 documentation. B is £24k/year, ships in 6 weeks, has comprehensive Article 13 documentation. CFO says "go with A". What do you say?

Answer

"The £9k delta is the cost of buying a vendor that has done its compliance work. The risk-adjusted TCO of A is significantly higher than B because (1) we'll have to build the Article 13 equivalent ourselves or absorb the deployer-obligation risk, (2) the audit story is weaker, (3) the procurement-readiness gate for our biggest customers may not pass with A. The 4-week delay buys us a contract our auditor + regulator + biggest-buyer can defend." Document the analysis; let the CFO override if they choose, but document.

Question 7

An AI vendor's standard contract has a £5k limitation of liability. Your contract value is £80k/year + the AI affects customer-facing decisions. Proceed?

Answer

No. The cap is misaligned with risk. Negotiate up. Typical AI vendor caps for material customer-affecting deployments are 12x annual fees or higher with carve-outs (IP indemnification + breach of confidentiality + wilful misconduct uncapped). Reject standard caps that don't reflect deployment risk.

Question 8

You're procuring an AI tool for a UK NHS practice. Vendor says they're "not a medical device" but the tool affects clinical workflow. What's the procurement workflow?

Answer

(a) Demand the vendor's documented intended-purpose statement; (b) Run it through MDR Rule 11 (software classification) with input from clinical lead; (c) If your analysis says it's a medical device, refuse "we're not a medical device" framing, require CE/UKCA marking + DCB0129 case file; (d) If MHRA classification is genuinely uncertain, engage MHRA's pre-submission service or a notified body; (e) Don't proceed on the vendor's bare assertion. See healthcare sector overlay sub-module 3.

Question 9

Your AI vendor announces a model update. Their email arrives 14 days before rollout. Your contract requires 60 days. What do you do?

Answer

(1) Email vendor in writing requiring full 60-day notice per contract; ask for delay; (2) If they refuse the delay, treat as material breach + invoke pause-right + termination-right per contract; (3) Notify your incident-management workflow, premature deployment of a model change is itself an incident; (4) Document the breach for any future negotiation or termination decision; (5) Consider whether the breach pattern + the vendor's responsiveness signal a relationship-level concern.

Question 10

You're an MSP procuring AI tooling to deliver via your customer base. The customer base spans FS, healthcare, and public sector. What's the procurement strategy?

Answer

(a) Procure tools that satisfy the most-stringent sector's requirements (typically healthcare or public sector); (b) The tools you procure for healthcare can usually serve FS and public sector with the regulatory layer added through sector overlays; (c) Multi-tenant + customer-level admin controls are mandatory; (d) Vendor must accept your customer-attribution data flow + audit; (e) Contract terms must permit your customer-handover of audit-pack artefacts; (f) Pricing model must support pass-through to customers without contractual gymnastics; (g) Each tool should be procurement-ready under DORA Art 13 + DCB0129 + Article 25 framework simultaneously. See MSP / IT Admin track.
