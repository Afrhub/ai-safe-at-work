Bias, fairness, and not embarrassing the business.

AI does not hold opinions. It holds patterns, and the patterns it learned from were not neutral. The risks here are reputational, legal and human, sometimes all three at once.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Understand the three sources of bias in AI outputs and why they're hard to remove
  • Recognise the three categories of work where AI bias becomes a legal problem, not just a quality one
  • Apply practical checks before AI-generated content goes near a customer, candidate, or public audience

Where bias comes from

An AI model's outputs reflect three things layered on top of each other:

  1. The training data. Mostly the public web, books, and code. The internet over-represents some voices and under-represents others. The model learns the over-representation as "normal".
  2. The fine-tuning. A second training stage where humans rate model outputs and the model learns to produce outputs that get good ratings. The humans doing the rating have their own preferences, blind spots, and demographics.
  3. Your prompt. You don't realise how much your wording shapes the output until you change one word and watch the answer change. "Write a CV for a software engineer" produces a different person, statistically, than "Write a CV for a software engineering manager".

You can't eliminate bias by switching to a different model. They all share the same internet. You can manage it by knowing where it shows up and putting checks in the right places.

The three categories that matter

1. Decisions about people

Hiring, firing, promotion, pay, performance reviews, references. Anything where an AI's output influences whether a specific person gets something they applied for or asked for.

The European pattern is consistent: any AI system used in this category counts as high-risk under the EU AI Act (Annex III, point 4). That triggers a long list of obligations on the deployer — your business — including risk assessment, logging, human oversight, instructions to staff, and registration where required.

In plain English: if you're a manager, recruiter, or HR person and you've been using ChatGPT to screen CVs or draft performance feedback, the rules now apply to that activity even if you didn't realise they did.

!
"It's just a draft" isn't a defence

"I only use AI to draft the feedback — a human writes the final version" sounds like a clean answer, but in practice the final version is usually 80% the draft with minor edits. If the draft was biased in tone, the final version is biased in tone. The mitigation has to be more concrete than "I'll edit it".

2. Public-facing or customer-facing content

Marketing copy, social media posts, customer-service chatbots, blog articles, product descriptions, autoresponders, anything with the company name on it.

The risk here is reputational rather than regulatory, but it lands faster: a screenshot of a customer-service chatbot saying something offensive can be on social media before you've finished your coffee. AI-generated marketing copy that excludes a major customer group — say, by defaulting to assumptions about gender, family structure, age, ability, or country — is a complaint, a refund, and sometimes a viral pile-on.

3. Decisions that affect access to services

Loans, insurance, education places, healthcare triage, fraud scoring, immigration applications, social benefits. These are the EU AI Act's other big high-risk categories. Most small businesses don't touch them directly — but you may be the procurement person who signs off on a vendor that does, or the operations person who configures one. The decisions to use these systems carefully are made by people just like you.

The specific failure modes

Gendered language and assumptions

AI-generated job descriptions sometimes default to masculine-coded language ("dominant", "competitive", "rockstar", "ninja") which is well-evidenced to reduce female application rates. AI-generated examples and case studies tend to default to male protagonists. AI-generated translations into gendered languages (French, German, Spanish) sometimes default to masculine forms even when the source was neutral.

Cultural defaults

Models trained on English-language internet text default to American cultural references, American legal context, American holidays, American spellings. A model writing customer copy for a French SMB will, unless told otherwise, produce something that sounds slightly off — pricing in dollars, references to Thanksgiving, "fall" instead of "autumn".

Stereotyped imagery

AI image generators, asked for "a CEO", over-produce one demographic. Asked for "a nurse", they over-produce another. Asked for "a person from [country]", they often produce caricatures. If you use AI-generated imagery in any public-facing material, you have to actively curate the output, not just take the first plausible image.

Excluding minorities and disabilities

AI-generated copy sometimes assumes a default user with no accessibility needs, no caring responsibilities, no minority identity. Forms drafted with AI sometimes lack the categories ("prefer not to say", "other", "non-binary") that real users need. AI-generated user research scripts sometimes ask questions that exclude or pathologise certain experiences.

Confident wrongness about underrepresented topics

Models trained on the public web know less about communities, languages, regions, and identities that have less representation in that data. The output for "a wedding ceremony" defaults heavily to one tradition; for "a successful entrepreneur", to one demographic. The error doesn't always show up as overt offence — it shows up as a quiet exclusion in the framing.

Practical checks

✓
Before AI-generated content goes to a real audience
  1. Read it as the person you'd most want to apologise to. Your toughest customer. Your least-represented colleague. The journalist who'd love a story.
  2. Search the text for the four words "he", "she", "guys", and "young". Each one is a signal to check whether a stereotype just slipped in.
  3. For imagery: generate at least three options. If they all look the same, the model is being lazy and so are you. Re-prompt with explicit specificity.
  4. For decisions about people: never let the AI's output be the final word. Document what you did differently from what the AI suggested, and why.
  5. For translations: have someone fluent in the target language read it. AI translations are excellent at fluency and ordinary at nuance. The nuance is what gets you in trouble.

"Bias-free AI" doesn't exist

Some vendors market their models as "fair" or "unbiased". That is marketing. Every model carries biases. Different models carry different biases. The useful question is "have we put checks in the right places to catch this model's specific weaknesses". That is good practice. That is what the standards require.

§
Where the standards land

EU AI Act Annex III: lists the categories of "high-risk" AI use. Most relevant for SMBs: employment-related decisions (point 4), education access (point 3), creditworthiness (point 5b), and access to essential services. Article 26: deployer obligations include monitoring for bias and instructing staff appropriately. Article 27: fundamental rights impact assessment, required for certain deployers of high-risk AI. ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Annex A.6.2: requirements for AI system design include fairness considerations. Annex A.7: data quality, including representativeness. GDPR Article 22: the data subject's right not to be subject to automated decision-making that produces legal or similarly significant effects on them — which means certain AI-driven decisions require human involvement, transparency, and the right to contest.

One sentence to take with you

"The AI's first draft is its laziest draft." Treat it that way. Edit harder than you would your own writing, especially in the places where it would be easiest to just nod and ship.
