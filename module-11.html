Run this list before you paste anything into an AI tool, including the things that feel safe. It takes a minute. The people who get burned skipped the minute.

Designed to print on one or two A4 pages. Pin it next to your screen.
Section 1 · The data

Before you paste, ask

Is this on the never-paste list? Customer/contact personal data · special-category data (health, ethnicity, religion, biometrics) · contracts I didn't draft · source code I don't own · non-public financials · passwords or API keys · documents marked confidential · HR records · client-engagement material. If yes — reformulate or use the enterprise tool.
Could I describe the shape of the problem instead of pasting the data? "I have a CSV with three columns: X, Y, Z — give me the Excel formula" rather than pasting the rows. Works for 80% of real tasks.
If this data leaks tomorrow, who would I be apologising to? Customers? Employees? Suppliers? My boss? My regulator? If the answer makes you wince, don't paste.
Section 2 · The tool

Before you choose the AI

Am I using the right tier? Free / paid-individual is consumer territory — no DPA with your employer. Team / Business / Enterprise tiers have proper contracts. For anything sensitive, use what your business has procured. If your business has nothing — ask.
Is training-on-my-conversations turned OFF? Check settings now if you've never checked. "Improve the model" / "share with provider" / "chat history & training" toggles all live in slightly different places. Find them.
Do I know where the data physically goes? For EU personal data, this matters. Enterprise tiers usually let you pick. Free tiers usually don't.
Section 3 · The output

Before you act on what the AI gave you

Did I read the whole thing, line by line? Not skimmed. Read. AI outputs that sound great often contain one sentence nobody would actually write or believe.
If there are facts or citations — did I verify them? Click every reference. If it doesn't open, the source isn't real. Two independent sources for anything high-stakes.
If this affects a person — did a human actually think? Hiring, firing, pricing, decisions about customers and employees — the human signing off has to do the work of signing off, not click "approve". The AI's suggestion is the start of the thinking, not the end.
Could a reasonable customer find anything excluding, stereotyped, or off-brand in this? Default check on AI-written marketing, public copy, generated images.
Section 4 · The trail

Before you close the tab

Is this a "significant" AI use that I should log? Affects another person? Represents the business? Touched sensitive data? Irreversible or expensive? Two yeses = log it. Eight fields: date · who · tool/tier · task · inputs · output · what I did with it · who else reviewed.
If this AI-assisted work was challenged in six months — could I explain how I used the AI? That's the standard. Not "could I replay it exactly" — "could I describe it honestly".
Section 5 · When you're suspicious of something coming AT you

Before you act on an unusual request

Did this arrive perfectly written, with the right context, asking me to do something unusual? Perfectly-written phishing is the current baseline. The red flag is the request, not the writing.
Have I verified the sender on a different channel that I initiated? Look up their number in your directory — don't use the one in the email. Call them on video. Ask them something only they would know. Especially: money, credentials, access.
Why is this urgent? If the answer is "because I said so" or "I'm in a meeting", slow down. Attackers depend on urgency. Slowing down is the defence.
Section 6 · If something goes wrong

The first ten minutes

Stop. Don't delete the evidence. Don't try to "fix" it alone. Pause the process. Take a screenshot. Keep the chat history. The reflex to delete is the wrong instinct.
Tell your manager / IT / DPO within the hour. If personal data leaked, the 72-hour GDPR breach clock starts when the business becomes aware. Speed of escalation is what saves it.
If money was sent or credentials shared — act on those immediately. Call the bank. Change the password. Disable the access. Rotation costs minutes; the breach costs months.

§
A note on context

This checklist is grounded in GDPR (Articles 5, 6, 9, 22, 32–34), ISO/IEC 27001:2022 (especially A.5, A.6, A.8), ISO/IEC 42001:2023 (Clauses 6–9 and Annex A), and the EU AI Act (Articles 4, 12, 14, 26, 50). It's not a substitute for your own business's policy or your own legal advice. But for most people on most days, working through this list before pasting will keep you out of trouble.
