Copyright, IP, and other people's content.

The law here is in motion. Court cases, legislation and licensing agreements are reshaping the rules every few months. What follows is what's true in 2026, with enough caveats that it'll still be useful in 2027.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Understand who owns AI-generated output in the EU, UK, and US — to the extent anyone owns it
  • Know what "input copyright" means and why pasting other people's content into AI is not always neutral
  • Apply a practical test before publishing AI-generated images, copy, code, or music
  • Recognise the ongoing major lawsuits and what they could mean for businesses that rely on AI output

Two copyright questions, not one

AI and copyright is two separate problems people often confuse:

  1. Input copyright. When you (or the AI vendor) feed copyrighted material into an AI — does that need permission?
  2. Output copyright. When the AI produces something — who owns it, and is it allowed to be published?

The legal position differs for each and differs by jurisdiction. Below is the practical headline for each.

Input — feeding copyrighted material to AI

When you paste a copyrighted article, book chapter, image, song lyric, or company document into an AI tool to ask it to summarise or transform, you're making a copy of that material and sending it to a third party. Depending on what you do next, that may be:

  • Probably fine — if it's your own content, public-domain material, or content you have a licence to use that includes "machine reading".
  • Probably a problem — if it's behind a paywall, marked as "no AI training", under a Creative Commons licence that excludes commercial reuse, or you're using the AI to produce something competitive with the original.
  • Definitely a problem — if it's a client's confidential information, a competitor's internal document, or material from a contract whose confidentiality clauses don't permit third-party processing (see Module 3).
!
"Summarise this article for me" isn't always neutral

News publishers are increasingly explicit that their content is licensed for personal reading, not for AI processing. Pasting a paywalled article into ChatGPT to summarise it for distribution may breach the publisher's terms. For occasional personal note-taking, this rarely matters. For systematic use ("I summarise the FT every morning and send to the team"), it does.

Output — who owns what the AI makes

The headline rule, in most jurisdictions in 2026: fully AI-generated material with no significant human creative input cannot be copyrighted. It's in the public domain by default. This was confirmed by the US Copyright Office in 2023, by the courts in multiple US cases since, and by similar reasoning in the EU, UK, Canada, and Australia.

The practical consequence: if you generate an image, a piece of music, or a piece of copy entirely from an AI prompt, your business has no copyright in it. Anyone else can use the same output. The same applies to derivative versions.

"Significant human creative input" is the moving line. Selecting between options, editing the output, combining multiple AI outputs into something new, using AI as one tool inside a longer creative process — these tend to attract some copyright protection, in the human-contributed parts. Pure prompt-and-paste does not.

Probably copyrightable

An illustrator uses AI for ideation, hand-redraws the result, composites it with other elements they made. The marketing team writes copy, uses AI to suggest variants, picks and edits the best. A developer writes the architecture and tests, uses AI for boilerplate, edits and structures the whole thing.

Probably not

One-shot AI image used directly in a campaign. AI-written blog post with light editing. AI-generated background music for a video, used as-is. AI-generated stock photo placed as-is on a website. Same applies if a competitor uses identical prompts.

Is the output safe to publish?

Two risks even when you "own" the output:

1. Substantial similarity to training data

Some AI image generators have been shown to reproduce specific copyrighted images almost verbatim under certain prompts — Disney characters, well-known photographs, particular illustrators' styles. Publishing such an output, even unintentionally, is copyright infringement, and the AI vendor's terms typically pass the risk to you.

The same applies in code: an AI coding assistant sometimes produces a function that is a near-verbatim copy of a specific piece of training-set code. If that code was under a copyleft licence (GPL, AGPL) that requires your code to also be open-source, using it in a proprietary codebase is a problem.

2. The right of publicity and the right of personality

AI can produce content that looks like a real, identifiable person — a celebrity, a politician, a former employee — without their consent. Using such content commercially may breach the right of publicity (US) or the right of personality (EU). Some jurisdictions have explicit rules against unauthorised AI imitation of real people; more are coming.

3. The vendor's terms

Most major AI vendors' terms say "you keep ownership of your output" but also say "you indemnify the vendor against any third-party claim arising from your use". Read: if the output infringes someone's copyright and they sue, that's your problem, not the vendor's. A few vendors now offer indemnification on certain enterprise tiers — Microsoft, Adobe, Google have all rolled out limited versions. Worth knowing whether your business has one of those.

The ongoing lawsuits

You don't need to follow these in detail, but it's worth knowing they exist, because the eventual outcomes will reshape what you can rely on:

  • The New York Times v. OpenAI and Microsoft — the NYT claims its articles were used to train models without permission.
  • Getty Images v. Stability AI — Getty claims Stability's image model was trained on Getty's watermarked stock photos.
  • The Authors Guild v. OpenAI — a class action by authors over use of copyrighted books in training data.
  • Multiple coding-assistant cases — Doe v. GitHub, claims that GitHub Copilot's training and output infringe open-source licences.

The honest summary: nobody knows yet. The cases are likely to settle or split into different outcomes by jurisdiction. But the trend is clear — the era of "the model was trained on the open web, deal with it" is closing. Licensing deals between AI vendors and publishers are now happening in volume. Expect more obligations on businesses that use AI output, not fewer.

Practical rules

✓
A safer use of AI output
  1. Treat purely AI-generated content as public domain. Don't put a copyright notice on something you didn't actually author. If you want a copyright, do enough creative work that it's actually yours.
  2. For images you're going to publish, generate three, edit them, and document the process. A short note (date, prompts used, what was edited) lives in the asset folder. If you ever need to defend the work, you have a record.
  3. For text that will be customer-facing or formal, AI is your editor, not your author. Write the substance yourself, use AI to tighten, smooth, translate, fact-check.
  4. For code, check your project's licensing rules. If you're working on something that must stay free of GPL-licensed code, treat unknown AI-generated snippets with caution. Run a tool that scans output against known training-set matches if your codebase is sensitive.
  5. For known third-party material — paywalled articles, leaked documents, competitor materials — assume it shouldn't be in the prompt at all. Module 3 covers the "describe the shape" reformulation that gets you the same value.

"My business uses AI to write our blog posts"

Common, and not in itself a problem. Considerations:

  • The content can be copied by competitors. The blog post, in its AI-generated form, has no copyright protection. Someone can lift it whole.
  • Search engines are increasingly distinguishing AI-generated content. Google's stated position is that quality, not authorship, is what matters — but pure AI content is treated as lower-quality in practice and often deranked. The economic case for "AI does all our blogging" has weakened, not strengthened.
  • Transparency expectations are rising. Disclosing that an article was AI-assisted is good practice and increasingly an audience expectation. The EU AI Act will eventually require explicit labelling for some categories of synthetic content (Article 50).
§
Where the standards land

EU AI Act Article 50: transparency for generative AI — providers must mark outputs as AI-generated where reasonably possible, and deployers using AI to produce "deepfakes" or text on matters of public interest must disclose. Article 53: providers of general-purpose AI models must publish a summary of training data used. Copyright Directive (EU) 2019/790, Articles 3 and 4: text-and-data-mining exception, including the right of rightholders to opt out — the legal basis many EU publishers are now using to forbid AI training on their content. ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Annex A.5: AI policies should cover intellectual property and licensing.
