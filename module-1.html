Module 01 · 7 min read

Why this course exists.

Three real incidents that cost real money. None of them happened at AI companies. They happened to people doing ordinary jobs, on a Tuesday, with the best of intentions.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Recognise the three most common ways AI causes problems for small and medium businesses
  • Understand why "I was just trying to be efficient" is the most expensive sentence in this whole field
  • Know where on the risk map your own AI use sits

Story one: the helpful intern

An intern at a logistics company has been asked to clean up a messy customer spreadsheet. Names, addresses, order histories — 40,000 rows. They paste the whole thing into ChatGPT and ask it to "deduplicate, then sort by region". It works beautifully. The intern is praised.

Six months later, a security researcher publishes an article showing that some chatbot conversations can be retrieved from cached search-engine results. The company's customer list is on page three of Google.

The cost: a regulator's letter. A mandatory breach notification to every customer. Twelve months of free identity-monitoring offered as goodwill. One lost enterprise contract. The intern still works there. The person who told them "just figure it out" doesn't.

!
What actually went wrong

The intern didn't make a stupid mistake. They used a tool that was on every device, that everyone in the office talked about, with no rule telling them not to. The mistake was organisational: no one had decided what kind of data was OK to paste into which tool.

Story two: the lawyer who didn't read the citations

A small law firm is preparing a brief on a Tuesday afternoon. The associate is behind. They ask ChatGPT to find precedents for a particular kind of contract dispute. It produces six perfect-looking case references — names, years, court, holding. The associate uses them.

The judge looks them up. None of them exist. The model invented all six, with believable case numbers, plausible parties, and quotes that sound exactly like real judgments.

The cost: a public sanction. The firm's name in legal news for the wrong reasons. Two clients leave. The associate's career took a permanent dent.

This isn't a hypothetical. Variations of it have happened in the US, the UK, Canada and Brazil. Mata v. Avianca was the first famous one. There have been many since.

Story three: the deepfake CEO

An accounts-payable clerk at a Hong Kong company joins a video call with the CFO and three other senior leaders. They've all been talking about an upcoming acquisition for weeks. On the call, the CFO authorises a series of urgent transfers totalling US$25 million.

Every person on the call was fake. The CFO's face, voice, and mannerisms were generated. So were the other three. The clerk processed the payments. The money is gone.

This happened in February 2024 to Arup, a real engineering firm. The technique is now cheap, fast, and increasingly common. The clerk did everything they were taught — verified the request was internal, joined a video call, saw familiar faces. The training they'd been given was from a world that doesn't exist anymore.

What these stories have in common

None of them involved hackers, malware, or anything most people would call "an attack". They involved good people using normal tools in ordinary ways, and the controls around those tools being five years out of date.

Risk falls into three buckets. This course covers all three:

Data you give the AI

You paste something sensitive. It leaks. Customer lists, contracts, source code, financials, HR records. Covered in Modules 2 and 3.

Output you trust

The AI invents something. You use it. Fake citations, hallucinated facts, biased decisions, exclusionary copy. Covered in Modules 5, 7, and 8.

AI used against you

Someone uses AI to impersonate, deceive, or manipulate you. Deepfakes, phishing, BEC, social engineering. Covered in Module 6.

And the response layer

What you do when something goes wrong, how you log AI-assisted decisions, what your business is required to evidence. Modules 9, 10, and 12.

"But I just use AI to write emails"

Most people reading this aren't building AI systems. They're using them. To write emails, draft proposals, summarise meetings, generate ideas, edit copy, translate, code, plan, brainstorm.

That's exactly the audience the rules are being written for. The EU AI Act, GDPR, ISO 42001 and ISO 27001 don't only apply to "AI companies". They apply to any organisation that uses AI to do its work — and the obligations land on the employees doing that work, just as much as on the IT team.

§
Where the standards land

GDPR Article 5 says you must process personal data "lawfully, fairly, and in a transparent manner" — pasting it into a tool whose terms you haven't read is none of those three. ISO 27001:2022 Annex A.5.34 requires organisations to protect personal information at every step. The EU AI Act (Article 4) requires that employees who interact with AI systems have an appropriate level of AI literacy. This course is, in part, the literacy that article requires.

What this course will not do

It won't make you a compliance officer. It won't qualify you to certify anything. It won't tell you which AI tools to use — that's your business's choice. And it won't pretend the rules are simpler than they are.

What it will do: give you enough situational awareness that you stop being the weakest link, and start being the person other people quietly ask before they click "send".

  2. Think of one moment in the last month when you pasted something into an AI tool and felt a flicker of "should I be doing this?". Hold onto it. Modules 3 and 11 will make that flicker feel less like paranoia and more like good judgement.
