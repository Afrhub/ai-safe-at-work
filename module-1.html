Module 01 · 7 min read

Why this course exists.

Three incidents that cost real money. Each one hit ordinary staff on an ordinary Tuesday, at firms with no AI products of their own.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Recognise the three most common ways AI causes problems for small and medium businesses
  • Understand why "I was just trying to be efficient" is the most expensive sentence in this whole field
  • Know where on the risk map your own AI use sits

Story one: the helpful intern

An intern at a logistics company needs to clean a messy customer spreadsheet: 40,000 rows of names, addresses and order histories. They paste the lot into ChatGPT, ask it to "deduplicate, then sort by region", and the output comes back clean. Their manager praises the work.

Six months later a security researcher publishes a finding. Search engines can sometimes serve cached chatbot conversations to anyone who searches the right way. The company's customer list shows up on page three of Google.

The company picks up the bill: a regulator's letter, a mandatory breach notification to every customer, twelve months of free identity-monitoring offered as goodwill, and one lost enterprise contract. The intern keeps their job. The person who told them to "just figure it out" loses theirs.

!
What actually went wrong

The intern wasn't careless. They used a tool installed on every laptop in the building, talked about openly at lunch, with no rule against it. The miss sits upstream of them: nobody had decided which data could go into which kind of tool.

Story two: the lawyer who didn't read the citations

Tuesday afternoon at a small law firm. An associate, running behind on a brief, asks ChatGPT for precedents on a particular contract dispute. ChatGPT returns six perfect-looking case references — names, years, court, holding. The associate files the brief with all six.

The judge looks them up. None exist. The model produced six plausible case numbers, six plausible parties, and six quotes pitched at the cadence of a real judgment.

The cost lands fast: a public sanction, the firm's name in legal news for the wrong reasons, two clients walking, and a permanent dent in the associate's career.

Variations of this story have played out in the US, the UK, Canada and Brazil. Mata v. Avianca was the first famous one. There have been many since.

Story three: the deepfake CEO

An accounts-payable clerk at a Hong Kong company joins a video call with the CFO and three other senior leaders. The team has been discussing an upcoming acquisition for weeks. On the call, the CFO greenlights urgent transfers totalling US$25 million.

Every face on that call was a deepfake. The CFO's voice and mannerisms, generated. The other three leaders, generated. The clerk processed the payments and the money disappeared.

This happened in February 2024 to Arup, a real engineering firm. The technique is now cheap, fast, and showing up in more cases each quarter. The clerk did everything the training said to do: verified the request was internal, joined a video call, saw familiar faces. The training came from a world that no longer exists.

What these stories have in common

Each incident featured good staff using normal tools in ordinary ways. No hackers, no malware, nothing most people would call "an attack". The controls around those tools were five years out of date.

Risk falls into three buckets. This course covers all three:

Data you give the AI

You paste something sensitive. It leaks. Customer lists, contracts, source code, financials, HR records. Covered in Modules 2 and 3.

Output you trust

The AI invents something. You use it. Fake citations, hallucinated facts, biased decisions, exclusionary copy. Covered in Modules 5, 7 and 8.

AI used against you

Someone uses AI to impersonate, deceive or manipulate you. Deepfakes, phishing, BEC, social engineering. Covered in Module 6.

And the response layer

What you do when something goes wrong, how you log AI-assisted decisions, what your business has to evidence. Modules 9, 10 and 12.

"But I just use AI to write emails"

Most readers of this course use AI rather than build it: writing emails, drafting proposals, summarising meetings, editing copy, translating, coding, planning, brainstorming.

That is the audience these rules target. The EU AI Act, GDPR, ISO 42001 and ISO 27001 apply to any organisation that uses AI to do its work. Obligations land on the staff doing that work, alongside the IT team.

§
Where the standards land

GDPR Article 5 says you must process personal data "lawfully, fairly, and in a transparent manner". Pasting it into a tool whose terms you haven't read fails all three tests. ISO 27001:2022 Annex A.5.34 requires organisations to protect personal information across the processing lifecycle. The EU AI Act (Article 4) requires that employees who interact with AI systems have an appropriate level of AI literacy. This course is part of the literacy that article requires.

What this course will not do

This course teaches you to spot AI risk and avoid the common mistakes. It does not make you a compliance officer, qualify you to certify anything, prescribe which AI tools your business should use, or pretend the rules are simpler than they are.

Pass it and your colleagues start asking your opinion before they hit send. That is what the literacy obligation looks like on a Tuesday.
