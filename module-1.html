Module 01 · 7 min read

Why this course exists.

Three true stories. Three normal workers. Three big messes that cost real money. None of these companies built AI. They just used it.

By the end of this module, you will
  • Spot the three main ways AI can cause trouble at work
  • See why "I was just trying to save time" can be a very costly thing to say
  • Work out how risky your own AI use is right now

Story one: the helpful intern

An intern at a delivery company has a messy list of customers to tidy up. The list has 40,000 rows: names, home addresses, and what each person ordered. The intern pastes the whole thing into ChatGPT and asks it to clean and sort the list. It comes back neat. Their boss is pleased.

Six months later, a security researcher spots a problem. Sometimes search engines can show old chatbot chats to anyone who looks the right way. The company's customer list turns up on Google.

Now the company has to pay. A letter from the regulator. A warning to every customer. A year of free ID-theft protection to say sorry. And one big client walks away. The intern keeps their job. The manager who said "just figure it out" does not.

!
What really went wrong

The intern was not being careless. They used a tool that sits on every laptop in the office. People talk about it at lunch. No rule said not to. The real mistake happened higher up: nobody had decided which information was safe to put into which tool.

Story two: the lawyer who didn't check

It is Tuesday afternoon at a small law firm. A lawyer is running late on a court document. They ask ChatGPT for past cases about a contract argument. ChatGPT gives back six cases that look perfect: names, years, courts, rulings. The lawyer puts all six in the document.

The judge checks them. Not one is real. The AI made up six cases that only sounded true.

The cost comes fast. A public telling-off. The firm's name in the news for the wrong reason. Two clients leave. And a big dent in the lawyer's career.

This has happened in the US, the UK, Canada and Brazil. The first famous one was a case called Mata v. Avianca. Many have followed.

Story three: the fake boss on the video call

A worker who handles payments at a Hong Kong company joins a video call. On the call are the finance boss and three other senior people. The team has been talking about buying another company for weeks. On the call, the finance boss tells them to send some urgent payments. They add up to 25 million dollars.

Every face on that call was fake. AI made the finance boss's voice and face. It made the other three people too. The worker sent the money. It vanished.

This really happened, in early 2024, to a real engineering firm called Arup. The trick is now cheap and quick, and it shows up more every few months. The worker did everything the old training said: they checked it was an inside request, joined a video call, and saw faces they knew. The training was built for a world that has changed.

What these stories share

Each time, good staff used normal tools in normal ways. No hackers. No viruses. Nothing most people would call an attack. The safety rules around those tools were years out of date.

AI risk comes in three main shapes. This course covers all three:

What you put in

You paste something private and it leaks. Customer lists, contracts, code, money records, staff files. See Modules 2 and 3.

What it gives back

The AI makes something up and you use it. Fake cases, made-up facts, unfair choices. See Modules 5, 7 and 8.

AI aimed at you

Someone uses AI to copy or trick you. Deepfakes, scam emails, fake bosses. See Module 6.

And what to do

How to act when something goes wrong, and how to keep a record of the AI you use. See Modules 9, 10 and 12.

"But I only use AI to write emails"

Most people on this course use AI. They do not build it. They write emails, draft proposals, sum up meetings, fix wording, translate, code, plan, and think out loud.

That is exactly who these rules are for. The big rules (the EU AI Act, GDPR, ISO 42001 and ISO 27001) cover any company that uses AI to do its work. The duty lands on the people doing the work, not only the IT team.

§
Where the rules land

GDPR Article 5 says you must handle people's personal data in a way that is lawful, fair and open. Paste it into a tool whose rules you have not read, and you break all three. ISO 27001:2022 (A.5.34) says a company must protect personal data the whole time it uses it. The EU AI Act (Article 4) says staff who use AI need enough know-how to use it safely. This course is part of that know-how.

What this course will not do

This course helps you spot AI risk and dodge the common mistakes. It will not turn you into a compliance officer. It will not certify anything. It will not tell you which AI tools to buy. And it will not pretend the rules are simpler than they are.

Finish it, and workmates start asking what you think before they hit send. That is what the rules look like on a normal Tuesday.
