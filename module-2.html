What AI tools do with what you type.

When you paste something into a chatbot, your words travel to a company, onto a computer, in some country, under rules almost nobody reads. This module explains those four things, in plain words.

By the end of this module, you will
  • Tell the difference between the free, paid, team and work versions of an AI tool, and what each does with your data
  • Understand what "training the model" means, and how to switch it off where you can
  • Find the "how long is my data kept" setting in any big AI tool in under a minute
  • Know what "a human may read your chat" really means

The five-question test

Before you paste anything more private than a recipe into an AI tool, ask five things. The first one is the real decision point in most organisations:

  1. What type of data am I about to paste? Public? Internal? Confidential? Personal? Special category (health, race, politics, and the rest of Module 3's list)? The more sensitive the class, the higher the bar for where it can go. If you are not sure, treat it as the most sensitive class until you know.
  2. Where is my data kept? Which country, whose computers.
  3. How long is it kept? Until you delete it? Days? Longer? It varies between vendors, products and settings.
  4. Is it used to train the next AI? On many free tools it may be, unless you turn it off. On most work tools, no.
  5. Who at the company can read it? Engineers fixing bugs? Safety staff? Nobody? It changes.

Every big AI company puts the answers online. They are different for each version, and they change. Check them twice a year, not once and never again.

The four versions, weakest to safest

1. Free version

Like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini or Copilot when you have not paid.

Things that surprise most people the first time:

  • Depending on the provider and your settings, conversations may be used to improve future models, services or safety systems, often unless you turn that off in settings.
  • Retention periods vary between vendors, products and settings. Some providers may retain data for a period after deletion for operational, legal or security purposes. "Delete" can mean "hidden from you" rather than "gone from their computers".
  • A few chats get read by real people to check for abuse and problems. The rules say so, in gentle words.
  • There is no deal between your company and the AI company. You are a member of the public using a public tool for work. So your company has no say over what happens to the data.
!
The "free for work" mistake

Most data leaks at small companies start the same way: someone pastes work into a free AI tool. Client details, a draft contract, pay information, code. The data then sits on a computer your company has no deal with, no right to check, and maybe no legal reach over.

When employees use AI tools outside approved company processes like this, it is often called Shadow AI. Shadow AI reduces visibility, increases risk and makes governance significantly more difficult, because no one can manage data they do not know is leaving. Module 4 covers it in depth.

2. Paid version (just you)

Like ChatGPT Plus, Claude Pro or Gemini Advanced.

Mostly the same as free, but with better answers and longer chats. The big catch: it is still a public tool. Still no deal between your company and the AI company. Paying for it yourself does not change the rules. It just gives you a faster version of the wrong tool.

One real plus: most paid versions have a clear "do not train on my chats" switch. Find it. Turn it on. Do not assume it is already on.

3. Team or business version

Like ChatGPT Team, Claude for Work, Microsoft 365 Copilot, or Gemini in Google Workspace.

Here the rules change. Your company has a proper deal (a Data Processing Agreement) with the AI company. Training on your data is usually off by default. You can set how long data is kept. There is an admin panel where your IT or security team makes the rules.

If your company has bought one of these, use it. If not, ask your manager before you paste work data into anything.

4. Work (enterprise) version

Like ChatGPT Enterprise, Microsoft 365 Copilot for big companies, Claude Enterprise, Azure OpenAI, AWS Bedrock, or Google Vertex AI.

Everything the team version has, plus: you can pick where the data is kept (often EU-only for European firms), full records of who did what, single sign-on, and security certificates the buying team can check.

This is the version the rules in the standards module expect when AI touches anything private, customer-facing at scale, or commercially sensitive.

Why organisations buy the enterprise version. It is rarely about better answers. It is about the controls a business needs to use AI responsibly and prove it later:

  • Administrative control, a central admin panel to set the rules, switch features on or off, and manage who has access.
  • Data governance, control over retention, data location, training opt-out, and how information is handled across the organisation.
  • Identity integration, single sign-on tied to your existing accounts, so joiners and leavers are managed automatically.
  • Auditability, logs of who used what and when, which is the evidence an auditor, insurer or regulator will ask for.
  • Compliance requirements, contractual terms (a DPA), security certifications, and data-residency options that let the business meet GDPR, the EU AI Act and sector rules.
G
Governance Insight

ISO/IEC 42001 encourages organisations to understand the AI systems they use, assess supplier risks and implement appropriate controls over AI-related activities. The enterprise-version controls above are how a smaller organisation puts that into practice without building it all itself.

"Trained on", what it really means

When a tool says "we may use your chats to train future AI", the simplest version is the worst one. Your words and the AI's replies join a huge pile of data. That pile trains the next AI. And there is no reliable way to make an AI "forget" something once it is baked in.

The real risk is not that the next ChatGPT repeats your client list word for word. It is that your client list becomes one of millions of tiny pieces inside it, and a clever person with the right question can pull small bits back out. This is a real, known attack, and it gets better every year.

You cannot fix this by deleting the chat later. You fix it by turning off training before you paste.

✓
Find the training switch

Right now, in the AI tool you use most for work:

  1. Open settings.
  2. Look for "data controls", "privacy", or "chat history & training".
  3. Turn off anything that says "improve the model", "help us get better", or "share for training".

Do this once for each tool. Check again after big updates, because some tools reset it.

"A human may read your chat", what it really means

Most big AI companies have safety teams. They look at a small number of chats (sometimes staff, sometimes hired helpers, sometimes abroad) to catch abuse, harm and mistakes.

The chance your exact chat gets read is small. But the point still stands: "no one will ever see this" is not something the tool promises. If you would not want a stranger reading it, do not type it into a tool where strangers can read chats.

i
A handy way to think about it

Treat anything you paste into a free or paid AI tool like writing it on a postcard. The chance a stranger reads one postcard is small. The chance a postcard could be read is total. So you would never write your bank password on a postcard.

Where the data actually sits

European companies have the hardest version of this. GDPR controls sending people's personal data outside Europe, and the rules got stricter after a big court case in 2020 (Schrems II).

On the free versions of most US AI tools, your data goes to US computers. There are legal ways to make this OK, but they only protect your company if your company has signed up to them. On a free tool, it has not.

Most big tools now offer to keep data inside the EU on their work version. If your company handles European personal data and uses AI a lot, that is one of the best reasons to pay for the work version.

§
Where the rules land

GDPR Articles 28 and 44–50: when you hand personal data to another company to handle for you, there must be a contract that says how. Sending it outside Europe needs extra protection. ISO/IEC 27001:2022 (A.5.23): think about security when you use cloud tools. ISO/IEC 42001:2023 (Clause 8.3, Annex A.10): check and manage the AI suppliers you use. EU AI Act Article 13: makers of high-risk AI must give you enough information to really understand the tool.

The takeaway

"Is it OK to use AI for this?" almost always has the same answer: it depends on which version of which tool, with which settings, on which data. Modules 3 and 4 turn that into a simple rule. For now, remember one line: the version you use matters as much as what you type.
