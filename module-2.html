What AI tools do with what you type.

When you paste text into a chatbot, you're not sending it to a robot in a vacuum. You're sending it to a company, on a server, in a country, governed by terms most people never read. Here's what's actually happening.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Know the difference between free, paid-individual, team, and enterprise tiers — and what changes about your data at each level
  • Understand what "model training" means and how to opt out where you can
  • Be able to find the data-retention setting in any major AI tool in under 60 seconds
  • Know what "your conversation may be reviewed by humans" actually means

The four-question test

For any AI tool, before you paste anything more sensitive than a recipe, you want to know:

  1. Where is my data stored? (Which country, which region, on whose servers.)
  2. How long is it kept? (Until you delete it. For 30 days. Forever. It varies.)
  3. Is it used to train future models? (Yes by default on many free tiers. No by default on most enterprise tiers.)
  4. Who at the vendor can read it? (Engineers debugging. Trust-and-safety reviewers. No one. It varies.)

Every major vendor publishes the answers. The answers differ by tier. The answers differ between the same vendor's consumer and business products. The answers occasionally change. Treat this as a thing you check twice a year, not once forever.

The tier ladder

From least protected to most:

1. Free consumer tier

Examples: chatgpt.com without a subscription, claude.ai without a paid plan, the free Gemini app, Microsoft Copilot's free web version, Perplexity free.

Defaults that often surprise people:

  • Your conversations may be used to train future models, unless you've explicitly opted out in settings.
  • Conversations are typically retained for at least 30 days, often longer, even if you "delete" them — the deletion frequently means "removed from your view", not "removed from the vendor's servers".
  • A subset of conversations is reviewed by human reviewers for safety, abuse, and quality purposes. The vendor's policy will say so, usually in language designed not to alarm you.
  • There is no contract between your employer and the vendor. You are a consumer using a consumer service for work, which means your employer has no legal grip on what happens to the data.
!
The "free for work" mistake

Most data leaks at small and medium businesses don't happen because someone hacked the AI vendor. They happen because someone used a free consumer tier for work — pasted client data, contract drafts, salary information, source code — and that data is now sitting on a server the business has no contract with, no audit rights over, and possibly no legal jurisdiction over.

2. Paid individual tier

Examples: ChatGPT Plus, Claude Pro, Gemini Advanced, Perplexity Pro.

Mostly the same as free, with better models and longer conversations. Crucially: still a consumer product. Still no contract between your employer and the vendor. Paying for it personally and using it for work doesn't change the legal status — it just gets you a faster version of the wrong tool.

One genuine improvement: most paid tiers give you an obvious "do not train on my conversations" toggle. Find it. Turn it on. Don't assume it's on by default.

3. Team / Business tier

Examples: ChatGPT Team, ChatGPT Business, Claude for Work, Microsoft 365 Copilot (with Microsoft 365 Business), Google Workspace's Gemini, Notion AI Business.

This is where the legal posture shifts. Your employer has a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with the vendor. Training-on-customer-data is usually off by default. Retention is configurable. There's an admin console where your IT or security team can set rules.

If your business has procured one of these for the team, use it. If your business hasn't, this is the conversation worth having before pasting work data into anything.

4. Enterprise tier

Examples: ChatGPT Enterprise, Microsoft 365 Copilot in enterprise tenants, Claude Enterprise, Azure OpenAI Service, AWS Bedrock, Google Cloud Vertex AI.

Everything the team tier offers, plus: choice of where the data is stored (often EU-only for European customers), full audit logging, SSO and role-based controls, encryption at rest with customer-managed keys in some cases, SOC 2 / ISO 27001 attestations available to the procurement team.

This is what the standards (Module 12) effectively assume you're using when AI touches anything regulated, customer-facing at scale, or commercially sensitive.

"Trained on" — what it actually means

When a vendor says "we may use your conversations to train future models", what they actually do varies, but the most worrying version is the simplest: your prompts and the model's responses become part of a giant dataset, that dataset is used to teach the next model, and there is no reliable technical way to make a model "forget" a specific piece of information once it's been baked in.

In practice, the risk isn't usually "the next ChatGPT will quote my client list verbatim". It's that the next ChatGPT will have absorbed your client list as one of millions of data points, and someone with the right kind of carefully-crafted prompt may be able to coax small fragments back out. The technical term is "training-data extraction attacks". They're real, they're published, they get more effective every year.

You can't fix this by deleting your conversation after it's been used for training. You fix it by not allowing it to be used for training in the first place.

✓
The training-opt-out hunt

Right now, on the AI tool you use most for work:

  1. Open settings.
  2. Find "data controls" or "privacy" or "chat history & training".
  3. Turn off any toggle that says "improve the model", "share with provider for training", "help us get better", or similar.

Do this once per tool. Re-check after major updates — vendors occasionally reset preferences.

"Reviewed by humans" — what it actually means

Most major AI vendors run trust-and-safety review programmes. A small fraction of conversations is sampled and reviewed by human reviewers — sometimes employees, sometimes contractors, sometimes overseas — to detect abuse, harmful content, model failures, and policy violations.

The probability your specific conversation gets reviewed is low. The point isn't that it's likely — it's that "no one will see this" is not a property the system gives you. If you'd be uncomfortable with a stranger reading a transcript of what you typed, don't type it into a tool where strangers can read transcripts of what you typed.

i
A useful mental model

Treat anything you paste into a free or paid-individual AI tool the way you'd treat anything you'd written on a postcard. The probability that a specific postcard gets read by an unrelated stranger is low. The probability that a postcard could be read is one hundred percent. So you don't put your bank password on a postcard.

Where the data physically is

For European businesses, this matters more than for most other reasons. GDPR has rules about transferring personal data outside the European Economic Area, and the rules have got tougher since the Schrems II court ruling in 2020.

In the consumer tiers of most major US AI vendors, your data goes to US servers. There are mechanisms (the EU-US Data Privacy Framework, Standard Contractual Clauses) that make this legal in many situations, but those mechanisms only protect your business if you've actually entered into them — which on a free tier, you haven't.

The enterprise tiers of most major vendors now offer EU-resident data processing. If your business handles European personal data and uses AI in any volume, this is one of the strongest arguments for paying for the enterprise tier.

§
Where the standards land

GDPR Articles 28 and 44–50: when you give personal data to a third party that processes it on your behalf (a "processor"), there must be a contract specifying how. International transfers outside the EU/EEA need extra safeguards. ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A.5.23: information security must be considered in the use of cloud services. ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Clause 8.3 and Annex A.10: AI system suppliers and third-party AI components must be evaluated and managed. EU AI Act Article 13: providers of high-risk AI must give you, the deployer, enough information to actually understand the system you're using.

The takeaway

"Is it OK to use AI for this?" almost always has the same answer: it depends on which tier of which tool, with which settings, on which data. Modules 3 and 4 turn that into a practical rule. For now, just internalise: the tier matters as much as the prompt.
