When something goes wrong.

No matter how careful, sometimes it happens. What matters is the first hour after you notice. This module walks that hour, in order, by category.

By the end of this module, you'll
  • Know the four kinds of "something went wrong" in AI use and what each one needs
  • Be able to write a clear escalation note to your manager or DPO without sounding panicked or evasive
  • Understand the potential 72-hour breach notification requirement and what it means in practice
  • Know what to preserve and what not to touch in the first hour

The four categories

Different incidents need different responses. Categorising it correctly in the first ten minutes will save days of confusion later.

1. Data leak

You (or someone on your team) put sensitive data into an AI tool that shouldn't have received it. Customer records pasted into an unapproved AI tool. Source code in a public model. A confidential document summarised by a free tier.

2. Bad output acted upon

The AI's output was used in a decision, and the output was wrong. Hallucinated legal citation filed with a court. Biased screening rejected a candidate. Made-up product features published on the website.

3. Successful attack

An AI-powered scam landed. Money was transferred. Credentials were given up. Software was installed. A deepfake call was acted on.

4. Vendor incident

Your AI vendor suffered a breach, or changed their terms without notice, or had an outage that affected an important workflow. The data you sent them is at unexpected risk through no fault of yours.

The first ten minutes (any category)

  1. Stop the bleeding, if you can do it safely. Pause whatever process is currently sending more data, taking more actions, or sending more money. Don't yet "fix" anything, just stop adding to the problem.
  2. Don't delete the evidence. The reflex to delete the chat, the email, the file is understandable. Don't. Investigators will need it.
  3. Tell someone. Your manager and/or whoever covers IT or security. The first sentence is enough: "I think we have an AI-related incident — can we talk in five minutes?" You're not asking for permission. You're not confessing. You're flagging.
  4. Write down what happened, while it's fresh. Five lines, plain language, no spin. You'll thank yourself two days from now.

Category 1 — data leak

If the data is personal data of EU residents

This may be a personal data breach under GDPR. Article 33 requires the controller (usually the business, not you personally) to notify the supervisory authority — ICO in the UK, CNIL in France, etc. — within 72 hours of becoming aware, where the breach is likely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons.

"Becoming aware" is from the moment the business — not the regulator — has a reasonable degree of certainty that a breach has occurred. The clock starts then. The 72-hour notification window runs continuously, including weekends and holidays.

You, the individual, are not the one notifying the regulator. Your DPO or your business's nominated person does that. But your speed of telling them is what makes that 72-hour clock survivable.

!
"It might not be a breach" is not an excuse to wait

The breach-notification clock starts when the business has reasonable awareness, not when the business is certain. If you think there's a real possibility — escalate today. Your DPO has 72 hours to investigate and decide whether to notify; they can't use any of those hours if they don't know yet.

What to preserve

  • The original conversation with the AI tool, including timestamps. (Take a screenshot. Don't delete the chat history.)
  • What you pasted (the source document or query — keep it).
  • Any output the AI produced.
  • Your own recollection of when and why.

What to do at the AI vendor

Once you've informed your manager / DPO, they may instruct you to:

  • Submit a data-deletion request to the vendor (most have a dedicated form).
  • Turn off any "use my data for training" setting that may have been on, if you haven't already.
  • Export and then delete the conversation from your account.

Don't do these unilaterally before your DPO/manager has agreed — for breach-investigation purposes they may want to preserve the originals first, even though preservation in a third-party system is itself imperfect.

Category 2 — bad output acted upon

The fix here depends on how far the bad output has travelled.

  • If it's still in your draft: easy. Fix it, learn, log it, move on.
  • If it's been sent to one person: write a clear correction. Don't blame the AI; own it. ("I sent you an inaccurate citation in my message yesterday — the case I referenced doesn't exist. The correct authority is...")
  • If it's been published or filed somewhere formal: this is a bigger correction. Tell your manager. Most professional settings have a "correction-of-record" procedure — court filings, regulatory submissions, formal client deliverables. Use it.
  • If a decision was made on the basis of bad output: depending on what kind of decision, the affected person may have a right to ask for it to be reconsidered (Module 9). Be prepared to reconsider it without the AI's input.
  • Assess whether any downstream decisions, reports or communications were influenced by the inaccurate output. One wrong output often feeds others — a flawed figure copied into a report, a bad summary forwarded onward. Trace where it went.

Category 3 — successful attack

Module 6 covered the prevention side. The response side:

  1. If money moved: call the bank within minutes, not hours. UK Faster Payments and SEPA Instant transfers can sometimes be recalled in the first hour. After that, recovery rates drop sharply.
  2. If credentials were compromised: change them. Then enable MFA if it wasn't on. Then rotate any other credentials that reused the same password.
  3. If software was installed: assume the device is compromised. Disconnect it from the network. Call IT.
  4. Tell your manager and IT/security immediately. Same minute, not same day. Same attack is probably still active in the inbox of someone else in the business.
  5. For UK businesses: report to Action Fraud (0300 123 2040) and consider reporting to the NCSC. For EU businesses: report to your national police cybercrime unit and your supervisory data-protection authority if personal data was involved.
  6. Follow your existing cyber incident response process where one exists. An AI-related attack is still a cyber incident — use the runbook, contacts and escalation path you already have rather than inventing a separate AI-only process.

Category 4 — vendor incident

The vendor will (or should) publish a notification. Your job is:

  • Read what they actually say about the incident. Don't rely on the press summary — read the vendor's status page or incident report.
  • Identify what your business had with them — what kinds of data, in which workspace, at which time.
  • If personal data was involved, this is now a Category 1 problem in disguise: your business may itself have a notification obligation to its own customers.
  • Treat the vendor's terms-change as a procurement event: if they've materially changed how they handle data, decide whether the relationship can continue.
  • As part of the response, review the Data Processing Agreement (DPA) and other contractual obligations, and reassess supplier risk in light of what happened.

What to write to your manager or DPO

Use this template. Short. Factual. No hedging. No drama.

✉
A clean incident note

Subject: Possible AI-related incident — needs review today

Hi [name],

I want to flag a possible incident from this morning. Brief facts:

  • What happened: [one sentence — "I pasted a customer spreadsheet into ChatGPT to deduplicate it"]
  • When: [date and approximate time]
  • What data was involved: [type and rough volume — "approx 400 rows including names, emails, order dates"]
  • Which AI tool / tier: [be specific — "ChatGPT, free tier, my personal account"]
  • What I've done so far: [stopped, screenshotted the chat, didn't delete, haven't told anyone else yet]
  • Current impact / known consequences: [what's affected right now — "data may have left our control; no customer contact yet; unsure if training was on"]

I think this may need to be treated as a personal data breach. I'm available any time today to talk through what's next.

Thanks,
[Your name]

After the dust settles

Three things, none of them optional:

  1. A short written record — what happened, what was done, what was decided. Goes into your AI-use log (Module 9) and, if material, into the business's incident register.
  2. One thing that changes. Process, policy, tool tier, training, settings. The lesson has to land somewhere outside your own head.
  3. No blame, no scapegoat. The point of incident response is to learn faster than the next incident happens. Cultures that punish the person who reports are cultures that don't get reports.
i
Governance insight: response, not blame

The goal of incident response is containment, evidence preservation, assessment and learning — not assigning blame. Organisations that treat incidents as failures to punish get fewer reports and slower responses; organisations that treat them as information get faster containment and real improvement.

§
Where the standards land

GDPR Article 33: 72-hour breach notification to the supervisory authority. Article 34: notification to data subjects when the breach is likely to result in high risk to their rights. ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A.5.24: information security incident planning and preparation. Annex A.5.25: assessment of and decision on information security events. Annex A.5.26: response to information security incidents. EU AI Act Article 73: providers must report serious incidents to authorities; Article 26(5): deployers should monitor and, where relevant, inform their providers. ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Annex A.6.2.6: AI system operation and monitoring (incident handling sits in the lifecycle); A.9: use of AI systems including incident response by people with authority.
